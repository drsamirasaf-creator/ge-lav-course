39.1 Both Tracks: Project Draft Workshop (60 minutes)

39.1.1 Workshop Learning Objectives

By the end of this workshop, students will be able to:

  1. Provide structured peer review on a classmate’s GE-LAV project draft.
  2. Identify common weaknesses in their own draft based on peer feedback.
  3. Plan revisions for the final paper and presentation.
  4. Practice delivering the project’s “elevator pitch” — the IC summary in 2 minutes.

39.1.2 Workshop Structure (Both Tracks, Same Activity)

Time Activity Detail
0:00–0:05 Logistics: pair assignments, rubric distribution Lecture
0:05–0:25 Reading time: read partner’s draft + take notes Individual
0:25–0:50 Peer feedback session (12.5 min each direction) Pair work
0:50–1:00 Wrap-up: list 3 priority revisions for your draft Individual

39.1.3 Peer Review Rubric

Each student provides written feedback to their partner addressing:

1. Clarity (5 questions, 1-5 scale) - Is the asset/question stated precisely? - Is the methodology clear? - Are the calibrations documented? - Are the results easy to follow? - Is the recommendation defensible?

2. Rigor (5 questions) - Is the GE-LAV implementation correct? - Are the comparisons (DCF vs. LAV vs. GE-LAV) accurate? - Are sensitivity analyses adequate? - Is the data appropriate? - For Track 2: are mathematical derivations correct?

3. Communication (3 questions) - Could you explain this paper to a non-finance friend? Try in 2 sentences. - Is the IC-relevance clear? - Is the writing professional quality?

4. Improvement priorities (open) - “The single most important revision before the final paper would be…” - “The presentation should emphasize…” - “One thing that would delete if I had to cut to 12 pages…”

39.1.4 Workshop Slide Briefs (New Slides Needed)

Slide 30.1 — Workshop Introduction

  • Title: “Project Draft Workshop”
  • Goals: Identify weaknesses in your own work via someone else’s perspective
  • Norms:
    • Be specific, not vague (“the GE-LAV calculation is unclear at step 3” not “I’m confused”)
    • Be constructive, not destructive
    • Focus on the work, not the person
    • Both you and your partner are still learning
  • Speaker notes: Set the tone for peer review. Many students are uncomfortable giving direct feedback. Frame as professional courtesy.

Slide 30.2 — Peer Review Process

  • Title: “How the next hour works”
  • Time blocks:
    • 20 min: Read your partner’s draft carefully (don’t skim)
    • 25 min: Take turns giving and receiving feedback (12.5 min each)
    • 10 min: Capture YOUR top 3 revision priorities
  • Speaker notes: Discipline the structure. Without time limits, peer review degrades into chat.

Slide 30.3 — Common Weaknesses to Watch For

  • Title: “Common weaknesses across project drafts”
  • For Track 1 (Practitioner) drafts:
    • Asset specification too vague
    • Calibration parameters unjustified
    • GE-LAV computation lacks regime variation
    • Recommendation doesn’t connect to the LAV/GE-LAV result
  • For Track 2 (Researcher) drafts:
    • Mathematical claims unproven or under-proven
    • Numerical implementation lacks convergence diagnostics
    • Connection to existing literature unclear
    • Extension proposal too ambitious for paper scope
  • Speaker notes: Make these visible. Both tracks share the same pitfalls in slightly different forms.

Slide 30.4 — What Goes in the Final Paper

  • Title: “Revising for the final submission”
  • Final paper requirements (Session 32):
    • 20 pages excluding references and appendices
    • All revisions from peer feedback addressed
    • Specific improvements over draft (state them explicitly in a “Revisions” footnote)
    • Polished writing quality
    • Reproducible computations
  • What gets graded:
    • 2% improvement over draft
    • 2% writing quality
    • 1% final correctness
  • Speaker notes: Set clear expectations. The final paper is not the draft with minor edits.

39.2 Track 2: Valuation Hierarchy Proof (15 additional minutes)

39.2.1 Track 2 Learning Objectives

By the end of the Track 2 portion, students will additionally be able to:

  1. State and prove Theorem 20.1 (strict valuation hierarchy DCF ⊊ LAV ⊊ GE-LAV).
  2. Identify Theorem 20.2 (conditions for approximate equality among the hierarchies).
  3. Compute the gap between hierarchy levels for arbitrary OU calibrations.
  4. Discuss why the strict nesting matters for regulatory and pedagogical purposes.

39.2.2 Track 2 Slide Briefs

Slide T2-30.1 — Theorem 20.1: Strict Valuation Hierarchy

  • Title: “Theorem 20.1: DCF ⊊ LAV ⊊ GE-LAV”
  • Statement: Under positive liquidity volatility (\(\sigma_L > 0\)) and positive general-equilibrium coupling (\(\gamma > 0\)):
    • \(V^{DCF}(L, t) \neq V^{LAV}(L, t)\) (strict inequality somewhere on the state space)
    • \(V^{LAV}(L, t) \neq V^{GE-LAV}(L, t)\) (strict inequality)
  • In words: The three operators give genuinely different answers when liquidity is stochastic and the externality is present.

Slide T2-30.2 — Theorem 20.1 Proof Sketch

  • Title: “Proof: strict nesting”
  • Part 1 (DCF ≠ LAV):
    • Jensen’s inequality applied to \(e^{-r(L)T}\): \(E[e^{-r(L)T}] > e^{-E[r(L)]T}\)
    • Strict whenever \(L\) is non-degenerate (i.e., \(\sigma_L > 0\))
  • Part 2 (LAV ≠ GE-LAV):
    • GE-LAV adds the equilibrium uplift to \(r(L)\): \(r_{GE}(L, \mu) = r(L) + \text{externality}(L, \mu)\)
    • Strict whenever the externality is positive (i.e., \(\gamma > 0\))
  • Combination: Both inequalities strict → strict nesting holds.

Slide T2-30.3 — Theorem 20.2: Conditions for Equality

  • Title: “When the hierarchies converge”
  • Statement: \(V^{DCF} = V^{LAV} = V^{GE-LAV}\) in the limit:
    • As \(\sigma_L \to 0\): liquidity is deterministic, no path dependence
    • As \(\gamma \to 0\): no externality, partial equilibrium
  • Result: The framework reduces to DCF in the “no-uncertainty, no-coupling” limit.
  • Speaker notes: This is the consistency check. GE-LAV doesn’t conflict with DCF in DCF’s domain of validity.

39.2.3 Track 2 Discussion Questions

  1. The strict nesting theorem requires \(\sigma_L > 0\) and \(\gamma > 0\). What if \(\sigma_L > 0\) but \(\gamma = 0\)? Is LAV strictly necessary then, or does DCF + simple Jensen suffice?
  2. The proof of strict inequality relies on Jensen’s inequality applied pointwise. What if some pricing kernel is approximately linear (e.g., very short horizons)? Does the inequality “vanish” in the limit?
  3. Theorem 20.2 (equality conditions) is the “consistency limit.” Is there a third limit where GE-LAV reduces to something other than DCF? Perhaps when the externality dominates?
