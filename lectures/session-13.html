22.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. Define a liquidity trap in the GE-LAV framework and distinguish it from a typical illiquidity event.
  2. Walk through the GE-LAV exit rule performance during three major historical episodes (GFC, COVID, 2022 rate shock).
  3. Identify the conditions under which the GE-LAV exit rule fails or under-performs.
  4. Connect the LP-level exit decision to the GP-level decision to extend or roll funds.
  5. Preview the Pigouvian exit tax as a corrective instrument (full treatment Session 24).

22.4 Slide Content Briefs (New Slides Needed)

22.4.1 Slide 13.1 — Title

  • Title: “Liquidity Traps and Historical Performance”
  • Tag line: “Does GE-LAV survive contact with the data?”
  • Speaker notes: Today is the empirical credibility test. If the framework can’t backtest well, it’s just theory.

22.4.2 Slide 13.2 — The Liquidity Trap, Formally

  • Title: “The liquidity trap”
  • Definition (boxed): “A liquidity trap is a state where (1) an LP wants to exit, (2) the secondary market is functional, but (3) the discount required to clear is so large that exiting destroys substantial value, leaving the LP economically worse off than holding through.”
  • Three conditions for a trap:
    • \(L_t \ll L^*\) (deep stress)
    • Secondary market is functioning but at extreme discounts
    • LP’s exit options are constrained (must sell soon, can’t wait for mean reversion)
  • Key insight: “A trap isn’t just stress — it’s stress + constraint. The framework handles the stress; the constraint is the friction.”
  • Speaker notes: Distinguish from “no buyers” scenarios (market closure). Traps are a price problem, not a quantity problem.

22.4.3 Slide 13.3 — GFC 2008–2010 Case Study

  • Title: “Case 1: The Global Financial Crisis”
  • Timeline:
    • Q3 2008: Lehman collapses; PE secondary market freezes briefly
    • Q4 2008–Q2 2009: Market re-opens at 35–50% discounts
    • Implied \(L_t\): −2.0 to −2.5 (worst on record)
  • What LPs did (empirical):
    • Most held through
    • Some forced sellers (denominator effect, funding obligations)
    • CalPERS, Yale, Harvard publicly discussed selling
  • What GE-LAV would have said:
    • Exit boundary triggered for positions with ≤5 years remaining
    • Hold recommendation for >7 year positions despite the stress
  • Outcome (counterfactual):
    • LPs who held: Recovered fully by 2013 (mean reversion)
    • LPs who sold: Crystallized 30-50% losses
    • GE-LAV-optimal decision (selective exit based on remaining horizon): Improved outcomes ~5-10%
  • Speaker notes: This is the framework’s strongest case. The mean-reversion bet paid off; GE-LAV got the timing roughly right.

22.4.4 Slide 13.4 — COVID 2020 Case Study

  • Title: “Case 2: COVID-19 Shock”
  • Timeline:
    • March 2020: Secondary discounts spike from 5% to 20% in 3 weeks
    • April 2020: Fed intervention; markets stabilize
    • Q3 2020: Discounts back to 8-10%
    • Implied \(L_t\) path: 0 → -1.0 → -0.3 → 0
  • What LPs did:
    • Some panicked sellers in late March
    • Most held; recovery was rapid
  • What GE-LAV would have said:
    • Brief exit signal triggered for very short-horizon positions only
    • Strong hold recommendation for typical positions
  • Outcome:
    • GE-LAV substantially correct on hold-through recommendation
    • The fast mean reversion (within months, not years) was atypical and favorable
  • Speaker notes: COVID was a brief stress, atypical of historical patterns. GE-LAV got it right because the OU mean-reversion speed (\(\kappa = 0.45\)) was appropriate for the recovery.

22.4.5 Slide 13.5 — 2022 Rate Shock Case Study

  • Title: “Case 3: The 2022 Rate Shock”
  • Timeline:
    • 2022 Q1: Fed begins aggressive tightening
    • Throughout 2022–2023: Public markets fall; PE NAVs don’t adjust → denominator effect
    • Secondary discounts: Widen to 15-20% and stay there for 18+ months
    • Implied \(L_t\): −0.6 to −0.8, persistent rather than spiking
  • What LPs did:
    • Increased secondary sales to manage allocation limits
    • Several sovereign wealth funds publicly stepped back from new commitments
  • What GE-LAV would have said:
    • Selective exit for short-horizon positions (≤3 years)
    • Hold for long-horizon (especially infrastructure)
    • Reduce new commitments (Session 14–15 portfolio implication)
  • Outcome:
    • Mixed performance — recovery slower than COVID, more like a slow grind
    • GE-LAV handled it reasonably, but the persistence of the stress tested the mean-reversion assumption
  • Speaker notes: This is the framework’s weakest case. Persistent moderate stress is harder for OU to model than sharp spikes followed by mean reversion.

22.4.6 Slide 13.6 — When the GE-LAV Rule Fails

  • Title: “Where the framework underperforms”
  • Three failure modes:
    1. Regime shift: If \(\bar{L}\) permanently shifts (e.g., post-2022 “higher for longer” rates), OU calibrated to historical \(\bar{L} = 1.0\) produces wrong hold recommendations
    2. Sustained stress without mean reversion: If \(\kappa\) is overestimated (mean reversion is slower than assumed), holds become value-destroying
    3. Crisis without secondary market function: If the secondary market closes (Q4 2008 briefly), the exit option is unavailable regardless of what the boundary says
  • Mitigation:
    • Regular re-calibration (book recommends quarterly)
    • Stress-test scenarios with alternative \(\kappa\), \(\bar{L}\)
    • Liquidity buffer for crisis-unavailable scenarios
  • Speaker notes: Honesty about failure modes. The framework is good, not perfect.

22.4.7 Slide 13.7 — LP-GP Friction: Extensions and Continuation Funds

  • Title: “What the GP wants vs. what the LP wants”
  • The conflict:
    • GPs often want fund extensions or continuation funds during stress (preserves AUM, defers underperformance crystallization)
    • LPs may want to exit per GE-LAV recommendation (avoid crystallizing in the trough)
  • Empirical pattern (2020–2024):
    • GP-led secondaries (continuation funds) exploded post-2020
    • Many LPs felt pressured to “go along” despite suboptimal economics
  • GE-LAV recommendation:
    • Evaluate continuation fund terms against the GE-LAV exit boundary
    • If the GP is offering you a continuation in stressed conditions, your hold is probably correct — but on different terms than they propose
  • Speaker notes: This connects theory to LP-GP politics. Useful for students entering institutional roles.

22.4.8 Slide 13.8 — NAV Loans: The Hidden Liquidity Mechanism

  • Title: “NAV loans and what they reveal”
  • Setup:
    • NAV loan: GP borrows against fund NAV, using future distributions as collateral
    • Emerged ~2020 as alternative to secondary sales for liquidity needs
  • What NAV loans reveal:
    • GPs implicitly believe NAVs are understated (otherwise no lender would advance against them)
    • Or alternatively: GPs need liquidity even when secondary discounts are wide (forced-seller equivalence)
  • GE-LAV interpretation:
    • NAV loans = holding against funding — an LP-equivalent strategy
    • Should be evaluated against the LAV (not DCF) value of the underlying
  • Speaker notes: NAV loans are a rapidly growing market. Students entering the industry should know what they are.

22.4.9 Slide 13.9 — The Pigouvian Exit Tax (Preview)

  • Title: “Tease: the Pigouvian exit tax”
  • The externality:
    • When many LPs exit simultaneously, secondary prices drop for everyone (the McKean-Vlasov effect)
    • Each individual LP doesn’t internalize this collective cost
    • This is a classic externality → calls for a Pigouvian correction
  • The GE-LAV solution:
    • Tax \(\tau(L_t)\) on secondary sales, varying by liquidity state
    • At normal liquidity (\(L \approx 0\)): tax ≈ 0
    • At GFC depth (\(L \approx -2\)): tax ≈ 6–8% of transaction value
  • Effect:
    • Discourages forced sales during stress
    • Internalizes the externality
    • Welfare gain ~2.3%/yr per book Ch. 19
  • Full treatment: Session 24 (intuition) + Session 29 Track 2 (full derivation)
  • Speaker notes: Don’t go deep — just plant the seed. The Pigouvian tax is the most policy-relevant GE-LAV result.

22.5 Discussion Questions

  1. CalPERS sold ~$5B of PE secondaries in 2009 at substantial discounts. Reviewing the GE-LAV exit boundary and the conditions at the time, was this decision (a) optimal given their constraints, (b) suboptimal because they should have held, or (c) unanswerable without more context? Defend your answer.
  2. NAV loans have grown from <$1B in 2018 to >$40B in 2024. Is this a healthy development (LPs accessing liquidity without forced sales) or unhealthy (papering over real valuation issues)? Apply the GE-LAV lens.
  3. Some scholars argue the post-2022 “higher for longer” rate environment represents a permanent regime shift in \(\bar{L}\), not a stress event. If true, what’s the implication for OU calibration and exit boundary recommendations?

22.6 Worked Numerical Example: The 2008 Yale Decision

Setup (stylized, based on public disclosures): Yale endowment in Q4 2008. PE portfolio NAV: $3B. Public market drawdown has pushed PE allocation from target 25% to actual 35% of portfolio. Cash needs from operating budget: $200M.

Decision context: - Sell PE secondaries to raise cash and reduce allocation? - Hold and find cash elsewhere (e.g., new commitments paused)?

Step 1: GE-LAV exit boundary check

Yale’s PE portfolio is mixed-vintage, average 5 years remaining. \(L_t\) at Q4 2008: approximately −1.8.

Exit boundary at 5-year horizon: \(L^* \approx -1.2\) (PE buyout)

\(L_t = -1.8 < L^* = -1.2\) → Exit signal triggered

Step 2: What size of exit?

Forced exit pressure: need $200M cash + want to reduce $300M of allocation = $500M total

At 35% discount: would need to sell ~$770M of NAV to get $500M cash

Step 3: Counterfactual analysis

If Yale had held: - 2009: PE NAVs rose ~15% (mean reversion in \(L\)) - 2010: PE NAVs rose another 20% - The $770M of NAV held would have been worth ~$1.06B by 2010 - Net “missed value” from selling: ~$290M

If Yale sold (what they actually did, approximately): - $500M cash secured immediately - $270M of value sacrificed permanently - Allocation problem solved

Step 4: GE-LAV verdict

The exit signal triggered, but in retrospect, the magnitude of GE-LAV-optimal exit was smaller than what Yale executed. A more targeted exit (perhaps $300M instead of $770M of NAV) would have: - Raised needed cash + partial allocation reduction - Preserved more upside for the mean reversion - Net improvement vs. the all-in exit: ~$150M

Teaching note: This is informed counterfactual analysis, not perfect hindsight. The point is GE-LAV provides a quantitative framework for choosing the size of an exit, not just the binary decision.

22.7 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “If the framework worked in GFC, why didn’t institutions use it?” It didn’t exist in publishable form until ~2024. The math tools (McKean-Vlasov, modern Itô calculus applied to liquidity) only matured in the 2010s.
  • “COVID and GFC look like different events. Does GE-LAV unify them?” Yes — both are excursions of \(L_t\) below \(\bar{L}\). The differences (speed, depth, recovery time) are captured by the OU parameters. Same framework, different parameter realizations.
  • “What if \(\kappa\) varies by regime?” Likely true empirically. The current GE-LAV uses constant \(\kappa\) as a simplification. Regime-dependent \(\kappa\) is a research extension (Ch 21).
  • “NAV loans seem to undermine the framework.” Not really — they’re an LP-equivalent strategy that GE-LAV can evaluate. They reveal that NAV is understated, which is exactly what GE-LAV predicts.

**Step 2: What size of exit?**

Forced exit pressure: need $200M cash + want to reduce $300M of allocation = $500M total

At 35% discount: would need to sell ~$770M of NAV to get $500M cash

**Step 3: Counterfactual analysis**

If Yale had held:
- 2009: PE NAVs rose ~15% (mean reversion in $L$)
- 2010: PE NAVs rose another 20%
- The $770M of NAV held would have been worth ~$1.06B by 2010
- Net "missed value" from selling: ~$290M

If Yale sold (what they actually did, approximately):
- $500M cash secured immediately
- $270M of value sacrificed permanently
- Allocation problem solved

**Step 4: GE-LAV verdict**

The exit signal triggered, but in retrospect, the *magnitude* of GE-LAV-optimal exit was smaller than what Yale executed. A more targeted exit (perhaps $300M instead of $770M of NAV) would have:
- Raised needed cash + partial allocation reduction
- Preserved more upside for the mean reversion
- Net improvement vs. the all-in exit: ~$150M

**Teaching note:** This is *informed counterfactual analysis*, not perfect hindsight. The point is GE-LAV provides a quantitative framework for choosing the *size* of an exit, not just the binary decision.

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"If the framework worked in GFC, why didn't institutions use it?"** It didn't exist in publishable form until ~2024. The math tools (McKean-Vlasov, modern Itô calculus applied to liquidity) only matured in the 2010s.
- **"COVID and GFC look like different events. Does GE-LAV unify them?"** Yes — both are excursions of $L_t$ below $\bar{L}$. The differences (speed, depth, recovery time) are captured by the OU parameters. Same framework, different parameter realizations.
- **"What if $\kappa$ varies by regime?"** Likely true empirically. The current GE-LAV uses constant $\kappa$ as a simplification. Regime-dependent $\kappa$ is a research extension (Ch 21).
- **"NAV loans seem to undermine the framework."** Not really — they're an LP-equivalent strategy that GE-LAV can evaluate. They reveal that *NAV* is understated, which is exactly what GE-LAV predicts.
