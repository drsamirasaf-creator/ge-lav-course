36.1 Track 1: Infrastructure Case — “The 57% Illusion”

36.1.1 Track 1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, Track 1 students will be able to:

  1. Apply GE-LAV to a 20+ year infrastructure asset (longest duration in private markets).
  2. Identify why long-duration assets carry the largest accumulated Jensen bias.
  3. Compute the “57% illusion” — the gap between DCF and GE-LAV for stressed infrastructure positions.
  4. Translate GE-LAV findings into pension fund and insurance regulatory language.
  5. Connect infrastructure valuation errors to systemic financial stability concerns.

36.1.2 Track 1 Pre-Class Assignment

  • Read: Book Chapter 22 case (or Class 22 slides if available)
  • Optional: Global Infrastructure Hub report on private infrastructure valuation

36.1.4 Track 1 Slide Briefs (New Slides Needed)

Slide T1-27.1 — Infrastructure: The Longest Horizon Asset

  • Title: “Why infrastructure breaks DCF the hardest”
  • Three features:
    • Horizon: 20-50 years (PPPs sometimes 99 years)
    • Cash flow stability: Contracted revenues (tolls, regulated rates) → low operational volatility
    • Liquidity sensitivity: Long-duration asset; tiny premium changes have huge value effects
  • Result: Infrastructure has the largest Jensen bias and the largest equilibrium correction in stressed regimes
  • Speaker notes: Frame the asset class. Most students won’t have valued infrastructure deeply.

Slide T1-27.2 — The 57% Illusion (The Master Number)

  • Title: “$1B infrastructure: DCF says $1B, GE-LAV at GFC says $430M”
  • Decomposition:
    • DCF mark: $1.000B (constant 7% rate, 30-year asset)
    • LAV mark (normal): $1.108B (Jensen bias 10.8% for 30 yr)
    • GE-LAV mark (normal): $1.000B (small equilibrium correction)
    • GE-LAV mark (GFC depth): $0.430B (57% gap to DCF)
  • Why so extreme:
    • 30-year duration amplifies discount rate changes
    • At GFC depth, equilibrium rate ~32% vs. DCF 7%
    • \((1.07/1.32)^{30} \approx 0.43\)
  • Speaker notes: This is the single most powerful number for the regulatory case. Cite this routinely.

Slide T1-27.3 — Implications for Pension Funds

  • Title: “What this means for your pension fund”
  • Standard pension fund infrastructure portfolio:
    • ~10% allocation to infrastructure
    • Duration: 15-25 years average
    • Reported NAVs: smooth, stable
  • Under GE-LAV:
    • In stressed regime, reported NAVs overstate true value by 30-57%
    • Hedge demand calls for ~3-5 percentage points lower allocation
    • Stress test exposes 40-60% downside (vs. 25% under standard methodology)
  • Speaker notes: Connect to specific public pension funds (CalPERS, CalSTRS, Oregon, ABP) — all heavily exposed to infrastructure.

Slide T1-27.4 — Insurance Implications

  • Title: “Insurance regulatory implications”
  • Solvency II treatment:
    • Long-duration infrastructure: capital charge 25-39%
    • Matched against long-term liabilities (annuities, life)
    • Conventional wisdom: “good match” between asset and liability duration
  • GE-LAV finding:
    • Asset side has hidden stress exposure (the 57% illusion)
    • Liability side does not adjust during liquidity stress
    • Match is worse than reported
  • Speaker notes: This is a regulatory angle. The Solvency II review process in EU is the relevant policy channel.

Slide T1-27.5 — Lab Setup

  • Title: “Lab: value the Indianapolis Toll Road”
  • Setup:
    • 75-year concession (acquired 2006 for $3.85B)
    • Annual toll revenues ~$200M, growing 2%/yr
    • LP investor: ITR Concession Co (Macquarie, etc.)
    • Question: what’s it worth today under DCF vs. GE-LAV? Where would secondaries clear?
  • Group output:
    • DCF baseline
    • GE-LAV at normal liquidity
    • GE-LAV at GFC-depth stress
    • “If you had to sell today, what’s your reservation price?”
  • Speaker notes: Real asset. Real numbers. Tangible.

36.2 Track 2: Fokker-Planck & Master Equation Derivations

36.2.1 Track 2 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, Track 2 students will be able to:

  1. Derive the Fokker-Planck equation from the SDE via Chapman-Kolmogorov.

36.2.4 Track 2 Slide Briefs (New Slides Needed)

Slide T2-27.1 — Derivation: Fokker-Planck from Chapman-Kolmogorov

  • Title: “Deriving FP from first principles”
  • Step 1: Chapman-Kolmogorov equation:
    • \(p(x, t+\Delta t) = \int p(x, t+\Delta t | y, t) \cdot p(y, t) dy\)
  • Step 2: Transition kernel for \(dX = f dt + g dW\) at small \(\Delta t\):
    • \(p(x, t+\Delta t | y, t) \approx \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi g^2 \Delta t}} \exp\left(-\dfrac{(x - y - f \Delta t)^2}{2 g^2 \Delta t}\right)\)
  • Step 3: Taylor expand \(p(y, t)\) around \(y = x\):
    • \(p(y, t) = p(x, t) + (y-x) p_x(x, t) + \frac{1}{2}(y-x)^2 p_{xx}(x, t) + O((y-x)^3)\)
  • Step 4: Substitute, integrate, take \(\Delta t \to 0\):
    • \(\dfrac{\partial p}{\partial t} = -\dfrac{\partial}{\partial x}[f p] + \dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}[g^2 p]\)
  • Speaker notes: Walk through each step. This is the fundamental derivation. Students should be able to reproduce.

Slide T2-27.2 — Well-Posedness (Theorem 14.2)

  • Title: “Well-posedness of the GE-LAV Fokker-Planck”
  • Theorem 14.2: For the GE-LAV FP equation with calibrated drift and diffusion:
    • Existence: A weak solution \(p(L, t)\) exists for all \(T > 0\)
    • Uniqueness: The solution is unique in \(L^2([0, T] \times \mathbb{R})\)
    • Regularity: \(p\) is smooth in the interior; satisfies \(C^\infty\)
  • Proof sketch:
    • Hörmander’s hypoellipticity theorem (sufficient regularity from nondegenerate diffusion)
    • \(L^2\) energy estimates for uniqueness
    • Maximum principle for boundedness
  • Speaker notes: State the theorem rigorously. Sketch the structure of the proof. Refer to book for full argument.

Slide T2-27.3 — Stationary Distribution Derivation

  • Title: “Stationary density of OU”
  • Setup: Set \(\partial p / \partial t = 0\) in the FP equation:
    • \(0 = -\kappa \dfrac{d}{dL}[(\bar{L} - L) p_\infty(L)] + \dfrac{\sigma_L^2}{2} \dfrac{d^2}{dL^2} p_\infty(L)\)
  • Solve via integrating factor:
    • Multiply by \(\exp(\kappa(L - \bar{L})^2 / \sigma_L^2)\)
    • Get \(\dfrac{d}{dL}[\) flux \(] = 0\)
    • Therefore flux \(= 0\) (since density decays at \(\pm\infty\))
    • Setting flux to zero: \(-\kappa(\bar{L} - L) p + \frac{\sigma_L^2}{2} p' = 0\)
  • Solve ODE: \(p_\infty(L) = C \exp(-\kappa(L - \bar{L})^2 / \sigma_L^2)\)
  • Normalize: \(C = \sqrt{2\kappa / (\pi \sigma_L^2)}\), giving Gaussian with mean \(\bar{L}\), variance \(\sigma_L^2/(2\kappa)\)
  • Speaker notes: This is the derivation Track 1 students saw the result of in Session 4. Now Track 2 sees the derivation.

Slide T2-27.4 — The Master Equation on Wasserstein Space

  • Title: “Master equation: HJB for MFG value functions”
  • Setting:
    • Value function \(V(L, t, \mu)\) where \(\mu\) is the population distribution
    • \(\mu\) lives in Wasserstein space \(\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R})\)
  • Master equation (informally):
    • \(\dfrac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \mathcal{L} V - r(L) V + \mathcal{H}(V_\mu) + C = 0\)
  • Where:
    • \(\mathcal{L}V\) = standard HJB operator on \(L\)
    • \(V_\mu = \delta V / \delta \mu\) = Wasserstein gradient
    • \(\mathcal{H}(V_\mu)\) = Hamiltonian coupling individual to population
  • Speaker notes: This is the most abstract slide of the session. Don’t expect deep mastery — just recognition that the master equation exists and is solvable in principle.

Slide T2-27.5 — Numerical FP Solver

  • Title: “Implementing the FP solver”

  • Pseudocode:

    Initialize: p_0(L_i) = delta at L_initial
For each time step:
  Apply finite-difference discretization
  Use Crank-Nicolson for stability
  Enforce boundary conditions: p → 0 at |L| → ∞
  Normalize: sum to 1
Output: p(L, t) for all t

  • Convergence check: As \(t \to T\) large, numerical \(p(L, T) \to\) analytical stationary

  • Performance: ~seconds for full GE-LAV time series

  • Speaker notes: Provide complete pseudocode. Students should be able to implement in <100 lines of Python.

36.2.6 Track 2 Discussion Questions

  1. The FP derivation assumes the transition density is Gaussian at small \(\Delta t\). This holds for Itô SDEs with smooth coefficients. What if there are jumps (Lévy processes)? How does the FP change?
  2. The master equation is on Wasserstein space — an infinite-dimensional manifold. Numerically, we discretize \(\mu\) on a grid. What’s the discretization error? When does this matter?
  3. The well-posedness theorem requires nondegeneracy (\(\sigma_L > 0\)). What happens if \(\sigma_L = 0\) (deterministic OU)? Does the framework break down?
