20  Session 11: Exit Timing · The Trapped Investor Problem

Unit 3 — Decision and Application

20.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. Frame the LP’s exit decision as an optimal stopping problem with stochastic state.
  2. Distinguish between forced exit (regulatory, operational) and discretionary exit (optimization).
  3. Identify the four conditions under which an LP becomes “trapped” — unable to exit at fair value despite wanting to.
  4. State the smooth pasting condition at the optimal exit boundary, at the intuition level (Track 2 will derive this in Session 25).
  5. Compute the value of holding vs. exiting for a representative position using the LAV operator.

20.4 Slide Content Briefs (New Slides Needed)

20.4.1 Slide 11.1 — Title and Unit 3 Setup

  • Title: “Exit Timing · The Trapped Investor”
  • Tag line: “Unit 3 begins: from diagnosis to decision”
  • Unit 3 preview: “Sessions 11–18 build the GE-LAV alternative: exit timing, portfolio, regulation, implementation”
  • Speaker notes: Pivot moment. Less critique, more building.

20.4.2 Slide 11.2 — The Exit Decision in Practice

  • Title: “What an LP actually faces”
  • Setup: “An LP holds a 7-year PE position currently 4 years in. NAV reported by GP: $50M. Recent secondary market bid: $42M (16% discount). What should they do?”
  • Standard practice:
    • Most LPs hold to natural liquidation (no decision)
    • Some LPs sell to secondaries when forced (denominator effect, liquidity needs)
    • Very few LPs systematically optimize the exit decision
  • GE-LAV question: “What’s the optimal policy as a function of \(L_t\)?”
  • Speaker notes: Frame the gap between practice and theory. Most LPs don’t optimize because they don’t have the framework.

20.4.3 Slide 11.3 — The Optimal Stopping Formulation

  • Title: “Exit as an optimal stopping problem”
  • Setup:
    • At any time \(t\), the LP can:
      • Hold: receive future distributions per the GP plan
      • Exit: sell into the secondary market at price \(P_\text{secondary}(L_t)\)
    • The LP wants to maximize expected discounted value
  • Value function: \(V(L, t) = \max\{ P_\text{secondary}(L), E[V_\text{hold}(L_t, t)] \}\)
  • Intuition: “Hold if expected hold value exceeds secondary price; otherwise exit”
  • Speaker notes: This is a textbook American option style problem. Students with derivatives background will recognize the structure.

20.4.4 Slide 11.4 — The Exit Boundary \(L^*(t)\) (Preview)

  • Title: “There is an optimal \(L^*\) — and we can compute it”
  • Setup:
    • For each time \(t\) remaining, there’s a critical liquidity level \(L^*(t)\)
    • Above \(L^*\): Hold (better expected value from waiting)
    • Below \(L^*\): Exit (secondary price exceeds expected hold value)
  • Boundary shape: \(L^*(t)\) rises as \(t \to T\) (near maturity, only marginally stressed liquidity should trigger exit)
  • Calibrated benchmark: “For typical mid-life PE position, \(L^* \approx -1.0\) — moderate stress”
  • Speaker notes: Numerical computation in Session 12. Today is conceptual.

20.4.5 Slide 11.5 — Smooth Pasting (Intuition Only)

  • Title: “Why \(L^*\) is special: smooth pasting”
  • Concept:
    • At \(L^*\), the value of holding equals the value of exiting
    • At \(L^*\), the slope of the hold value with respect to \(L\) also equals the slope of the exit value
    • This “smooth pasting” condition determines \(L^*\) uniquely
  • Visual: Two curves (hold value, exit value) meeting tangentially at \(L^*\)
  • Track 2 note: “Full derivation in Session 25 — for now, accept it as the optimality condition”
  • Speaker notes: Don’t derive. Just establish that smooth pasting is how we compute \(L^*\). Track 2 students will see this for real with HJB and variational inequalities.

20.4.6 Slide 11.6 — The Four Trapped Investor Scenarios

  • Title: “When an LP is ‘trapped’”
  • Definition: “Trapped = unable to exit at fair value despite wanting to exit”
  • Four scenarios:
    1. Liquidity stress: \(L_t \ll L^*\), secondary market discounts widen beyond LAV value → exit destroys value
    2. Denominator effect: Public market decline forces PE % allocation above policy limit → LP must sell at bad time
    3. Regulatory constraint: Solvency II / Basel capital charge rises with stress → insurer must reduce private exposure
    4. Funding obligation: LP needs cash to meet pension payouts or other liabilities → must sell regardless of price
  • Speaker notes: Scenarios 2–4 are forced exits; scenario 1 is value-destroying voluntary exit. Both create trapped behavior.

20.6 Discussion Questions

  1. CalPERS publicly sold PE secondaries at significant discounts in 2009. Were they “trapped” by scenario 1, 2, 3, or 4 from today’s slide? Could they have avoided the discount through better planning?
  2. The IPEV valuation guidelines suggest GPs should “hold to liquidation when feasible.” Does this guideline implicitly assume scenario 1 doesn’t exist? What would the guideline say if it acknowledged GE-LAV?
  3. A pension fund’s actuarial assumption is 7% expected return on private markets. If GE-LAV-optimal exit timing reduces realized returns by ~50 bps relative to “hold to liquidation,” is the actuarial assumption violated? Or is the realized return improvement on a risk-adjusted basis still positive?

20.7 Worked Numerical Example: Hold vs. Exit Decision

Setup: An LP holds a 5-year remaining PE position. Reported NAV: $100M. Current liquidity state: \(L_t = -0.8\) (moderately stressed). OU parameters: κ = 0.45, σ = 0.32, \(\bar{L} = 1.0\).

Step 1: Expected hold value

Under OU dynamics, \(E[L_{t+5}] = \bar{L} + (L_t - \bar{L}) e^{-5\kappa} = 1.0 + (-0.8 - 1.0) \times e^{-2.25} = 1.0 + (-1.8)(0.105) = 0.81\)

The expected liquidity state recovers from −0.8 toward \(\bar{L} = 1.0\) over 5 years, ending at +0.81.

Using \(\pi(L) = 0.045 - 0.025L + 0.021L^2\): - Average premium over 5-year horizon ≈ \(\pi(0.0) = 0.045 - 0 + 0 = 4.5\%\) (rough average of current and future) - More carefully: integrate path; result ~ 4.0% effective

Hold value (Jensen-adjusted LAV): \(V_\text{hold} \approx \$100M \times (1 + \text{Jensen bias at 5 years})\) With \(A = 0.16\%\)/yr (buyout): \(B(5) = 0.8\%\) \(V_\text{hold} \approx \$100.8M\)

Step 2: Current secondary price

At \(L_t = -0.8\), secondary discount calibration suggests a 20% discount to NAV: \(P_\text{secondary} = \$100M \times (1 - 0.20) = \$80M\)

Step 3: Compare

\(V_\text{hold} = \$100.8M\) vs. \(P_\text{secondary} = \$80M\)

Decision: Hold. The mean-reversion benefit (≈$20M expected over 5 years) exceeds any liquidity insurance value at this stress level.

Step 4: Where would exit make sense?

If \(L_t\) dropped to \(-1.5\) (GFC depth) and secondary discount widened to 40%: - \(P_\text{secondary} = \$60M\) - But \(V_\text{hold}\) also drops (higher expected premium during recovery period) — maybe to ~\(\$70M\) - At \(L_t = -1.5\), hold still slightly dominates exit for a 5-year position - For shorter remaining horizons (e.g., 2 years), the trade flips: exit becomes optimal because there’s less time for mean reversion

Teaching note: Walk through this on the board. Students should leave with a quantitative feel for when exit makes sense.

20.8 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “Why is mean reversion the dominant force here?” Because \(\kappa = 0.45\)/yr is moderately fast — over 5 years, \(L_t\) reverts substantially toward \(\bar{L}\). Short positions don’t have this luxury.
  • “What if I think the secondary market is underpricing my position?” That’s exactly what the hold-vs-exit calculation tests. If \(V_\text{hold} > P_\text{secondary}\), you agree the secondary is underpricing, and you hold.
  • “How is this different from American option exercise?” Mathematically very similar. Both use optimal stopping. Difference: PE has more complex cash flow structure (J-curve, distributions over time, not a single payoff).
  • “Does the GP have a role in the LP’s exit decision?” No directly — secondary market transactions don’t require GP consent under most LPAs. But GPs often have right of first refusal, and GP attitude affects valuation. Practical complication beyond pure theory.

  • The \(L^*(t)\) curve across remaining horizons
  • Sensitivity to OU parameters and asset class
  • Historical episodes where the exit rule would have changed outcomes
