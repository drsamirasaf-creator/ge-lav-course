32.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. State the LAV operator in plain language — how a single asset’s value depends on the entire \(L_t\) path.
  2. Distinguish the LAV operator from a standard DCF operator (constant rate) and from a simple Jensen-corrected operator.
  3. Articulate what “GE equilibrium” means in the GE-LAV framework: the simultaneous determination of \(\lambda^*\) and individual decisions.
  4. Describe market clearing in the secondary market: how the equilibrium price reflects the marginal investor’s required premium.
  5. Identify the valuation hierarchy mathematically: DCF ⊊ LAV ⊊ GE-LAV as nested operators.

32.4.2 Slide 23.2 — Where We Are

  • Title: “Putting it together”
  • Building blocks (from Sessions 19–22):
    • Stochastic process: \(L_t\) follows OU dynamics
    • Optimal control: HJB gives individual exit decisions
    • Mean-field games: Population coupling via McKean-Vlasov
    • Distribution dynamics: Fokker-Planck and master equation track the population
  • Today: Combine these to compute valuations (LAV operator) and equilibrium prices (GE-LAV)
  • Speaker notes: Show the synthesis arc visually. Each piece was needed.

32.4.3 Slide 23.3 — The LAV Operator (Definition)

  • Title: “The Liquidity-Adjusted Valuation operator”
  • Formal definition (boxed):
    • \(V_0^{LAV} = E^Q\left[ \int_0^T C_t \cdot \exp\left(-\int_0^t r(L_s) ds\right) dt \right]\)
  • What this says:
    • \(C_t\): Expected cash flows from the asset
    • \(r(L_s)\): Discount rate at time \(s\), dependent on liquidity state
    • \(\int_0^t r(L_s) ds\): Cumulative discount factor along the path of \(L\)
    • \(\exp(-\cdot)\): Discount factor applied to cash flow
    • \(E^Q\): Expectation under risk-neutral measure
  • Plain English: “Discount each cash flow by the path-integrated discount rate, then take the expectation across all possible paths.”
  • Speaker notes: This is the operational formula behind LAV. Track 2 will derive its properties in Session 28; Track 1 needs the structure.

32.4.4 Slide 23.4 — How LAV Differs from DCF

  • Title: “LAV vs. DCF: side-by-side”
  • DCF: \(V_0^{DCF} = \sum_{t=0}^T \dfrac{C_t}{(1+r)^t}\) — constant \(r\)
  • LAV: \(V_0^{LAV} = E^Q[\int_0^T C_t e^{-\int_0^t r(L_s) ds} dt]\) — path-dependent \(r(L_s)\)
  • Key differences:
    • Path dependence: LAV depends on the full path of \(L_t\), not just current value
    • Expectation operator: LAV integrates across the distribution of paths
    • Convexity correction built-in: Jensen bias is naturally produced
  • Bottom-line: “LAV is the honest generalization of DCF when discount rates are stochastic.”
  • Speaker notes: This is the slide that students should memorize. The LAV/DCF distinction is the conceptual heart of the framework.

32.4.5 Slide 23.5 — LAV Properties

  • Title: “Key properties of LAV (proofs in Track 2, Session 28)”
  • Property 1: Strict path dependence
    • \(V^{LAV}\) depends on the full path of \(L_t\), not just the terminal value
    • Theorem 16.1 in book — formal statement
  • Property 2: Positive Jensen bias for convex \(r(L)\)
    • \(V^{LAV} > V^{DCF}\) whenever \(r(L)\) is convex in \(L\)
    • This is always true for the calibrated quadratic premium \(\pi(L)\)
  • Property 3: Reduction to DCF under deterministic \(L\)
    • If \(L_t\) is constant, LAV = DCF
    • This is the limiting case
  • Speaker notes: These properties are what make the framework rigorous. State them; don’t prove.

32.4.6 Slide 23.6 — GE Equilibrium (Setup)

  • Title: “From LAV to GE-LAV: adding market clearing”
  • The setup:
    • LAV uses an exogenous discount rate function \(r(L)\)
    • In general equilibrium, \(r(L)\) is endogenous — determined by the market
    • Each LP wants a premium based on their needs; the market clears at the marginal LP’s required premium
  • The fixed point:
    • Population distribution \(\mu_t\) → equilibrium premium \(\lambda^*\) → LP decisions → new \(\mu_t\)
    • GE-LAV equilibrium: this loop is closed; \(\mu_t\) is consistent with \(\lambda^*\)
  • Speaker notes: Connect to Session 21’s MFG fixed-point structure. Same idea, applied to pricing.

32.4.7 Slide 23.7 — Market Clearing

  • Title: “How the secondary market clears”
  • The clearing condition:
    • At each \(L_t\), sellers (LPs wanting to exit at current premium) = buyers (LPs willing to buy at current premium)
    • The equilibrium premium \(\lambda^*(L_t, \mu_t)\) is the value that clears
  • Calibrated structure:
    • At normal \(L\): \(\lambda^* \approx \pi_0 = 4.5\%\) (close to long-run mean)
    • At GFC \(L \approx -1.5\): \(\lambda^* \approx 13\%\) (much wider due to externality)
  • Visual: Supply and demand curves intersecting at \(\lambda^*\), with both shifting as \(L\) changes
  • Speaker notes: Demonstrate how the equilibrium price emerges. This is standard microeconomics applied to the secondary market.

32.4.8 Slide 23.8 — GE-LAV Operator

  • Title: “The GE-LAV operator”
  • Formal definition:
    • \(V_0^{GE-LAV} = E^Q\left[ \int_0^T C_t \cdot \exp\left(-\int_0^t r_{GE}(L_s, \mu_s) ds\right) dt \right]\)
  • The new ingredient: \(r_{GE}(L, \mu) = r_f + \beta \cdot \text{ERP} + \lambda^*(L, \mu)\)
  • Where \(\lambda^*(L, \mu)\) is the equilibrium premium — depends on both the individual state \(L\) and the population state \(\mu\)
  • Key property (proved in Session 28):
    • In deep stress regimes, \(\lambda^*(L, \mu) \gg \pi(L)\) — the equilibrium premium exceeds the partial-equilibrium premium
    • This is the McKean-Vlasov externality manifested in pricing
  • Speaker notes: GE-LAV is LAV with one additional ingredient. Make sure students see the layering.

32.4.9 Slide 23.9 — The Crisis Amplification, Quantified

  • Title: “Where the 4.31× amplification comes from”
  • At normal liquidity (\(L = 0\)):
    • \(r_{GE-LAV}(0, \mu_\text{normal}) \approx 7.5\%\) (close to DCF rate)
  • At GFC depth (\(L = -1.5\)):
    • Partial equilibrium premium: \(\pi(-1.5) \approx 13\%\)
    • GE-LAV equilibrium premium: \(\lambda^*(-1.5, \mu_\text{stressed}) \approx 28\%\) (much higher due to coordinated stress)
    • Total \(r_{GE-LAV}\): ~32.3% (vs. DCF 7.5%)
  • Amplification: \(32.3 / 7.5 = 4.31×\)
  • Decomposition:
    • Part 1: ~5% from LAV path correction (Jensen bias)
    • Part 2: ~20% from GE equilibrium uplift (McKean-Vlasov externality)
  • Speaker notes: This is the number from Session 16 (Crisis Dynamics). Now students see where it mathematically comes from.

32.4.10 Slide 23.10 — The Valuation Hierarchy (Mathematically)

  • Title: “DCF ⊊ LAV ⊊ GE-LAV: strict nesting”
  • Statement (theorem 20.1 in book):
    • \(V^{DCF} \leq V^{LAV} \leq V^{GE-LAV}\) in normal conditions
    • Inequalities can reverse in deep stress (GE-LAV can be much lower than DCF when externality dominates)
    • Equality conditions:
      • \(V^{DCF} = V^{LAV}\) iff \(L_t\) is deterministic
      • \(V^{LAV} = V^{GE-LAV}\) iff there is no MFG externality (\(\gamma \to 0\))
  • In words: “Each level of the hierarchy is a strict generalization of the previous. The simpler operator is a special case.”
  • Speaker notes: This is the theorem of the framework. State the result; don’t prove. Track 2 derives in Session 28+.

32.4.11 Slide 23.11 — What This Means for Practitioners

  • Title: “Practitioner takeaways”
  • For valuation:
    • Default to GE-LAV in stressed regimes; LAV in normal; DCF only when state is known deterministic
  • For portfolio construction:
    • The GE-LAV operator accounts for the systematic effect of stress on private market values
    • Multi-asset hedge demand correctly aggregates this across positions
  • For risk management:
    • The amplification factor is the appropriate stress scenario, not the partial-equilibrium estimate
  • For regulation:
    • Solvency II and Basel III stress factors should be calibrated against GE-LAV magnitudes
  • Speaker notes: Connect math to practice.

32.6 Discussion Questions

  1. The valuation hierarchy DCF ⊊ LAV ⊊ GE-LAV implies that DCF is a special case of GE-LAV (vanishing liquidity volatility) and LAV is a special case of GE-LAV (vanishing MFG coupling). Does this nesting make the framework “obviously correct,” or does it just mean GE-LAV has more parameters to fit?
  2. The 4.31× amplification factor decomposes into ~5% LAV path correction + ~20% GE externality uplift. Which decomposition piece is more important for regulators? For practitioners managing portfolios?
  3. The GE equilibrium requires solving a fixed-point problem (HJB + Fokker-Planck + market clearing). What’s the practical implication if the fixed-point has multiple solutions (e.g., in extreme stress when stability condition fails)?

32.7 Worked Example: Computing LAV vs. GE-LAV vs. DCF for One Asset

Setup: 10-year PE buyout fund. Annual cash flow stream: \(\$10M\)/year. Current liquidity state \(L_0 = -0.5\).

Step 1: DCF

Using constant rate \(r_{DCF} = 12\%\):

\(V^{DCF} = \sum_{t=1}^{10} \dfrac{10}{1.12^t} = 56.5M\)

Step 2: LAV (path-dependent, partial equilibrium)

Need to integrate \(E[\exp(-\int_0^t r(L_s) ds)]\) over the OU path distribution.

At calibrated parameters and starting state \(L_0 = -0.5\):

  • Expected path of \(L_s\): mean reverts from -0.5 toward 1.0 over time
  • Expected \(\pi(L_s)\): starts at \(\pi(-0.5) = 0.058\), decreases to \(\pi(1.0) = 0.041\) at long horizon
  • Effective average \(r(L)\) over 10 years: approximately 7.5% + 0.045 = \(11.5%\)
  • Including Jensen correction: approximately 0.7%

\(V^{LAV} \approx \sum_{t=1}^{10} \dfrac{10}{1.115^t} \cdot (1 + 0.7\%) \approx 58.2M\)

Step 3: GE-LAV (full equilibrium)

At normal liquidity, equilibrium premium ≈ partial equilibrium premium. So GE-LAV ≈ LAV at \(L_0 = -0.5\).

\(V^{GE-LAV} \approx 58.0M\)

Step 4: Now stress the scenario

Suppose we evaluate at \(L_0 = -1.5\) (GFC conditions):

  • DCF (using same fixed rate): still $56.5M (DCF is regime-blind)
  • LAV (partial eq): premium higher, \(V^{LAV} \approx 38M\)
  • GE-LAV (full eq): equilibrium premium amplifies, \(V^{GE-LAV} \approx 22M\)

Comparison at GFC depth:

Method Value Gap from DCF
DCF $56.5M —
LAV $38M -33%
GE-LAV $22M -61%

Interpretation: “DCF says $56.5M regardless. LAV says $38M because the path-corrected discount rate is higher. GE-LAV says $22M because the equilibrium premium amplifies under collective stress. The 4.31× amplification factor shows up in this comparison.”

Teaching note: This is the quantitative manifestation of the framework’s hierarchy. Make sure students see the dollar magnitudes.

32.8 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “Why does GE-LAV give a lower value than DCF? Shouldn’t a more sophisticated model give a higher value (more nuanced)?” GE-LAV is more nuanced; it gives a more accurate value. In stress, the correct value is lower. The framework is right.
  • “If DCF systematically overprices in stress and underprices normally (via Jensen bias), the average should be wash, right?” Not quite. Asymmetric distribution of stress (rare but large) means DCF on average overstates value in expectation. Stafford (2022) makes this argument empirically.
  • “What if I have empirical \(L_t\) data? Should I compute LAV directly from the data, or use the parametric GE-LAV?” Both. Parametric provides robust forecasting; empirical provides validation. Best practice uses both.
  • “Is GE-LAV the ‘final’ framework, or will something supersede it?” GE-LAV is the first framework that satisfies the five requirements (Session 8). Extensions (jumps, regime shifts) are research frontiers. The structure of GE-LAV is likely durable; specific calibrations will evolve.

- **Amplification:** $32.3 / 7.5 = 4.31×$
- **Decomposition:**
  - **Part 1:** ~5% from LAV path correction (Jensen bias)
  - **Part 2:** ~20% from GE equilibrium uplift (McKean-Vlasov externality)
- **Speaker notes:** This is *the* number from Session 16 (Crisis Dynamics). Now students see where it mathematically comes from.

### Slide 23.10 — The Valuation Hierarchy (Mathematically)

- **Title:** "DCF ⊊ LAV ⊊ GE-LAV: strict nesting"
- **Statement (theorem 20.1 in book):**
  - $V^{DCF} \leq V^{LAV} \leq V^{GE-LAV}$ in normal conditions
  - Inequalities can reverse in deep stress (GE-LAV can be much lower than DCF when externality dominates)
  - Equality conditions:
    - $V^{DCF} = V^{LAV}$ iff $L_t$ is deterministic
    - $V^{LAV} = V^{GE-LAV}$ iff there is no MFG externality ($\gamma \to 0$)
- **In words:** "Each level of the hierarchy is a *strict generalization* of the previous. The simpler operator is a special case."
- **Speaker notes:** This is the *theorem* of the framework. State the result; don't prove. Track 2 derives in Session 28+.

### Slide 23.11 — What This Means for Practitioners

- **Title:** "Practitioner takeaways"
- **For valuation:**
  - Default to GE-LAV in stressed regimes; LAV in normal; DCF only when state is known deterministic
- **For portfolio construction:**
  - The GE-LAV operator accounts for the *systematic* effect of stress on private market values
  - Multi-asset hedge demand correctly aggregates this across positions
- **For risk management:**
  - The amplification factor is the appropriate stress scenario, not the partial-equilibrium estimate
- **For regulation:**
  - Solvency II and Basel III stress factors should be calibrated against GE-LAV magnitudes

## Worked Example: Computing LAV vs. GE-LAV vs. DCF for One Asset

**Setup:** 10-year PE buyout fund. Annual cash flow stream: $\$10M$/year. Current liquidity state $L_0 = -0.5$.

**Step 1: DCF**

Using constant rate $r_{DCF} = 12\%$:

$V^{DCF} = \sum_{t=1}^{10} \dfrac{10}{1.12^t} = 56.5M$

**Step 2: LAV (path-dependent, partial equilibrium)**

Need to integrate $E[\exp(-\int_0^t r(L_s) ds)]$ over the OU path distribution.

At calibrated parameters and starting state $L_0 = -0.5$:

- Expected path of $L_s$: mean reverts from -0.5 toward 1.0 over time
- Expected $\pi(L_s)$: starts at $\pi(-0.5) = 0.058$, decreases to $\pi(1.0) = 0.041$ at long horizon
- Effective average $r(L)$ over 10 years: approximately 7.5% + 0.045 = $11.5%$
- Including Jensen correction: approximately 0.7%

$V^{LAV} \approx \sum_{t=1}^{10} \dfrac{10}{1.115^t} \cdot (1 + 0.7\%) \approx 58.2M$

**Step 3: GE-LAV (full equilibrium)**

At normal liquidity, equilibrium premium ≈ partial equilibrium premium. So GE-LAV ≈ LAV at $L_0 = -0.5$.

$V^{GE-LAV} \approx 58.0M$

**Step 4: Now stress the scenario**

Suppose we evaluate at $L_0 = -1.5$ (GFC conditions):

- DCF (using same fixed rate): still $56.5M (DCF is regime-blind)
- LAV (partial eq): premium higher, $V^{LAV} \approx 38M$
- **GE-LAV (full eq):** equilibrium premium amplifies, $V^{GE-LAV} \approx 22M$

**Comparison at GFC depth:**

| Method | Value | Gap from DCF |
|---|---|---|
| DCF | $56.5M | — |
| LAV | $38M | -33% |
| GE-LAV | $22M | -61% |

**Interpretation:** "DCF says $56.5M regardless. LAV says $38M because the path-corrected discount rate is higher. GE-LAV says $22M because the *equilibrium* premium amplifies under collective stress. The 4.31× amplification factor shows up in this comparison."

**Teaching note:** This is the *quantitative* manifestation of the framework's hierarchy. Make sure students see the dollar magnitudes.

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"Why does GE-LAV give a *lower* value than DCF? Shouldn't a more sophisticated model give a *higher* value (more nuanced)?"** GE-LAV is more nuanced; it gives a *more accurate* value. In stress, the correct value is lower. The framework is right.
- **"If DCF systematically overprices in stress and underprices normally (via Jensen bias), the average should be wash, right?"** Not quite. Asymmetric distribution of stress (rare but large) means DCF on average overstates value in expectation. Stafford (2022) makes this argument empirically.
- **"What if I have empirical $L_t$ data? Should I compute LAV directly from the data, or use the parametric GE-LAV?"** Both. Parametric provides robust forecasting; empirical provides validation. Best practice uses both.
- **"Is GE-LAV the 'final' framework, or will something supersede it?"** GE-LAV is the first framework that satisfies the five requirements (Session 8). Extensions (jumps, regime shifts) are research frontiers. The *structure* of GE-LAV is likely durable; specific calibrations will evolve.
