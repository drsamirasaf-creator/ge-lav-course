13  Session 3: The Liquidity Illusion · Secondary Market Evidence

Unit 1 — Why DCF Fails
Book Chapter 2 (sections 2.1–2.7)
Slide source New (~12 slides) + Classes 21–22 excerpts
Slides needed ~12 new
Track Common core (both tracks)

13.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. Describe the structure of the private market secondary market — who trades, why, through which brokers, at what volume.
  2. Interpret the discount-to-NAV ratio as a market-implied measurement of the aggregate liquidity premium.
  3. Identify the four regimes of secondary market discounts 2003–2024 and describe what economic conditions characterize each.
  4. Define the liquidity illusion rigorously: the gap between GP-reported NAV and economic clearing value.
  5. Distinguish level illusion (level of NAV is wrong) from convexity illusion (NAV’s sensitivity to liquidity state is wrong).

13.4 Slide Content Briefs (New Slides Needed)

13.4.1 Slide 3.1 — Title and Recap

  • Title: “The Liquidity Illusion: What Secondary Markets Reveal”
  • Tag line: “Failure 1, examined empirically”
  • Speaker notes: Today is the evidence day. Session 2 told them the premium is stochastic. Today we look at the data.

13.4.2 Slide 3.2 — Secondary Market: Structure

  • Title: “The PE secondary market — who participates”
  • Layout: Diagram or three-column grid
  • Sellers:
    • LPs needing liquidity (denominator effect, funding obligations, portfolio rebalancing)
    • LPs exiting underperforming relationships
    • Institutional restructurings (insurance run-offs, sovereign wealth reallocation)
  • Buyers:
    • Dedicated secondaries funds (Ardian, Lexington, Coller, HarbourVest, Pantheon)
    • Strategic buyers (other GPs acquiring stakes in companies they know)
    • LP-led tender offers
  • Brokers/intermediaries: Lazard, Evercore, Goldman Sachs Asset Management, Hamilton Lane, Setter Capital, PJT Park Hill
  • Speaker notes: Students may not know how this market is organized. Establish basic plumbing.

13.4.3 Slide 3.3 — Market Scale

  • Title: “Secondary market in numbers”
  • Stat callouts:
    • $130B+ Annual transaction volume (2024)
    • ~1,200 Transactions per year tracked by Preqin
    • 5–8% of total PE NAV trades through secondaries each year
    • Growing 12–15%/yr since 2015
  • Bottom-line: “Large enough to be informationally relevant; structured enough to extract a price signal”
  • Speaker notes: This is not a distressed market. It’s a real, deep, growing market.

13.4.4 Slide 3.4 — The Discount-to-NAV Ratio

  • Title: “Reading the price signal”
  • Equation: \(\text{Discount} = 1 - \dfrac{\text{Secondary transaction price}}{\text{GP-reported NAV}}\)
  • Worked example:
    • GP reports NAV of $100M for an LP position
    • Secondary buyer pays $85M cash
    • Discount = 15%
  • Translation to liquidity premium: “For a 5-year hold position with normal mean reversion, a 15% discount corresponds to roughly a +5% addition to the implied annual discount rate over the hold period”
  • Speaker notes: This is the data we observe. Everything that follows is interpreting this number across time and assets.

13.4.5 Slide 3.5 — The Four Regimes (Overview)

  • Title: “Four regimes of secondary discounts (2003–2024)”
  • Visual: Full time series chart 2003–2024, shaded by regime
  • Regimes:
    • 2003–2007: Boom (discounts 0–10%)
    • 2008–2010: Crisis (discounts 25–50%)
    • 2011–2019: Normalization (discounts 5–15%)
    • 2020–2024: Multi-shock era (discounts 10–25% with volatility)
  • Speaker notes: Each regime gets one slide of detail.

13.4.6 Slide 3.6 — Regime 1: The Boom (2003–2007)

  • Title: “The boom: cheap money, no shocks”
  • Key features:
    • Tight discounts (often 0–5%)
    • High deal volume but driven by GP-side restructurings, not LP distress
    • Liquidity state \(L_t\) persistently above \(\bar{L}\)
    • Implied premium: 1–3% (vs. long-run 3.5%)
  • Speaker notes: This is the regime DCF was calibrated against. The “constant premium” assumption is least wrong here.

13.4.7 Slide 3.7 — Regime 2: The Crisis (2008–2010)

  • Title: “The crisis: the constant-premium assumption breaks”
  • Key features:
    • Discounts widen to 35–50%
    • Volume initially collapses (no buyers willing to bid), then recovers in mid-2009 as opportunistic buyers enter
    • Liquidity state \(L_t\) at −1.5σ or worse
    • Implied premium peak: ~28%
  • Anecdote: “In Q1 2009, some endowments sold PE positions at 60% of NAV to meet liquidity needs. CalPERS publicly disclosed selling secondaries at large discounts to rebalance.”
  • Speaker notes: This is the regime that should have killed the constant-premium assumption. It didn’t, because there was no theoretical alternative.

13.4.8 Slide 3.8 — Regime 3: Normalization (2011–2019)

  • Title: “The normalization: false comfort”
  • Key features:
    • Discounts settle at 5–15%
    • Deal volume grows steadily
    • GP-led secondaries (continuation funds) become common
    • Implied premium: 4–7%
  • Speaker notes: Practitioners drew the wrong lesson from this period. “The crisis was a one-off; the normal state is stable.” Wrong.

13.4.9 Slide 3.9 — Regime 4: Multi-Shock Era (2020–2024)

  • Title: “The multi-shock era: volatility is the new normal”
  • Key shocks:
    • March 2020: COVID liquidity shock — discounts spike to 15–25%, then recover
    • 2022: Rate shock + denominator effect — discounts widen to 15–20% sustained
    • 2023–24: Persistent elevated discounts as rates stayed high
  • Implied premium: 8–15% sustained
  • Speaker notes: This regime is the strongest argument for GE-LAV. Repeated shocks, persistent dislocation, no return to pre-2020 calm.

13.4.10 Slide 3.10 — The Liquidity Illusion Defined

  • Title: “What is the liquidity illusion?”
  • Definition (boxed): “The systematic gap between GP-reported NAV (computed via DCF with constant premium) and the economic clearing value (computed via GE-LAV equilibrium).”
  • Decomposition:
    • Level illusion: Level of GP-reported NAV is wrong (Failure 1: stochastic premium)
    • Convexity illusion: Sensitivity of NAV to changes in liquidity state is wrong (Failure 2: Jensen bias)
  • Bottom-line: “Both illusions persist because the GP’s mark uses a model that cannot see liquidity state dynamics”
  • Speaker notes: The term “illusion” is deliberate. NAVs appear stable but mask large, regime-dependent valuation gaps.

13.4.11 Slide 3.11 — Level Illusion: Numerical Example

  • Title: “Level illusion: a 25-year infrastructure asset”
  • Setup:
    • GP-reported NAV (DCF, constant 7.5% rate): $100M
    • True economic value:
      • In Regime 3 (normalization): ~$95M (close to GP mark)
      • In Regime 2 (crisis): ~$60M (40% gap)
      • In Regime 4 (multi-shock): ~$80M (20% gap)
  • Bottom-line: “GP mark of $100M is approximately correct ONLY in normalization regimes. Across the full cycle, it’s wrong by an average of ~12%.”
  • Speaker notes: Anchor this with the dollar magnitudes. Make the gap viscerally real.

13.4.12 Slide 3.12 — Implications for Portfolio Mark-to-Market

  • Title: “What this means for your portfolio”
  • Bullets:
    • GP-reported NAVs smooth and lag true economic value
    • During crisis: GP marks overstate value (no markdown despite secondary market signal)
    • During boom: GP marks understate value (no markup despite tight discounts)
    • The smoothing effect is what makes private markets appear uncorrelated with public markets
    • The diversification benefit of private markets is partly an artifact of stale marks
  • Bottom-line: “If you mark to market correctly using secondary market evidence, the diversification benefit is smaller than commonly believed.”
  • Speaker notes: This is the controversial slide. It directly challenges a core marketing pitch of private markets — “uncorrelated returns.” Be prepared for pushback. Lock-step with academic literature (Stafford, Phalippou) that has made this point.

13.6 Discussion Questions

  1. The 2008 crisis revealed the constant-premium assumption was wrong. Why did it take 17+ years (and counting) for the industry to seriously revisit DCF methodology?
  2. If GP-reported NAVs smooth and lag, is private market “diversification” partly an illusion? How big is the illusion?
  3. The denominator effect (LPs selling secondaries because public market declines push private allocations over limits) is mechanical, not a true liquidity signal. How would you adjust the discount-to-NAV interpretation to account for this?

13.7 Worked Numerical Example

Computing the implied liquidity state from a secondary discount

Setup:

  • Secondary discount: 18%
  • Remaining fund life: 6 years
  • Risk-free rate: 4%, ERP × β = 4%, size = 1%
  • GP-assumed illiquidity premium: 3.5%
  • DCF total rate: 12.5%

Step 1: Implied total discount rate from secondary:

For a perpetuity approximation: \(\text{Discount} \approx 1 - e^{-r \cdot T_\text{eff}}\) where \(T_\text{eff}\) is the duration-weighted hold.

With 6 years, the implied rate \(r_\text{implied}\) satisfies \(0.18 = 1 - e^{-r_\text{implied} \cdot 6}\), giving \(r_\text{implied} \approx 3.3\%\) premium above the DCF rate.

Step 2: Implied excess premium:

Excess above GP-assumed: ~3.3 percentage points. So implied total illiquidity premium = 3.5% + 3.3% = 6.8%, roughly double the GP assumption.

Step 3: Liquidity state inversion:

Using \(\pi(L) = \pi_0 - \pi_1 L + \pi_2 L^2\) with calibrated \(\pi_0 = 0.045\), \(\pi_1 = 0.025\), \(\pi_2 = 0.021\), and current premium 6.8%, solve for \(L\):

\(0.068 = 0.045 - 0.025 L + 0.021 L^2\)

Quadratic in \(L\): \(0.021 L^2 - 0.025 L - 0.023 = 0\)

Solutions: \(L = (0.025 ± \sqrt{0.000625 + 0.001932}) / 0.042 = (0.025 ± 0.0506) / 0.042\)

Taking the negative root (since \(L\) should drop below \(\bar{L}\) when premium rises): \(L \approx -0.61\), or 0.61σ below the long-run mean.

Interpretation: Current secondary market is pricing in a moderate-stress liquidity state. Not crisis (which would be −1.5σ or worse), but not normalization either.

13.8 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “Aren’t secondary discounts noisy / not representative?” Volume of $130B+/yr with $1,200+ transactions is more than adequate for statistical inference. The Preqin and Setter aggregations smooth idiosyncratic noise.
  • “Doesn’t the denominator effect contaminate the signal?” Partially yes, but the regime-level signal is robust. Even excluding pure denominator-driven sales, the 2022 widening was clear.
  • “How is this different from the bond yield curve telling us about expected default rates?” Conceptually similar. The secondary market is doing for private equity what corporate bond spreads do for credit — extracting market-implied premia. But private markets had no such signal before secondaries scaled up.
  • “If GP NAVs smooth, doesn’t reported portfolio variance underestimate true variance?” Yes — Stafford (2022) and others document this. Once you correct for smoothing, the Sharpe ratio of PE is much closer to public equity than gross numbers suggest.

13.9 What to Expect Next Session

Session 4 introduces the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process that governs the liquidity state \(L_t\) — but at the intuition level only. We’ll cover:

  • What an OU process is (the simplest mean-reverting random process)
  • Why it’s the right model for liquidity dynamics
  • The parameters κ, \(\bar{L}\), σ and what they mean economically
  • How to simulate paths
  • Connection to Vasicek interest rate models (for students who know them)

Session 4 is a deliberate self-diagnostic for track selection. If the OU process feels intuitive and you want more depth, you may be a Track 2 candidate. If you find it manageable but don’t want the proofs, Track 1 is right for you.

With 6 years, the implied rate $r_\text{implied}$ satisfies $0.18 = 1 - e^{-r_\text{implied} \cdot 6}$, giving $r_\text{implied} \approx 3.3\%$ premium *above* the DCF rate.

**Step 2:** Implied excess premium:

Excess above GP-assumed: ~3.3 percentage points. So implied total illiquidity premium = 3.5% + 3.3% = **6.8%**, roughly double the GP assumption.

**Step 3:** Liquidity state inversion:

Using $\pi(L) = \pi_0 - \pi_1 L + \pi_2 L^2$ with calibrated $\pi_0 = 0.045$, $\pi_1 = 0.025$, $\pi_2 = 0.021$, and current premium 6.8%, solve for $L$:

$0.068 = 0.045 - 0.025 L + 0.021 L^2$

Quadratic in $L$: $0.021 L^2 - 0.025 L - 0.023 = 0$

Solutions: $L = (0.025 ± \sqrt{0.000625 + 0.001932}) / 0.042 = (0.025 ± 0.0506) / 0.042$

Taking the negative root (since $L$ should drop below $\bar{L}$ when premium rises): $L \approx -0.61$, or **0.61σ below the long-run mean**.

**Interpretation:** Current secondary market is pricing in a moderate-stress liquidity state. Not crisis (which would be −1.5σ or worse), but not normalization either.

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"Aren't secondary discounts noisy / not representative?"** Volume of $130B+/yr with $1,200+ transactions is more than adequate for statistical inference. The Preqin and Setter aggregations smooth idiosyncratic noise.
- **"Doesn't the denominator effect contaminate the signal?"** Partially yes, but the regime-level signal is robust. Even excluding pure denominator-driven sales, the 2022 widening was clear.
- **"How is this different from the bond yield curve telling us about expected default rates?"** Conceptually similar. The secondary market is doing for private equity what corporate bond spreads do for credit — extracting market-implied premia. But private markets had no such signal before secondaries scaled up.
- **"If GP NAVs smooth, doesn't reported portfolio variance underestimate true variance?"** Yes — Stafford (2022) and others document this. Once you correct for smoothing, the Sharpe ratio of PE is much closer to public equity than gross numbers suggest.
