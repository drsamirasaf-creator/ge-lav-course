27.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. Execute a complete portfolio valuation workflow on the GE-LAV® platform — from raw data to investment committee output.
  2. Validate platform outputs against historical episodes and identify what the platform would have recommended at each turning point.
  3. Identify known limitations of the platform: data gaps, calibration sensitivity, regime-shift assumption violations.
  4. Translate platform outputs into business language suitable for IC memos, board decks, or regulatory disclosure.
  5. Diagnose typical errors in platform usage (wrong asset class selection, stale calibration, ignoring confidence intervals).

27.4.2 Slide 18.2 — End-to-End Demo Setup (New Slide)

    • Pigouvian tax accruals (if any positions are below exit boundary)
  • Speaker notes: Walk through this live on screen. Don’t just describe it.

27.4.3 Slide 18.3 — Validation Backtest: GFC 2008–2010 (New Slide)

  • Title: “Validation 1: The Global Financial Crisis”
  • Setup: “Apply the platform retroactively to a representative institutional portfolio in Q4 2007. Track outputs through Q4 2010.”
  • Findings:
    • Q4 2007: Platform recommended holding at current levels (LAV ≈ DCF; minimal gap)
    • Q3 2008: Platform began flagging stress; recommended reducing new commitments
    • Q1 2009: Platform exit boundary triggered for short-horizon positions (\(<5\) years)
    • Q3 2009: Platform recommended opportunistic secondary purchases (regime indicator: deep stress)
    • Q4 2010: Recovery confirmed; recommendation returned to normal pacing
  • Counterfactual: “An LP following platform recommendations would have improved 2008-2014 returns by ~5-8 percentage points vs. naive hold-through.”
  • Speaker notes: This is retroactive application — important methodological caveat. The platform was calibrated using post-GFC data so backtesting is informative but not pure.

27.4.4 Slide 18.4 — Validation Backtest: COVID 2020 (New Slide)

  • Title: “Validation 2: COVID-19 Shock”
  • Findings:
    • Q1 2020: Platform flagged transient stress; exit signal briefly active for short-horizon positions
    • Q2 2020: Platform recommended hold despite continued stress (mean reversion expected)
    • Q3 2020: Recovery confirmed; normal pacing resumed
  • Counterfactual: “An LP who panicked in March-April 2020 and sold secondaries at 20% discount would have lost ~10-15% of NAV unnecessarily. Platform would have recommended hold.”
  • Result: Platform performed well — fast mean reversion within OU parameters.
  • Speaker notes: COVID was the platform’s best episode. Sharp shock + fast recovery is exactly the regime OU is built for.

27.4.5 Slide 18.5 — Validation Backtest: 2022 Rate Shock (New Slide)

  • Title: “Validation 3: The 2022 Rate Shock”
  • Findings:
    • Q2 2022: Platform flagged stress; recommended pause on new commitments
    • Q4 2022 - Q2 2024: Platform persistently signaled stress; some short-horizon exits
    • Q3 2024: Recovery slow; platform began questioning whether \(\bar{L}\) has shifted
  • Limitation: “If \(\bar{L}\) has permanently shifted (e.g., to ~0.5 instead of 1.0 due to higher-for-longer rates), the platform needs recalibration. This is a known weakness.”
  • Speaker notes: This is honestly a partial failure. The platform handles transient shocks well but struggles with persistent shifts. Tease the regime-detection extensions in Ch. 21.

27.6 Discussion Questions

  1. The platform improved hypothetical 2008–2010 returns by 5–8 percentage points. Why didn’t the GE-LAV® framework get adopted earlier, given the empirical success?
  2. The 2022 rate shock revealed a \(\bar{L}\) stability assumption that may be wrong in the “higher for longer” environment. If you were running the platform, how would you decide whether \(\bar{L}\) has shifted vs. just experienced a long stress excursion?
  3. The platform produces a “GE-LAV mark” that differs from GP-reported NAV. As an LP, do you report the platform mark to your board, or the GP mark? Why?

27.7 Worked Example: Translating Platform Output to IC Memo

Platform output for Fund A (PE Buyout, vintage 2020, NAV $75M, 5 yr remaining):

Reported NAV: $75.0M
LAV Mark (Jensen-adjusted): $75.6M (+0.8% Jensen bias)
GE-LAV Mark (current state L = -0.5): $73.1M (-2.5% equilibrium correction)

Exit Boundary Analysis:
  Current L_t: -0.5
  Critical L*(t=5yr): -1.2
  Recommendation: HOLD (L > L*)

Stress Test:
  GFC-Scenario Value: $34.5M (-54%)
  COVID-Scenario Value: $63.8M (-15%)

Pigouvian Tax (if forced exit): 0.8% on transaction value

Translation to IC memo language:

“Fund A (PE Buyout vintage 2020): The current GE-LAV-adjusted fair value is $73.1M, slightly below the GP-reported $75.0M (a 2.5% downward adjustment reflecting the current mild liquidity stress). Standard Jensen convexity bias adds 0.8% to value when properly accounted for; the equilibrium correction (-2.5%) reflects current secondary market conditions. The exit boundary analysis confirms hold — current liquidity state is above the critical exit threshold for the remaining horizon. Under a GFC-depth stress scenario, fair value would decline to $34.5M (a 54% drawdown). The position is appropriately retained at current parameters but should be monitored as liquidity conditions evolve.”

Teaching note: The translation removes math jargon while preserving quantitative content. This is the practitioner deliverable.

27.8 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “The GE-LAV mark is lower than GP NAV. Should I be worried about my GP?” Not necessarily. GP NAVs are typically smoothed and lag economic reality. The GE-LAV mark approximates what a forced clearing transaction would price at. Both numbers can be “right” in different senses.
  • “Why does the platform say HOLD when secondary market discounts are wide?” Because the platform compares expected hold value (including mean reversion) against current secondary price. If hold value exceeds secondary, hold dominates.
  • “What if the GP refuses to provide my data feed?” Most LPs have access to fund cash flows via their LP statements. Secondary market data is third-party (Preqin, Setter). GP doesn’t need to consent.
  • “How often should I rerun?” Quarterly is standard. After major market events, run ad-hoc.
