26.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. Identify the major regulatory frameworks affecting private market valuation: Solvency II, AIFMD, IFRS 13, Basel III, GASB 72.
  2. Explain how each framework currently treats private market valuation and where GE-LAV would change the treatment.
  3. Apply the crisis amplification factor (4.31×) and explain what it represents.
  4. Discuss the Financial Stability Board (FSB) concerns about private markets and how GE-LAV addresses them.
  5. Connect correct private market valuation to systemic financial stability — and articulate why this is a regulator-relevant issue, not just an LP-level concern.

26.4 Slide Content Briefs (New Slides Needed)

26.4.1 Slide 16.1 — Title

  • Title: “Regulatory Implications · Crisis Dynamics”
  • Tag line: “When LP-level valuation errors become systemic risk”
  • Speaker notes: Today is the policy session. Connect what we’ve learned to who regulates it and why they should care.

26.4.2 Slide 16.2 — Why Regulation Cares About Valuation

  • Title: “Private market valuation as a systemic issue”
  • Three reasons:
    1. Capital adequacy: Banks, insurers, pension funds hold private assets. Wrong valuations → wrong capital charges → wrong risk capacity
    2. Liquidity stress propagation: If $13T of private assets is mispriced, forced sales during stress amplify and propagate
    3. Run-equivalence: In open-end private market vehicles (newer in Europe), wrong NAVs create run incentives
  • Speaker notes: Frame regulation broadly. This isn’t just about whether one pension fund overstates returns.

26.4.3 Slide 16.3 — Solvency II Current Treatment

  • Title: “Solvency II and private market valuation”
  • Current Solvency II treatment of private assets:
    • Insurers report private market assets at fair value (typically DCF-based NAV)
    • Solvency Capital Requirement (SCR) computed against the NAV
    • “Look-through” allowed for some funds (treats fund as the underlying assets for SCR)
    • Stress factor for type 2 equity (private equity): 49% capital charge
  • Where GE-LAV changes things:
    • LAV-adjusted NAV typically lower than DCF NAV (after Jensen + GE corrections)
    • Lower NAV → lower SCR (insurer holds less capital against)
    • BUT — GE-LAV stress mark is much lower than current stress floor → higher stressed SCR
  • Net effect: GE-LAV adoption would reduce current SCR but raise stress-scenario capital
  • Speaker notes: This is counterintuitive. Many practitioners expect GE-LAV to increase capital requirements always. Actually it’s regime-dependent.

26.4.4 Slide 16.4 — GE-LAV vs. Solvency II Stress Factor (Class 19 reuse)

  • Title: “Comparing stress factors”
  • Table (from Class 19):
Asset Class Solvency II Stress GE-LAV GFC-Depth Stress
Private equity -49% -65% to -70%
Infrastructure -25-39% -55% to -60%
Real estate -25% -45% to -50%
Private credit -38% -40% to -45%
  • Implication: “Solvency II stress factors understate the GFC-depth loss for most private asset classes”
  • Speaker notes: This is the regulator-relevant comparison. Solvency II was calibrated against post-GFC data with smoothed NAVs. GE-LAV using secondary market evidence gives a different (worse) stress picture.

26.4.5 Slide 16.5 — AIFMD: The European Fund Regulation

  • Title: “AIFMD and the open-end question”
  • AIFMD basics:
    • Regulates EU alternative investment fund managers (AIFMs)
    • Requires fair value of assets for NAV reporting
    • Liquidity stress testing required for open-end vehicles
  • The open-end private market problem:
    • LTAFs (Long-Term Asset Funds) in UK
    • ELTIFs (European Long-Term Investment Funds)
    • Allow retail / smaller investor access to private markets
    • Daily / monthly NAV but illiquid underlying
  • Run equivalence:
    • If NAV is stale and one investor exits at NAV, other investors are diluted
    • Creates run incentive: “Sell before others realize NAV is overstated”
    • GE-LAV-correct NAV would close this gap
  • Speaker notes: This is the new regulatory frontier for private markets. ELTIFs are scaling rapidly; the run risk is real.

26.4.6 Slide 16.6 — IFRS 13, GASB 72, Basel III — Survey

  • Title: “Other regulatory regimes”
  • IFRS 13 (international accounting):
    • Defines fair value as exit price (could be GE-LAV equilibrium price)
    • Three-level hierarchy: Level 3 covers most private market assets
    • Currently allows DCF; GE-LAV would qualify as a Level 3 technique
  • GASB 72 (US public sector accounting):
    • Similar to IFRS 13 for governmental pension funds and other public entities
    • Same flexibility re: methodology
  • Basel III (banking):
    • Banks hold private market exposure mostly through fund commitments
    • Risk weights based on look-through or asset class defaults
    • GE-LAV could update look-through risk weights with regime-conditional terms
  • Speaker notes: No regulatory regime currently requires GE-LAV. None prohibits it. The question is whether adoption is voluntary or mandated.

26.4.7 Slide 16.7 — Crisis Amplification: The 4.31× Factor

  • Title: “The crisis amplification factor”
  • Setup:
    • At normal liquidity (\(L = 0\)): equilibrium rate \(r_\text{GE-LAV} \approx 7.5\%\) (close to DCF)
    • At GFC depth (\(L = -1.5\)): equilibrium rate \(r_\text{GE-LAV} \approx 32.3\%\)
    • Amplification factor: 32.3 / 7.5 = 4.31×
  • Why so amplified:
    • McKean-Vlasov collective action: when everyone wants to exit, premium rises sharply
    • Convex effect: the premium function \(\pi(L) = \pi_0 - \pi_1 L + \pi_2 L^2\) has positive convexity in deep stress
    • \(\pi(L = -1.5) = 0.045 + 0.025 \times 1.5 + 0.021 \times 2.25 = 0.045 + 0.0375 + 0.047 = 0.13\) ≈ 13% premium alone
  • Speaker notes: This 4.31× is the systemic concern. Private market valuations don’t just fall in crisis; they fall non-linearly.

26.4.8 Slide 16.8 — Crisis Dynamics: From LP Decision to Systemic Impact

  • Title: “How LP-level decisions become systemic”
  • Mechanism (sequential):
    1. Public markets fall → denominator effect → LPs over-allocated to PE
    2. Some LPs sell secondaries to rebalance
    3. Secondary discounts widen
    4. Wider discounts signal stress to GP-led continuation funds (priced off secondaries)
    5. Stress propagates to all private asset class valuations (multi-asset hedge demand)
    6. Insurance and pension capital tied up in private markets becomes mismeasured
    7. Forced sales accelerate; the system clears at extreme discounts
  • Without GE-LAV: Steps 5–7 invisible to regulators looking at smoothed NAVs
  • With GE-LAV: Steps 5–7 visible quarterly via secondary market signal
  • Speaker notes: This is the systemic risk story. Make it concrete.

26.4.9 Slide 16.9 — Financial Stability Board (FSB) Concerns

  • Title: “What the FSB is watching”
  • FSB priorities re: private markets:
    • Leverage in private markets (NAV loans, fund-level leverage, GP leverage)
    • Valuation transparency (NAVs not stress-tested)
    • Interconnectedness with banking and insurance
    • Retail/semi-retail access (run-equivalence in open-end funds)
  • GE-LAV’s role:
    • Provides a quantitative framework for the second and fourth concerns
    • Could become regulatory standard for stress testing
    • Pigouvian exit tax addresses the first concern indirectly (raises cost of crisis sales)
  • Speaker notes: The FSB has been writing reports on this. GE-LAV is positioned to be cited.

26.4.10 Slide 16.10 — Should GE-LAV Be Mandatory?

  • Title: “The mandate question”
  • Arguments for mandatory adoption:
    • Eliminates the practitioner consensus trap (book Ch. 1.4)
    • Aligns NAVs with economic reality
    • Reduces systemic risk via better stress testing
    • Pigouvian instruments require regulatory backing
  • Arguments against:
    • Premature standardization could lock in flaws
    • Burden on smaller GPs
    • Cross-border coordination problem
    • Existing regulatory structures already costly to change
  • Realistic path:
    • Voluntary adoption by sophisticated LPs first
    • Solvency II disclosure requirement (not capital charge) next
    • Eventual mandate in 2030s, possibly
  • Speaker notes: Honest assessment. Mandates are politically hard. Adoption likely comes from LP demand, not regulator mandate.

26.6 Discussion Questions

  1. The 2024 SEC private fund advisor rule increased disclosure requirements for private market funds. Is this a step toward GE-LAV adoption, or orthogonal to it? What additional disclosure would GE-LAV require?
  2. If GE-LAV’s crisis amplification factor (4.31×) is correct, the 2008 PE losses were partly unmeasurable under DCF accounting. Should historical PE returns be retroactively restated? What are the institutional barriers?
  3. The Pigouvian exit tax (~6-8% at GFC depth) would have generated $5-15B in 2008. Where should that revenue go — back to the affected fund, the SIPC equivalent, the general fund? Who decides?

26.7 Worked Numerical Example: Solvency II Capital Charge

Scenario: A European insurer (Allianz-style) holds €5B in private equity (single-fund concentration). Need to compute SCR under three approaches.

Approach 1: Standard Solvency II - Type 2 equity (private) stress factor: -49% - SCR = €5B × 49% = €2.45B - Capital charge: €2.45B

Approach 2: GE-LAV at normal liquidity - LAV-adjusted NAV: €5B × (1 + 1.7% Jensen) = €5.085B (mild increase) - GE-LAV equilibrium rate at \(L = 0\): ~8.5% (vs. DCF 7.5%, so ~5% adjustment) - Adjusted NAV: ~€4.75B (after equilibrium correction) - SCR using same 49% on adjusted NAV: €2.33B - Capital charge: €2.33B (slight reduction)

Approach 3: GE-LAV stress test (GFC depth) - GE-LAV value at \(L = -1.5\): €5B × (1 - 0.70) = €1.5B - The stress drawdown to €1.5B exceeds Solvency II’s 49% stress floor - Stress-scenario capital required: €5B - €1.5B = €3.5B (significantly more than Solvency II)

Comparison: | Approach | Capital Charge | |—|—| | Solvency II standard | €2.45B | | GE-LAV normal | €2.33B | | GE-LAV stressed | €3.50B |

Interpretation: GE-LAV would reduce normal-state capital requirement by ~€120M, BUT under stress requires €1.05B more capital than Solvency II. Net effect depends on stress probability weighting.

Teaching note: This is the actual policy debate. Lower normal-state burden + higher stress capacity = better aligned with risk. But it’s a redistribution of the capital burden in time, not a reduction.

26.8 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “Won’t GE-LAV always increase capital requirements? That’s not politically viable.” No — it can decrease them in normal regimes. The increase comes only in stressed regimes. Net effect depends on probability-weighted aggregate.
  • “Doesn’t Solvency II already do stress testing?” Yes — Pillar 2 (ORSA). But it uses static stress factors, not regime-conditional ones. GE-LAV would refine this.
  • “How would auditors verify GE-LAV calibration?” Same way they verify any Level 3 valuation — review of methodology, parameters, and back-testing. The GE-LAV platform is designed to be audit-friendly.
  • “Is the Pigouvian tax practical politically?” Hard. But Tobin tax discussions for currencies set the precedent. Could be implemented as a stamp duty rather than a tax.

## Worked Numerical Example: Solvency II Capital Charge

**Scenario:** A European insurer (Allianz-style) holds €5B in private equity (single-fund concentration). Need to compute SCR under three approaches.

**Approach 1: Standard Solvency II**
- Type 2 equity (private) stress factor: -49%
- SCR = €5B × 49% = €2.45B
- **Capital charge: €2.45B**

**Approach 2: GE-LAV at normal liquidity**
- LAV-adjusted NAV: €5B × (1 + 1.7% Jensen) = €5.085B (mild increase)
- GE-LAV equilibrium rate at $L = 0$: ~8.5% (vs. DCF 7.5%, so ~5% adjustment)
- Adjusted NAV: ~€4.75B (after equilibrium correction)
- SCR using same 49% on adjusted NAV: €2.33B
- **Capital charge: €2.33B** (slight reduction)

**Approach 3: GE-LAV stress test (GFC depth)**
- GE-LAV value at $L = -1.5$: €5B × (1 - 0.70) = €1.5B
- The stress drawdown to €1.5B exceeds Solvency II's 49% stress floor
- **Stress-scenario capital required: €5B - €1.5B = €3.5B** (significantly more than Solvency II)

**Comparison:**
| Approach | Capital Charge |
|---|---|
| Solvency II standard | €2.45B |
| GE-LAV normal | €2.33B |
| GE-LAV stressed | €3.50B |

**Interpretation:** GE-LAV would reduce normal-state capital requirement by ~€120M, BUT under stress requires €1.05B more capital than Solvency II. Net effect depends on stress probability weighting.

**Teaching note:** This is the *actual* policy debate. Lower normal-state burden + higher stress capacity = better aligned with risk. But it's a redistribution of the capital burden in time, not a reduction.

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"Won't GE-LAV always increase capital requirements? That's not politically viable."** No — it can decrease them in normal regimes. The increase comes only in stressed regimes. Net effect depends on probability-weighted aggregate.
- **"Doesn't Solvency II already do stress testing?"** Yes — Pillar 2 (ORSA). But it uses static stress factors, not regime-conditional ones. GE-LAV would refine this.
- **"How would auditors verify GE-LAV calibration?"** Same way they verify any Level 3 valuation — review of methodology, parameters, and back-testing. The GE-LAV platform is designed to be audit-friendly.
- **"Is the Pigouvian tax practical politically?"** Hard. But Tobin tax discussions for currencies set the precedent. Could be implemented as a stamp duty rather than a tax.
