18.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. State the five requirements a correct private market valuation theory must satisfy.
  2. Evaluate alternative frameworks (DCF, DLOM, factor models, GE-LAV) against these five requirements.
  3. Articulate why no framework prior to GE-LAV satisfies all five simultaneously.
  4. Connect Unit 1 (why DCF fails) and Unit 2 (measurement problems) to Unit 3 (the GE-LAV alternative).
  5. Identify the five main results of GE-LAV by name and recognize them when encountered.

18.4 Slide Content Briefs (New Slides Needed)

18.4.1 Slide 8.1 — Title

  • Title: “What a Correct Theory Must Deliver”
  • Tag line: “Five requirements · The standard GE-LAV satisfies and no other framework meets”
  • Speaker notes: Session 8 is the conceptual synthesis. Students should leave knowing the criteria against which any valuation theory should be judged.

18.4.2 Slide 8.2 — Why a Theory, Not Just Better Numbers

  • Title: “Why we need a theory”
  • The problem with ad hoc fixes:
    • DLOM with regime-dependent percentages: which regime are we in?
    • “Improved” IRR with reinvestment assumptions: which rate?
    • Factor models: which factors?
  • What a theory provides:
    • Falsifiable predictions that data can confirm or reject
    • Internal consistency — pieces fit together
    • Generalization to new asset classes without recalibration of every parameter
    • Welfare implications — can we say what the socially optimal outcome is?
  • Speaker notes: Be philosophical here. Students from MBA backgrounds often resist theory in favor of “just give me the number.” Make the case for theoretical rigor.

18.4.3 Slide 8.3 — Requirement 1: Stochastic Premia

  • Title: “Requirement 1: The illiquidity premium must be stochastic”
  • Why: “Secondary market evidence shows the premium varies 10× the magnitude of its assumed constant” (Session 3 result)
  • What this rules out:
    • Standard DCF (constant premium)
    • DLOM (constant percentage)
    • Standard CAPM (no liquidity term)
  • What this requires:
    • A stochastic process model: OU is the minimal choice
    • Calibration to time-series data
  • Speaker notes: This is the empirical requirement. The data demands it.

18.4.4 Slide 8.4 — Requirement 2: Investor Heterogeneity

  • Title: “Requirement 2: The theory must accommodate investor heterogeneity”
  • Why: “Different LPs have different liquidity preferences (insurers with long liabilities, endowments with intergenerational concerns, sovereign wealth funds with no horizon)”
  • What this rules out:
    • Representative agent models (assumes one LP type)
    • Most factor models (single discount factor)
  • What this requires:
    • Heterogeneous beta in the GE-LAV setting
    • Distributional dynamics of investor liquidity states
  • Speaker notes: Heterogeneity is what makes the McKean-Vlasov machinery necessary. One representative LP wouldn’t need it.

18.4.5 Slide 8.5 — Requirement 3: Endogenous Market Clearing

  • Title: “Requirement 3: Premium must be determined in equilibrium, not assumed”
  • Why: “The illiquidity premium is what the marginal investor requires to clear the market. Taking it as a fixed parameter ignores the price-setting mechanism.”
  • What this rules out:
    • DCF with fixed premium (no market clearing)
    • Partial equilibrium asset pricing
  • What this requires:
    • Fixed-point structure: premium depends on behavior, behavior depends on premium
    • General equilibrium: simultaneous solution for premium and behavior
    • McKean-Vlasov mechanism: mathematical formalization
  • Speaker notes: This is the structural requirement. GE-LAV’s main theoretical contribution.

18.4.6 Slide 8.6 — Requirement 4: Empirical Implementability

  • Title: “Requirement 4: The theory must be implementable on real data”
  • Why: “A theory that only works on synthetic data is not a theory of finance”
  • What this requires:
    • Calibration procedures with confidence intervals
    • Robustness checks
    • Software / platform for practitioners
    • Reproducibility
  • GE-LAV implementation:
    • OU calibration via MLE on Preqin / Setter data
    • Closed-form Jensen bias formula
    • Numerical HJB solution for exit boundary
    • GE-LAV® platform at liquidityillusion.com
  • Speaker notes: Distinguish GE-LAV from purely academic theories that lack empirical implementation. The platform is a deliberate part of the framework.

18.4.7 Slide 8.7 — Requirement 5: Welfare and Regulatory Support

  • Title: “Requirement 5: The theory must support welfare and regulatory analysis”
  • Why: “Private markets are systemically important ($13T AUM); valuation methodology has financial-stability implications”
  • What this requires:
    • Ability to compute constrained social optimum (welfare-maximizing allocation given liquidity friction)
    • Identification of externalities
    • Corrective instruments (taxes, subsidies, capital charges)
  • GE-LAV outputs:
    • Welfare gap: Quantified cost of the McKean-Vlasov externality (~2.3%/year)
    • Pigouvian exit tax: Optimal regulatory correction
    • Capital adequacy adjustments: Solvency II / Basel III implications
  • Speaker notes: This is what elevates GE-LAV from “better valuation method” to “framework with policy implications.” Critical for regulators.

18.4.8 Slide 8.8 — Summary: The Five Requirements

  • Title: “Five requirements, summarized”
  • Numbered list:
    1. Stochastic premia (data demands it)
    2. Investor heterogeneity (institutional reality)
    3. Endogenous market clearing (theoretical necessity)
    4. Empirical implementability (engineering requirement)
    5. Welfare and regulatory support (social responsibility)
  • Speaker notes: This slide is the poster for the rest of the course. Have students photograph it.

  • Table:

18.4.10 Slide 8.10 — The GE-LAV Model at a Glance

  • Title: “GE-LAV in one slide”
  • Layout: Six components, one box each
  • Components:
    • Liquidity process: \(dL_t = \kappa(\bar{L} - L_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t\)
    • Premium function: \(\pi(L) = \pi_0 - \pi_1 L + \pi_2 L^2\)
    • Effective discount rate: \(r(L) = r_f + \beta(\text{ERP}) + \pi(L)\)
    • Valuation operator (LAV): \(V_0 = E^Q[\int_0^T C_t e^{-\int_0^t r(L_s) ds} dt]\)
    • General equilibrium correction: \(r_{GE-LAV}(L) = r(L) + \text{collective externality term}\)
    • Welfare instrument: Pigouvian exit tax \(\tau(L)\)
  • Speaker notes: This is the architecture. Each component gets its own treatment in Unit 3 and onward. Track 2 sees the derivation of each in Sessions 25–29.

18.4.11 Slide 8.11 — The Five Main Results of GE-LAV

  • Title: “The five results (Ch. 1 preview, restated)”
  • Numbered:
    1. Jensen Convexity Bias: Closed-form, calibrated 0.8–5.4% across asset classes (Ch. 18)
    2. Optimal Exit Boundary \(L^*(t)\): Smooth-pasting solution to HJB (Ch. 6, 11)
    3. Welfare Gap: Quantified externality cost ~2.3%/year (Ch. 19)
    4. Pigouvian Exit Tax: Welfare-maximizing corrective \(\tau(L)\) (Ch. 19)
    5. Strict Valuation Hierarchy: DCF ⊊ LAV ⊊ GE-LAV proven (Ch. 20)
  • Speaker notes: Each of these has been mentioned before. Now they’re named. Students should know what each is by Session 24.

18.5 Discussion Questions

  1. Of the five requirements, which do you think is most controversial within the practitioner community? Which is most controversial within the academic community? Why might they differ?
  2. The Pastor-Stambaugh liquidity factor satisfies “stochastic premia” but fails “endogenous market clearing.” Does that matter for using it as a factor in a portfolio? When?
  3. If GE-LAV becomes the regulatory standard for private market valuation, what new operational burdens does it impose on a typical PE fund GP? How would you mitigate those?

18.6 Worked Conceptual Example: Evaluating a New Framework

Setup: Suppose a new valuation method is proposed called “Adaptive DCF” — it uses a different discount rate depending on which of four manually-defined regimes the analyst declares the market to be in.

Evaluation against the five requirements:

  1. Stochastic? Partial. Discount rate varies across regimes, but not within. No process model.
  2. Heterogeneous? No. Same regime classification for all investors.
  3. Endogenous? No. Regime is exogenously declared, not equilibrium-determined.
  4. Implementable? Yes. Easy to implement; analyst judgment-based.
  5. Welfare? No. No mechanism for welfare analysis or regulatory correction.

Conclusion: Adaptive DCF is an improvement over constant-premium DCF but doesn’t satisfy requirements 2, 3, or 5. It would be acceptable as a transition methodology but not as a long-term framework.

Teaching note: Use this exercise to show students how to evaluate frameworks they encounter in their careers. The five-requirement template is the rubric.

18.7 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “Aren’t these requirements just GE-LAV-specific?” No — they’re general criteria any theory should meet. GE-LAV happens to satisfy them; that’s the test, not the definition.
  • “What if a new framework satisfied 4 of 5 but missed welfare? Could that be acceptable?” Depends on use case. For internal LP valuation: probably fine. For regulatory adoption: no, welfare matters.
  • “Why is welfare requirement 5 and not 1?” Logical ordering — without stochastic premia (1), you can’t even ask welfare questions. The five build on each other.
  • “Is there a sixth requirement we’re missing?” Plausibly: jump dynamics / tail risk. GE-LAV addresses these in Ch. 13 (Lévy extension) but not as a base requirement. Reasonable critique.

5. **Welfare?** *No.* No mechanism for welfare analysis or regulatory correction.

**Conclusion:** Adaptive DCF is an improvement over constant-premium DCF but doesn't satisfy requirements 2, 3, or 5. It would be acceptable as a *transition methodology* but not as a long-term framework.

**Teaching note:** Use this exercise to show students *how* to evaluate frameworks they encounter in their careers. The five-requirement template is the rubric.

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"Aren't these requirements just GE-LAV-specific?"** No — they're general criteria any theory should meet. GE-LAV happens to satisfy them; that's the test, not the definition.
- **"What if a new framework satisfied 4 of 5 but missed welfare? Could that be acceptable?"** Depends on use case. For internal LP valuation: probably fine. For regulatory adoption: no, welfare matters.
- **"Why is welfare requirement 5 and not 1?"** Logical ordering — without stochastic premia (1), you can't even ask welfare questions. The five build on each other.
- **"Is there a sixth requirement we're missing?"** Plausibly: jump dynamics / tail risk. GE-LAV addresses these in Ch. 13 (Lévy extension) but not as a base requirement. Reasonable critique.
