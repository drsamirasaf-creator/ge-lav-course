14.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. Describe the empirical term structure of private capital returns by asset class (PE buyout, growth equity, infrastructure, real estate, private credit).
  2. Explain why long-horizon assets are disproportionately exposed to the liquidity illusion.
  3. Compute the Jensen bias \(B(T)\) for a given asset class and horizon using the affine formula.
  4. Compare the GE-LAV liquidity-adjusted term structure to the standard DLOM (Discount for Lack of Marketability) methodology.
  5. State and justify their Track selection by the end of class.

14.2 Pre-Class Assignment

  • Read: Book Chapter 3, all sections (~25 pages)
  • Optional: Section 1 of Phalippou & Gottschalg (2009), “The Performance of Private Equity Funds,” Review of Financial Studies

14.4 Slide Content Briefs (New Slides Needed)

14.4.1 Slide 5.1 — Title

  • Title: “The Term Structure of Private Capital Returns”
  • Tag line: “Why long-horizon assets are most affected by the liquidity illusion”
  • Reminder banner: “Track declaration deadline today”

14.4.2 Slide 5.2 — What Is a Term Structure?

  • Title: “Term structures everyone knows”
  • Examples:
    • Bond yield curve: yield as a function of maturity (1Y, 2Y, 5Y, 10Y, 30Y)
    • Volatility term structure: implied vol as a function of option maturity
    • Credit spread term structure: spread by tenor
  • New idea: “What does the risk of private capital look like as a function of holding period?”
  • Speaker notes: Connect to fixed income students already know. Then pivot to the novel object.

14.4.3 Slide 5.3 — Empirical Term Structure: PE Returns by Vintage

  • Title: “PE returns by vintage and time-to-liquidation”
  • Visual: Heatmap or 3D surface of pooled IRR by vintage year × years to liquidation
  • Key observations:
    • 2006–2008 vintages show large vintage-year effects (returns depend on entry conditions)
    • Mid-vintage years (2010–2015) cluster near long-run averages
    • 2018–2020 vintages still uncertain (recent)
  • Speaker notes: This is the empirical term structure. It’s noisy. The theoretical question is: what does GE-LAV predict?

14.4.4 Slide 5.4 — The Liquidity Risk Term Structure

  • Title: “Risk grows with horizon — but not the way DCF says”
  • DCF prediction: Risk scales as \(\sigma \sqrt{T}\) (standard random walk scaling)
  • GE-LAV prediction (OU + Jensen): Risk has two components:
    • Diffusion component: scales as \(\sqrt{T}\) for short \(T\), plateaus for long \(T\) (mean reversion)
    • Jensen bias component: grows linearly with \(T\)
  • Visual: Two curves overlaid:
    • DCF risk: straight \(\sqrt{T}\) line
    • GE-LAV risk: rises, plateaus, then rises again (Jensen kicking in)
  • Speaker notes: This is the key conceptual contribution of Chapter 3. The shape is qualitatively different from DCF.

14.4.5 Slide 5.5 — The Jensen Bias Formula

  • Title: “The Jensen bias as a function of horizon”
  • Formula (boxed): \(B(T) = A \cdot T + C\) (affine in \(T\), to leading order)
  • Where:
    • \(A\) = annual Jensen bias accrual rate (calibrated 0.1–0.4% per year by asset class)
    • \(C\) = small constant offset
  • Calibrated values by asset class:
Asset Class A (per year) C
PE Buyout 0.16% 0.0%
Growth Equity 0.17% 0.1%
Real Estate 0.18% 0.0%
Infrastructure 0.18% 0.0%
Private Credit 0.10% 0.0%
  • Speaker notes: This is a closed-form result. Track 2 students will derive it in Session 29.

14.4.6 Slide 5.6 — Worked Example: Jensen Bias by Horizon

  • Title: “Computing \(B(T)\) for typical horizons”
  • Table:
Asset Hold T A \(B(T)\)
5-yr Buyout 5 0.16% 0.8%
10-yr Growth 10 0.17% 1.7%
15-yr Secondary 15 0.17% 2.5%
20-yr Infrastructure 20 0.18% 3.6%
25-yr Core RE 25 0.18% 4.5%
30-yr PPP Asset 30 0.18% 5.4%
  • Bottom-line: “30-year PPP assets carry Jensen bias 7× larger than 5-year buyouts.”
  • Speaker notes: This is just the Jensen bias channel. The GE-LAV equilibrium correction can be much larger.

14.4.7 Slide 5.7 — Why Long-Horizon Assets Are Most Affected

  • Title: “Why infrastructure is more affected than buyout”
  • Three reasons:
    1. Jensen bias scales with \(T\) — longer hold means more cumulative convexity effect
    2. More time for liquidity state to drift — variance grows with \(T\) (up to stationary limit)
    3. Discount factor is more sensitive to rate changes at long \(T\) — modified duration effect
  • Visual: Three small charts showing each effect
  • Speaker notes: This is the implication for infrastructure and long-hold strategies. Pension funds and insurers — the biggest holders of long-duration private assets — face the largest valuation errors.

14.4.8 Slide 5.8 — The Lévy Extension

  • Title: “GE-LAV term structure with jumps”
  • Setup: “Pure OU is Gaussian. Real data has jumps (March 2020, GFC). The Lévy extension adds compound Poisson jumps to OU.”
  • Effect on term structure:
    • Short \(T\): jumps add fat tails to short-horizon variance
    • Long \(T\): jump effects average out, term structure resembles pure-OU result
  • Calibrated jump intensity: ~0.3 jumps per year, mean jump size −0.4σ
  • Speaker notes: Brief mention. Full Lévy treatment is in Track 2 advanced topics.

14.4.9 Slide 5.9 — DLOM: The Industry Alternative

  • Title: “DLOM: Discount for Lack of Marketability”
  • What it is:
    • Industry-standard approach to adjusting valuations for illiquidity
    • Typical magnitudes: 15–40% reduction in computed DCF value
    • Methods: Restricted stock studies, pre-IPO studies, option-based models (Finnerty, Longstaff)
  • How it works: Apply a flat percentage discount to the DCF mark
  • Common assumption: “DLOM is constant for an asset class” (e.g., 25% for PE, 30% for VC)
  • Speaker notes: Most students will have encountered DLOM in valuation classes or CFA. Establish it as the current best industry alternative, then critique.

14.4.10 Slide 5.10 — DLOM by Asset Class (Industry Convention)

  • Title: “Typical DLOM ranges”
  • Table:
Asset Class Typical DLOM
Late-stage private equity 15–25%
Early-stage venture 30–45%
Real estate private equity 20–30%
Infrastructure 10–20%
Private credit 5–15%
  • Speaker notes: Source: AICPA Valuation Guidelines, various restricted stock studies. Vary across appraisers and contexts.

14.4.11 Slide 5.11 — DLOM vs. GE-LAV: Comparison

  • Title: “Same problem, two answers”
  • Side-by-side table:
Feature DLOM GE-LAV
Treatment of premium Flat percentage discount Stochastic OU dynamics
Regime dependence None Endogenous to liquidity state
Horizon dependence Flat Affine in T
Theoretical foundation Empirical (restricted stock studies) First-principles asset pricing
Crisis behavior Static Amplifies via McKean-Vlasov
Calibration Subjective / appraiser judgment OLS / MLE on secondary data
  • Bottom-line: “DLOM was the best available method when secondary market data didn’t exist. GE-LAV is the first method that can use the secondary market data correctly.”
  • Speaker notes: Be respectful of DLOM. It was a reasonable approach. Now we have better tools.

14.4.12 Slide 5.12 — DLOM vs. GE-LAV: Numerical Comparison

  • Title: “A 20-year infrastructure asset, three methods”
  • Three columns:
    • DCF: $100M (constant 7.5%, premium 3.5%)
    • DCF + DLOM (15%): $85M (just shave 15% off)
    • GE-LAV (normal markets): $95M (Jensen-corrected + small GE adjustment)
    • GE-LAV (Regime 4 multi-shock): $80M
    • GE-LAV (Regime 2 GFC depth): $30M
  • Punchline: “DLOM is a constant adjustment. GE-LAV is a regime-dependent calculation. In normal markets, they’re close. In crises, they’re completely different.”

14.4.13 Slide 5.13 — Performance Measurement Implications (Tease)

  • Title: “What does this mean for performance measurement?”
  • Setup: “If valuations are wrong systematically, returns are wrong systematically”
  • Implications:
    • IRR is computed against wrong valuations
    • PME comparisons are biased
    • Vintage-year analysis is contaminated
  • Speaker notes: This is the bridge to Session 6 (IRR) and Session 7 (PME).

14.5 Discussion Questions

  1. The Jensen bias for a 30-year PPP asset is ~5.4%. The DLOM convention for infrastructure is 10–20%. Are these incompatible? How should they be reconciled?
  2. Pension funds and insurers hold most of the long-duration private assets where Jensen bias is largest. If GE-LAV becomes adopted methodology, what happens to reported PE returns at large pension funds?
  3. Some private credit assets have short effective duration (1–3 years for direct lending). Jensen bias is small there. Does that mean private credit is “GE-LAV-immune”? What other GE-LAV channels might still matter?

14.6 Worked Numerical Example: Choosing Between DLOM and GE-LAV

Setup: Insurance company holds a 15-year core infrastructure asset (regulated water utility). DCF mark = $500M.

Method 1: DLOM at 15% - Adjusted value: $500M × 0.85 = $425M - Applied uniformly across all market conditions - “Always 15% off DCF”

Method 2: GE-LAV (current state, normalization regime) - Jensen bias for T = 15: \(A \cdot 15 + C = 0.18\% \cdot 15 = 2.7\%\) - LAV value (just Jensen): $500M × (1 + 0.027) = \(513.5M\) - GE-LAV (normal regime, small equilibrium correction): ~$495M - “About flat with DCF in normal markets”

Method 3: GE-LAV (current state, stressed regime, L = −0.8) - Equilibrium rate: \(r_\text{GE-LAV}(L = -0.8) \approx 12\%\) (vs. DCF 7.5%) - GE-LAV value: $500M × e^{-(12% - 7.5%) } $250M - “50% off DCF in stress”

Method 4: GE-LAV (GFC depth, L = −1.5) - Equilibrium rate: ~32% - GE-LAV value: ~$150M - “70% off DCF at GFC depth”

Comparison: DLOM gives one answer ($425M). GE-LAV gives a menu of answers conditional on regime. The insurance regulator should care about the GFC-depth number ($150M) for solvency purposes.

14.7 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “If Jensen bias is small (1–4%), why is it a big deal?” It compounds across the $13T market = $130-520B in systematic underreporting. Also it’s the only always-positive correction — the GE-LAV equilibrium correction can be positive or negative depending on regime.
  • “DLOM at 15-25% is much bigger than Jensen bias at 2-4%. Doesn’t DLOM dominate?” DLOM and Jensen are not the same thing. DLOM is trying to capture the entire illiquidity adjustment in one number. Jensen is just one channel. GE-LAV’s full correction (Jensen + GE equilibrium) can match or exceed DLOM in stressed regimes.
  • “How do we know the calibration is right?” Maximum likelihood on 22 years of secondary market data. Reasonably robust. Confidence intervals are tight enough that the qualitative conclusions don’t change under reasonable parameter variation.
  • “What about private credit? Spread compensation already exists.” Private credit has its own GE-LAV variant (Ch 23). Spread is partly compensation for credit risk, partly for illiquidity. Decomposing these requires GE-LAV.

14.8 What to Expect Next Session

Session 6 begins Unit 2 (Measurement and Theory) with a deep look at IRR. Why IRR is the dominant performance metric in private markets, what its two structural biases are, and why a constant-rate metric cannot correctly measure performance when rates are stochastic.

Reading: Book Chapter 4, sections 4.2–4.4 (~10 pages).

For Track 2 students who declared today: Begin reading Karatzas-Shreve Ch. 5 (Brownian motion and stochastic calculus). You’ll need this background by Session 25.

## Worked Numerical Example: Choosing Between DLOM and GE-LAV

**Setup:** Insurance company holds a 15-year core infrastructure asset (regulated water utility). DCF mark = $500M.

**Method 1: DLOM at 15%**
- Adjusted value: $500M × 0.85 = $425M
- Applied uniformly across all market conditions
- "Always 15% off DCF"

**Method 2: GE-LAV (current state, normalization regime)**
- Jensen bias for T = 15: $A \cdot 15 + C = 0.18\% \cdot 15 = 2.7\%$
- LAV value (just Jensen): $500M × (1 + 0.027) = $513.5M$
- GE-LAV (normal regime, small equilibrium correction): ~$495M
- "About flat with DCF in normal markets"

**Method 3: GE-LAV (current state, stressed regime, L = −0.8)**
- Equilibrium rate: $r_\text{GE-LAV}(L = -0.8) \approx 12\%$ (vs. DCF 7.5%)
- GE-LAV value: $500M × e^{-(12\% - 7.5\%) \cdot 15} \approx $250M
- "50% off DCF in stress"

**Method 4: GE-LAV (GFC depth, L = −1.5)**
- Equilibrium rate: ~32%
- GE-LAV value: ~$150M
- "70% off DCF at GFC depth"

**Comparison:** DLOM gives one answer ($425M). GE-LAV gives a *menu* of answers conditional on regime. The insurance regulator should care about the GFC-depth number ($150M) for solvency purposes.

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"If Jensen bias is small (1–4%), why is it a big deal?"** It compounds across the $13T market = $130-520B in systematic underreporting. Also it's the *only* always-positive correction — the GE-LAV equilibrium correction can be positive or negative depending on regime.
- **"DLOM at 15-25% is much bigger than Jensen bias at 2-4%. Doesn't DLOM dominate?"** DLOM and Jensen are not the same thing. DLOM is trying to capture the entire illiquidity adjustment in one number. Jensen is just one channel. GE-LAV's full correction (Jensen + GE equilibrium) can match or exceed DLOM in stressed regimes.
- **"How do we know the calibration is right?"** Maximum likelihood on 22 years of secondary market data. Reasonably robust. Confidence intervals are tight enough that the *qualitative* conclusions don't change under reasonable parameter variation.
- **"What about private credit? Spread compensation already exists."** Private credit has its own GE-LAV variant (Ch 23). Spread is partly compensation for credit risk, partly for illiquidity. Decomposing these requires GE-LAV.
