31.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. State the key challenge of mean-field games: each agent optimizes, but the optimum depends on what other agents do.
  2. Define a McKean-Vlasov SDE in plain language and recognize the McKean-Vlasov coupling term.
  3. Articulate the fixed-point structure of MFG equilibrium: distribution → individual optimum → aggregate distribution.
  4. Explain the collective externality in private markets — why individual LP exit decisions create costs for all LPs.
  5. Connect MFG to game-theoretic frameworks students may know (Nash equilibrium, congestion games, mechanism design).

31.4 Slide Content Briefs (All New Slides)

31.4.1 Slide 21.1 — Title

  • Title: “Mean-Field Games”
  • Subtitle: “When everyone solves HJB simultaneously · Concepts only · Track 2 derivation in Session 26”
  • Speaker notes: This is the conceptually hardest session in Unit 4. Pace carefully.

31.4.2 Slide 21.2 — The Problem MFG Solves

  • Title: “Two LPs in the same market”
  • Setup:
    • LP A is solving its own HJB to decide when to exit
    • LP B is also solving its own HJB at the same time
    • But: the secondary market price depends on how many LPs are trying to exit
    • So LP A’s optimal decision depends on what LP B (and 1,000 other LPs) is doing
  • The challenge: “Standard HJB takes the environment as given. But the environment is the other agents’ decisions.”
  • Speaker notes: Set up the puzzle. Students should feel the conceptual challenge.

31.4.3 Slide 21.3 — Why Nash Equilibrium Isn’t Quite Right

  • Title: “Why standard Nash doesn’t work”
  • Nash equilibrium: Each player chooses best response given others’ strategies, and the strategies are mutually consistent
  • Problem in finance: “There are thousands of LPs. Standard Nash requires tracking each LP’s individual strategy. Computationally infeasible.”
  • The mean-field idea: “When there are many agents, replace ‘what each individual does’ with ‘what the distribution of agents does.’”
  • Result: “Each agent reacts to the aggregate distribution; the aggregate is determined by individual choices.”
  • Speaker notes: Connect to standard game theory most students know.

31.4.4 Slide 21.4 — The McKean-Vlasov SDE

  • Title: “McKean-Vlasov SDEs: SDEs with population coupling”
  • Standard SDE: \(dL_t = \mu(L_t, t) dt + \sigma(L_t, t) dW_t\)
  • McKean-Vlasov SDE: \(dL_t = \mu(L_t, t, \mu_t) dt + \sigma(L_t, t, \mu_t) dW_t\)
  • The new ingredient: \(\mu_t\) = probability distribution of the population’s state at time \(t\)
  • Key property: “Drift and/or diffusion depend on the distribution of the population, not just the individual state”
  • In GE-LAV:
    • \(L_t\) = liquidity state of a representative LP
    • \(\mu_t\) = distribution of liquidity states across all LPs
    • The equilibrium secondary market price depends on \(\mu_t\)
  • Speaker notes: Don’t expect students to internalize the McKean-Vlasov form. Just establish that there’s a distribution-coupled version of the SDE.

31.4.5 Slide 21.5 — Why “McKean-Vlasov”

  • Title: “Where the name comes from”
  • Background:
    • Anatoly Vlasov (1938): Plasma physics — describing many-particle systems
    • Henry McKean (1966): Mathematical formalization for stochastic systems
    • Mean-field game theory (2006+): Lasry & Lions, Carmona & Delarue — modern formulation
  • Connection: “GE-LAV is not the first application of MFG to finance. Recent applications include systemic risk, optimal execution, herd behavior in trading.”
  • Speaker notes: Establish intellectual lineage. The framework isn’t ad hoc.

31.4.6 Slide 21.6 — The Fixed-Point Structure

  • Title: “MFG equilibrium is a fixed point”
  • The logic (sequential):
    1. Suppose population distribution \(\mu_t\) is given
    2. Each LP solves its individual HJB given this \(\mu_t\) → optimal policy \(u^*(L, t, \mu_t)\)
    3. Apply the optimal policy across the population → updated distribution \(\tilde{\mu}_t\)
    4. If \(\tilde{\mu}_t = \mu_t\) → MFG equilibrium
  • Visual: Loop diagram — distribution → optimization → distribution
  • Existence question: Does this fixed point always exist? Sometimes. Under what conditions? See Theorem 13.2 in book (Schauder fixed-point theorem)
  • Uniqueness question: Can there be multiple equilibria? Yes, in general. GE-LAV proves uniqueness under a stability condition (mean reversion \(\kappa\) exceeds coupling \(\gamma\)).
  • Speaker notes: This is the abstract core of MFG. Make sure students see the fixed-point structure visually.

31.4.7 Slide 21.7 — The Stability Condition

  • Title: “When MFG equilibrium is well-behaved”
  • Stability condition (GE-LAV calibration):
    • Mean reversion speed \(\kappa = 0.45\)/yr
    • Mean-field coupling strength \(\gamma\) (small, calibrated)
    • Condition: \(\kappa > \gamma\) → equilibrium exists and is stable
  • What violates stability:
    • If coupling is too strong, multiple equilibria possible (markets can “panic”)
    • In extreme stress, the framework’s basic stability assumption may break — Ch 19 covers this
  • GE-LAV is calibrated to be in the stable regime: “Empirically, \(\kappa = 0.45\) exceeds reasonable \(\gamma\) estimates by 3-5×”
  • Speaker notes: This is a known limitation. In a true panic (worse than GFC), the framework may break. Worth flagging.

31.4.8 Slide 21.8 — The Collective Externality

  • Title: “Why MFG matters: the collective externality”
  • The setup:
    • LP A decides to exit secondary
    • LP A’s exit slightly increases supply on secondary market
    • This slightly widens discount for all other LPs trying to exit
    • LP A doesn’t internalize this small effect on others
  • Aggregate:
    • When many LPs exit, the cumulative effect on prices is large
    • Each LP only sees their own benefit; nobody internalizes the collective cost
  • This is the McKean-Vlasov externality — the central pricing distortion in GE-LAV
  • Speaker notes: This is the policy story. Pigouvian tax (Session 24) corrects this externality.

31.4.9 Slide 21.9 — Quantifying the Externality

  • Title: “How big is the externality?”
  • The welfare gap:
    • Difference between aggregate welfare under decentralized exit decisions vs. socially-optimal exit decisions
    • Calibrated magnitude: ΔW ≈ 2.3% per year
    • In dollars: Roughly $300B/year in aggregate welfare loss across the $13T private capital market
  • Where the loss is concentrated:
    • Stress regimes (the externality is small in calm markets)
    • Long-duration assets (more time for the externality to accumulate)
    • Smaller LPs (most affected by widened discounts)
  • Speaker notes: Make the dollar magnitude visceral. $300B/year is a real number.

31.4.10 Slide 21.10 — Solving the MFG (Numerically)

  • Title: “How to solve a McKean-Vlasov MFG”
  • Iterative method:
    1. Start with a guess for the population distribution \(\mu_t^{(0)}\)
    2. Solve the individual HJB given \(\mu_t^{(0)}\) → optimal policy
    3. Simulate the population under that policy → updated \(\mu_t^{(1)}\)
    4. Check convergence: if \(|\mu_t^{(0)} - \mu_t^{(1)}|\) is small, equilibrium found; else iterate
  • For GE-LAV:
    • Typically converges in 10–20 iterations
    • Computational cost: minutes on a laptop
    • The GE-LAV platform does this automatically
  • Speaker notes: Don’t expect students to implement. Just establish that solving MFG is computationally tractable.

31.4.11 Slide 21.11 — Propagation of Chaos

  • Title: “Why mean-field approximation is good”
  • Setup:
    • We pretend each LP only cares about the distribution \(\mu_t\), not about individual other LPs
    • Is this approximation valid when there are 100 LPs? 1,000? 10,000?
  • Propagation of chaos (informal): “As the number of LPs \(N \to \infty\), individual LP outcomes become statistically independent of each other, conditional on the population distribution”
  • For finite \(N\):
    • Error scales as \(1/\sqrt{N}\)
    • At \(N = 100\) LPs: error ~10%
    • At \(N = 1000\) LPs: error ~3%
    • At \(N = 10000\) LPs: error <1%
  • In private markets: “There are ~10,000 LPs in major institutional private markets. Mean-field is a very good approximation.”
  • Speaker notes: Justify the approximation empirically. The framework’s assumptions aren’t wild.

31.4.12 Slide 21.12 — MFG in Other Finance Contexts

  • Title: “Where else MFG shows up”
  • Recent finance applications:
    • Optimal execution: When many traders work the same order, MFG-aware execution is better
    • Systemic risk: Bank networks with stress propagation
    • Robo-advisors: Aggregated retail behavior creating market patterns
    • Decentralized finance (DeFi): Liquidity provider rebalancing in AMMs
  • GE-LAV is the first application to private market valuation. This is the framework’s novel contribution.
  • Speaker notes: Validation by analogy. MFG is established in finance; private markets is a fresh application.

31.4.13 Slide 21.13 — What Track 1 Needs to Know

  • Title: “Track 1: takeaways for application”
  • You don’t need to:
    • Solve McKean-Vlasov SDEs
    • Derive the fixed-point existence theorem
    • Implement MFG iteration algorithms
  • You do need to know:
    • MFG explains why GE-LAV’s equilibrium correction is different from DCF
    • The collective externality is real and quantifiable (~2.3%/yr welfare loss)
    • Pigouvian tax (next session) corrects this externality
    • When recommending portfolio decisions, you’re navigating a system where everyone’s decisions interact
  • Speaker notes: Track 1 students should walk away feeling competent without feeling overwhelmed.

31.4.14 Slide 21.14 — What Track 2 Will Build

  • Title: “Track 2: what Session 26 will derive”
  • Track 2 in Session 26 will:
    • Set up the McKean-Vlasov SDE for GE-LAV liquidity dynamics
    • Prove existence of MFG equilibrium via Schauder fixed-point theorem
    • Prove uniqueness under the stability condition \(\kappa > \gamma\)
    • Quantify the propagation-of-chaos rate of convergence
    • Implement the MFG fixed-point iteration numerically
  • Recommended pre-reading for Track 2:
    • Carmona & Delarue, Probabilistic Theory of Mean-Field Games, Ch. 1–2
  • Speaker notes: Track 2 students should leave knowing they have substantial work ahead.

31.4.15 Slide 21.15 — Bridge to Session 22

  • Title: “Where we go next”
  • Today: MFG equilibrium as a fixed point on population distributions
  • Session 22: Computing those distributions — the Fokker-Planck equation and the master equation
  • Sessions 23–24: Putting it all together — the LAV operator, the GE equilibrium pricing, the Pigouvian tax
  • Speaker notes: Tease the rest of Unit 4. We’re building toward Session 24 where everything reunifies.

31.5 Discussion Questions

  1. The McKean-Vlasov externality is calibrated at ~2.3%/yr aggregate welfare loss across the private capital market. As a fraction of total private market AUM ($13T), that’s ~$300B/year. How should this magnitude affect regulator priorities relative to other systemic risk concerns (bank runs, sovereign debt, climate transition risk)?
  2. Propagation of chaos says individual LP outcomes are approximately independent given the population distribution. Does this break down in concentrated markets (few large LPs)? How would you test this empirically?
  3. MFG equilibrium has a stability condition \(\kappa > \gamma\). In a hypothetical market with very fast information transmission and slow mean reversion (perhaps DeFi), what happens to the equilibrium? Multiple? Unstable?

31.6 Worked Example: The McKean-Vlasov Externality, Quantified

Setup: Consider 1,000 LPs each with \(\$100M\) in PE positions during the 2008–2010 GFC.

Step 1: Individual exit decision

LP A exits \(\$100M\) at a 30% discount: saves \(\$70M\) in stress capital but takes \(\$30M\) loss vs. NAV.

Step 2: Externality

LP A’s exit increases secondary supply marginally. Result: secondary discounts widen by ~0.1 basis point for all other LPs.

For 999 other LPs each with \(\$100M\): - Additional loss: \(999 \times \$100M \times 0.001\% = \$1M\) in aggregate

LP A captured a \(\$70M\) private benefit. They imposed a \(\$1M\) aggregate externality.

Step 3: When everyone exits simultaneously

Now 1,000 LPs all exit: - Each one creates a 0.1 bp externality - Aggregate widening: 100 bp = 1% additional discount on top of the original - \(\$100M\) positions all worth \(\$1M\) less each because of collective behavior - Aggregate externality: \(\$1B\)

Step 4: Welfare gap

Of the \(\$30B\) in losses from the collective exit, approximately \(\$1-2B\) is externality cost — losses that wouldn’t exist if exits had been coordinated.

Generalization to the calibrated 2.3%/yr figure:

Over a typical decade with one or two stress episodes, aggregate externality losses accumulate to approximately 2.3% of average private market AUM per year. At $$13T AUM = $$300B/year welfare loss.

Teaching note: This calculation is illustrative. Real MFG calibration is more involved (book Ch 19). But the order of magnitude is right.

31.7 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “If MFG is the right framework, why don’t all macro models use it?” Many do now. MFG is the modern descendant of representative-agent macro models. Adoption is gradual; the math is new.
  • “How is McKean-Vlasov different from just adding correlation to multiple SDEs?” Correlation is bilateral (between pairs). McKean-Vlasov is aggregative — each agent reacts to the distribution of all others, not individual others. Different structure.
  • “Does the externality require irrationality?” No — fully rational LPs create the externality. Each one optimizes its own welfare; nobody internalizes the aggregate effect.
  • “Is the Pigouvian tax just another regulatory burden?” It’s a welfare-improving instrument — it makes everyone better off in aggregate by aligning private and social incentives. Standard externality theory.

31.8 What to Expect Next Session

Session 22 covers Fokker-Planck and the master equation — the computational tools for tracking how distributions evolve over time. These are the PDEs that the MFG framework requires.

Topics: - The Fokker-Planck equation: how the distribution of \(L_t\) evolves - The master equation: how the value function evolves on the space of distributions - Why these are intuitive (heat-equation analogues)

Reading: Book Chapters 14, 15 (concepts only).

### Slide 21.11 — Propagation of Chaos

- **Title:** "Why mean-field approximation is good"
- **Setup:**
  - We pretend each LP only cares about *the distribution* $\mu_t$, not about individual other LPs
  - Is this approximation valid when there are 100 LPs? 1,000? 10,000?
- **Propagation of chaos (informal):** "As the number of LPs $N \to \infty$, individual LP outcomes become statistically independent of each other, conditional on the population distribution"
- **For finite $N$:**
  - Error scales as $1/\sqrt{N}$
  - At $N = 100$ LPs: error ~10%
  - At $N = 1000$ LPs: error ~3%
  - At $N = 10000$ LPs: error <1%
- **In private markets:** "There are ~10,000 LPs in major institutional private markets. Mean-field is a very good approximation."
- **Speaker notes:** Justify the approximation empirically. The framework's assumptions aren't wild.

## Worked Example: The McKean-Vlasov Externality, Quantified

**Setup:** Consider 1,000 LPs each with $\$100M$ in PE positions during the 2008–2010 GFC.

**Step 1: Individual exit decision**

LP A exits $\$100M$ at a 30% discount: saves $\$70M$ in stress capital but takes $\$30M$ loss vs. NAV.

**Step 2: Externality**

LP A's exit increases secondary supply marginally. Result: secondary discounts widen by ~0.1 basis point for all other LPs.

For 999 other LPs each with $\$100M$:
- Additional loss: $999 \times \$100M \times 0.001\% = \$1M$ in aggregate

LP A captured a $\$70M$ private benefit. They imposed a $\$1M$ aggregate externality.

**Step 3: When everyone exits simultaneously**

Now 1,000 LPs all exit:
- Each one creates a 0.1 bp externality
- Aggregate widening: 100 bp = 1% additional discount on top of the original
- $\$100M$ positions all worth $\$1M$ less each because of collective behavior
- Aggregate externality: $\$1B$

**Step 4: Welfare gap**

Of the $\$30B$ in losses from the collective exit, approximately $\$1-2B$ is *externality cost* — losses that wouldn't exist if exits had been coordinated.

**Generalization to the calibrated 2.3%/yr figure:**

Over a typical decade with one or two stress episodes, aggregate externality losses accumulate to approximately 2.3% of average private market AUM per year. At $\$13T AUM = $\$300B/year welfare loss.

**Teaching note:** This calculation is illustrative. Real MFG calibration is more involved (book Ch 19). But the order of magnitude is right.

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"If MFG is the right framework, why don't all macro models use it?"** Many do now. MFG is the modern descendant of representative-agent macro models. Adoption is gradual; the math is new.
- **"How is McKean-Vlasov different from just adding correlation to multiple SDEs?"** Correlation is bilateral (between pairs). McKean-Vlasov is *aggregative* — each agent reacts to the *distribution* of all others, not individual others. Different structure.
- **"Does the externality require irrationality?"** No — fully rational LPs create the externality. Each one optimizes its own welfare; nobody internalizes the aggregate effect.
- **"Is the Pigouvian tax just another regulatory burden?"** It's a *welfare-improving* instrument — it makes everyone better off in aggregate by aligning private and social incentives. Standard externality theory.
