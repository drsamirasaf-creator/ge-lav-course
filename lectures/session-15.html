23.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. Define the pacing problem — maintaining target allocation given irregular GP capital calls and distributions.
  2. Distinguish strategic from tactical private market allocation decisions.
  3. Apply the GE-LAV regime-conditional pacing framework: increase/maintain/reduce commitments by liquidity regime.
  4. Compute stress-scenario allocations for key episodes (GFC depth, COVID, 2022 rate shock).
  5. Recognize the multi-asset extension and why aggregate liquidity risk exceeds the sum of asset-class risks.

23.4 Slide Content Briefs (New Slides Needed)

23.4.1 Slide 15.1 — Title

  • Title: “Pacing · Tactical Allocation · Stress Scenarios”
  • Tag line: “Operationalizing the GE-LAV portfolio framework”
  • Speaker notes: Today is the how to actually do it session. Strategic allocation (Session 14) tells you the target; today tells you how to get there and what to do under stress.

23.4.2 Slide 15.2 — The Pacing Problem

  • Title: “Maintaining target allocation: harder than it looks”
  • The challenge:
    • Commit to a PE fund today; capital calls trickle in over 3-5 years
    • Distributions come back unpredictably starting year 4-5
    • Public market positions also move (denominator effect)
    • Net result: actual PE allocation drifts substantially from target
  • Pacing tools:
    • Forward commitment pacing: Schedule new commitments to maintain target as old funds wind down
    • Tactical rebalancing: Sell secondaries / pause commitments when allocation drifts above target
    • Liquidity buffer: Hold cash reserves to handle unexpected capital calls without forced public market sales
  • Speaker notes: Pacing is operationally critical. Many LPs spend more time on pacing than on manager selection.

23.4.3 Slide 15.3 — Standard vs. GE-LAV Pacing

  • Title: “Two approaches to pacing”
  • Standard pacing (industry practice):
    • Constant annual commitment based on long-run target
    • Adjust mechanically for denominator effect
    • No explicit liquidity-state consideration
  • GE-LAV regime-conditional pacing:
    • Pace based on current liquidity state \(L_t\) AND allocation drift
    • More aggressive in boom regimes (capacity is cheap)
    • Defensive in stress regimes (preserve liquidity, capture distressed opportunities)
  • Speaker notes: Standard practice ignores half the information. GE-LAV uses both portfolio state and market state.

23.4.4 Slide 15.4 — Regime-Conditional Pacing Framework (Class 24 reuse)

  • Title: “Commitment pacing by liquidity regime”
  • Table:
Regime \(L_t\) range Action Commitment level
Boom \(L > +0.5\) Increase 1.3× steady-state
Normal \(-0.5 < L < +0.5\) Maintain 1.0× steady-state
Stress \(-1.0 < L < -0.5\) Pause new; hold 0.5× steady-state
Deep stress \(L < -1.0\) Opportunistic secondaries; pause primaries 0.7× (secondaries-weighted)
  • Speaker notes: This is operational. Allocators can implement this. Note that deep stress increases via secondaries — you’re a buyer when prices are low.

23.4.5 Slide 15.5 — Stress Scenario Analysis (New Slide)

  • Title: “Calibrated stress scenarios”
  • Three scenarios:
Scenario \(L_t\) Path Duration NAV Impact
GFC-depth (2008) -2.0 over 6 months, recovery in 24 months 30 months -35% to -50%
COVID-style -1.0 over 1 month, recovery in 6 months 7 months -15% to -25%
Rate shock (2022) -0.8 sustained for 24+ months 24+ months -15% to -20%
  • What stress testing reveals:
    • GFC: severe but recoverable for long-horizon LPs
    • COVID: brief; minimal impact for any non-forced-sellers
    • Rate shock: persistent — tests mean-reversion assumption
  • Speaker notes: These are the three regimes you should plan for. GE-LAV can stress against each.

23.4.6 Slide 15.6 — Stress Test Output: Portfolio Diagnostics

  • Title: “Running a portfolio through the stress test”
  • For each position:
    • Current NAV
    • GE-LAV value at current regime
    • GE-LAV value at GFC depth
    • GE-LAV value at COVID-style scenario
    • GE-LAV value at rate-shock scenario
    • Exit recommendation under each scenario
  • Output: Three rebalanced portfolios for three regimes — your contingency plans
  • Speaker notes: The point isn’t to be in stress; it’s to know what you’d do if stress happened.

23.4.7 Slide 15.7 — Multi-Asset GE-LAV: Systemic Liquidity Beta

  • Title: “Why aggregate liquidity risk > sum of asset risks”
  • The setup:
    • PE, infrastructure, private credit all have their own \(L_t\) dynamics
    • BUT they all load on a common aggregate liquidity factor \(L_\text{sys,t}\)
    • Define systemic beta: \(\beta_i = \dfrac{\text{Cov}(dL_{i,t}, dL_\text{sys,t})}{\text{Var}(dL_\text{sys,t})}\)
  • Empirical pattern:
    • PE buyout: \(\beta_i = 1.2\) (most sensitive to aggregate liquidity)
    • Growth equity: \(\beta_i = 1.1\)
    • Real estate: \(\beta_i = 0.9\)
    • Infrastructure: \(\beta_i = 0.7\)
    • Private credit: \(\beta_i = 0.8\)
  • Implication: “During systemic stress (2008), all of these decline simultaneously. Diversification across private asset classes is illusory.”
  • Speaker notes: This is a critical risk management insight. LPs who hold “diversified” private market portfolios often face correlated liquidity risk.

23.4.8 Slide 15.8 — The Diversification Illusion in Private Markets

  • Title: “Why ‘diversification across alternatives’ isn’t”
  • Setup: “An LP holds 15% PE, 10% private credit, 8% infrastructure, 5% real estate = 38% in ‘diversified’ private markets”
  • Apparent diversification: Correlation of reported NAVs: 0.3-0.5 → looks diversified
  • GE-LAV reality: Correlation of liquidity-adjusted values during stress: 0.8-0.9 → highly concentrated
  • Reason: All asset classes load on the same \(L_\text{sys,t}\); smoothed NAVs hide this
  • Multi-asset hedge demand at GFC depth: Total private allocation drops to ~12-15% (vs. 38% target)
  • Speaker notes: This is the multi-asset risk management lesson. The illusion of diversification is the largest blind spot in institutional private market portfolios.

23.6 Discussion Questions

  1. CalPERS, Yale, and your school’s endowment all have stated PE targets between 10% and 30%. Apply the multi-asset framework: are these “PE targets” actually private-markets-totals targets when you include private credit, infrastructure, real estate? How does that change the over-allocation analysis?
  2. The 2008 stress test reveals that aggregate private market exposure can fall 40-50%. Does that mean LPs should hold a cash buffer of ~$0.50 per $1 of private markets exposure? What does GE-LAV actually recommend?
  3. The pacing framework recommends increasing commitments in deep stress (via secondaries). Most institutions do the opposite — they cut commitments in crises. Why does the framework recommend the contrarian approach, and why don’t institutions follow it?

23.7 Worked Numerical Example: Pacing Through a Stress Scenario

Scenario: A $10B pension fund with target allocation of 15% PE ($1.5B). Current state: late 2023, allocation is 18% ($1.8B) due to public market gains. Liquidity state \(L_t \approx -0.7\) (mild stress). Future calendar of commitments and distributions known approximately.

Step 1: Identify the regime

\(L_t = -0.7\) → Stress regime (between \(-1.0\) and \(-0.5\))

GE-LAV pacing recommendation: Pause new commitments; hold existing

Step 2: Determine target trajectory

If commitments paused, expected drawdown from natural fund maturities: - 2024: $200M distributions in, $0 new commitments → allocation drops to ~17% - 2025: $300M distributions in, $0 new → ~15.5% - 2026: Reach target 15% if conditions normalize

Step 3: Stress test

If GFC-style scenario hits in 2024: - NAV impact: -35% on PE → allocation appears to fall to ~12% (mechanically) - But liquidity-adjusted value is even lower - Forced rebalancing into PE? Or wait for recovery?

GE-LAV recommendation: Pause primary commitments; allocate to secondaries at distressed pricing (would increase PE exposure but at favorable entry \(L_t\))

Step 4: Pivot in different scenarios

  • If stress deepens (L → -1.5): Deep-stress regime → opportunistic secondaries, ~0.7× steady-state pacing
  • If stress eases (L → 0): Normal regime → resume primary commitments at steady-state pace
  • If rate-shock persists 2+ years: Recalibrate; possibly accept that target should drop given persistent stress

Teaching note: This is adaptive allocation management. Most pension funds operate on fixed pacing schedules. GE-LAV is more responsive.

23.8 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “Why would I increase secondaries when everyone else is selling?” Because everyone selling is the reason the discount is wide. Buying at the wide discount is the GE-LAV-optimal contrarian play.
  • “Doesn’t this require predicting recovery? Isn’t that hard?” Yes, prediction is hard. But OU mean reversion is a property of the data, not a prediction — over enough time, \(L_t\) returns to \(\bar{L}\). The exit boundary uses this property without requiring point forecasts.
  • “What if my IPS doesn’t allow secondary purchases?” Then your IPS reflects an obsolete framework. Bringing this to the IC for IPS amendment is one career-relevant action a GE-LAV-trained allocator can take.
  • “How is multi-asset hedge different from CAPM beta?” CAPM beta is return covariance with market. Multi-asset hedge here is liquidity state covariance with aggregate liquidity. Different math, different implications.
