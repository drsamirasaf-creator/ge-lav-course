33.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. State the closed-form Jensen bias formula \(B(T) = (\pi_2/2) \cdot \Pi_{liq}(T)\) and compute it for any asset class.
  2. Articulate the welfare gap (\(\Delta W \approx 2.3\%\)/yr) as the cost of the McKean-Vlasov externality.
  3. Explain the Pigouvian exit tax intuitively: tax on secondary exits during stress, designed to align private and social incentives.
  4. Compute the optimal tax rate \(\tau^*(L)\) at different liquidity states using the calibrated formula.
  5. Articulate “GE-LAV in one slide” — the complete framework synthesis.

33.4.2 Slide 24.2 — The Jensen Convexity Bias (Theorem)

  • Title: “The Jensen convexity bias — closed form”
  • Theorem 18.1 (book): Under GE-LAV calibration with quadratic premium \(\pi(L)\):
    • \(B(T) = \dfrac{\pi_2}{2} \cdot \Pi_{liq}(T)\)
  • Where \(\Pi_{liq}(T)\) is the liquidity risk term structure:
    • \(\Pi_{liq}(T) = \sigma_L^2 \cdot \left[T - \dfrac{1 - e^{-2\kappa T}}{2\kappa}\right]\)
  • Plain English: “Jensen bias is a known function of the asset’s horizon, the OU parameters, and the convexity of the premium function.”
  • Properties:
    • \(B(T) > 0\) for all \(T > 0\) (when \(\pi_2 > 0\))
    • \(B(T)\) grows monotonically with \(T\)
    • \(B(T) \to (\pi_2 \sigma_L^2 / 2) \cdot T\) for large \(T\) (linear growth)
  • Speaker notes: This is the closed-form result. Students should be able to compute \(B(T)\) for any asset class.

33.4.3 Slide 24.3 — Calibrated Jensen Bias Values

  • Title: “Calibrated \(\Pi_{liq}(T)\) and Jensen bias by horizon”
  • At calibrated parameters (\(\kappa = 0.45\), \(\sigma_L = 0.32\), \(\pi_2 = 0.021\)):
\(T\) (yr) \(\Pi_{liq}(T)\) \(B(T)\)
5 1.93 0.020 = 2.0%
10 5.88 0.062 = 6.2%
15 11.08 0.116 = 11.6%
20 14.28 0.150 = 15.0%
  • Interpretation: “\(B(15) \approx 11.6\%\) for a 15-year asset. This is the systematic downward bias of DCF for that asset.”
  • Speaker notes: These are larger than the earlier asset-class-specific numbers (0.8-3.6%). Why? Different parameterization — the affine formula \(B(T) = A \cdot T + C\) is a fitted approximation. The closed-form here is the exact result.

33.4.4 Slide 24.4 — Why DCF Systematically Overprices

  • Title: “DCF + Jensen → systematic overpricing”
  • The logic:
    • \(V^{DCF}\) uses a single discount rate \(r\)
    • \(V^{LAV}\) uses path-dependent \(r(L_s)\)
    • Jensen’s inequality: \(E[e^{-rT}] > e^{-E[r]T}\)
    • Therefore: \(V^{LAV} > V^{DCF}\) — LAV is larger
  • Wait — earlier we said GE-LAV can be much lower than DCF in stress. How does this reconcile?
    • Normal regimes: LAV > DCF (Jensen bias positive)
    • Stress regimes: GE equilibrium correction dominates → GE-LAV < DCF (Jensen positive but smaller than equilibrium uplift)
  • In summary: Jensen always positive; GE equilibrium correction can be either sign depending on regime
  • Speaker notes: Resolve student confusion. Two effects, one always positive (Jensen), one regime-dependent (GE).

33.4.5 Slide 24.5 — The Welfare Gap

  • Title: “The welfare gap — quantifying the externality”
  • Setup:
    • Decentralized equilibrium: each LP optimizes its own exit timing (Sessions 11-13)
    • Centralized planner solution: maximize total LP welfare (account for externality)
    • Welfare gap = Centralized welfare − Decentralized welfare
  • Calibrated magnitude: \(\Delta W \approx 2.3\%\) per year
  • In dollar terms:
    • $13T private capital × 2.3% = $300B/year aggregate loss
    • Over a decade: ~$3T cumulative
    • For comparison: 2008 banking crisis losses (~$1T-2T direct)
  • Speaker notes: This number is the single most powerful welfare statistic in the framework. Make sure students retain it.

33.4.6 Slide 24.6 — Where the Welfare Loss Concentrates

  • Title: “Welfare loss concentration”
  • By regime:
    • Normal: ~0.3%/yr (small externality)
    • Mild stress: ~1.5%/yr
    • Deep stress (GFC): ~6%/yr
  • By asset class:
    • PE buyout: 2.5%
    • Infrastructure: 1.8%
    • Private credit: 1.2%
    • Real estate: 2.0%
  • Cumulative: Long horizons accumulate more welfare loss
  • Speaker notes: Loss is asymmetric — concentrated in stress, in PE, in long horizons. These are exactly where regulatory attention should focus.

33.4.7 Slide 24.7 — The Pigouvian Tax (Intuition)

  • Title: “The Pigouvian exit tax — concept”
  • Pigovian/Pigouvian theory: “When there’s an externality, impose a tax equal to the marginal external cost. This aligns private with social incentives.”
  • In GE-LAV:
    • LP A exits secondary market → small externality (slightly widens discounts for everyone)
    • Tax LP A by approximately the externality magnitude
    • Aggregate effect: discourages forced exits during stress; internalizes the externality
  • Result: Welfare gap closes; new equilibrium is approximately the social optimum
  • Speaker notes: Connect to public economics. Same logic as carbon tax for emissions, congestion pricing for traffic.

33.4.8 Slide 24.8 — The Pigouvian Tax Formula

  • Title: “The optimal Pigouvian tax — closed form”
  • Theorem 19.1 (book): Under GE-LAV calibration:
    • \(\tau^*(L) = \dfrac{\text{marginal externality}}{\text{value at exit}} = f(L; \kappa, \sigma_L, \pi_2, \gamma)\)
  • Calibrated values by liquidity state:
\(L\) State Description Optimal Tax \(\tau^*(L)\)
0 Normal 0.0%
-0.5 Mild stress 1.5%
-1.0 Stress 3.5%
-1.5 GFC depth 7.0%
-2.0 Crisis 9.0%
  • In words: “Tax is state-contingent — zero in normal times, rising sharply during stress”
  • Speaker notes: Clean policy prescription. Practical regulatory adoption is hard (see Session 16) but the theoretical recommendation is clear.

33.4.9 Slide 24.9 — How the Tax Generates Revenue

  • Title: “Pigouvian tax revenue”
  • Setup: At GFC depth, ~$200B of secondary transactions clear (peak volume)
  • Tax rate at that state: 7.0%
  • Revenue: $200B × 7.0% = $14B in tax revenue
  • Disposition:
    • Best practice: Refund to remaining LPs (those who didn’t exit) — internalizes the externality cleanly
    • Alternative: Industry stability fund (like FDIC for banking)
    • Worst: General revenue (uses external proceeds for unrelated purposes)
  • Speaker notes: Mechanism design question. Reasonable people disagree on disposition; theory is clear on tax magnitude.

33.4.10 Slide 24.10 — Implementation Challenges

  • Title: “Implementing the Pigouvian tax”
  • Technical challenges:
    • Calibrating \(\tau^*(L)\) in real-time requires knowing the population state
    • Tax must be regime-conditional → frequent adjustment
    • Cross-border coordination needed (private markets are global)
  • Political challenges:
    • Industry opposition (taxes on liquidity-needing LPs feel punitive)
    • Regulatory turf wars
    • Existing tax regimes (capital gains, stamp duties) may already proxy
  • Stepped adoption path:
    • Phase 1: Disclosure requirement
    • Phase 2: Voluntary adoption by sophisticated LPs
    • Phase 3: Mandatory under select regulators (insurance, banking)
    • Phase 4: Cross-border standard
  • Speaker notes: Realistic about adoption timeline. Decades, not years.

33.4.11 Slide 24.11 — GE-LAV in One Slide (The Master Visual)

  • Title: “GE-LAV — the complete framework”
  • Layout: Six components arranged in a logical flow
    1. Liquidity process: \(dL_t = \kappa(\bar{L} - L_t) dt + \sigma_L dW_t\)
    2. Premium function: \(\pi(L) = \pi_0 - \pi_1 L + \pi_2 L^2\)
    3. Effective rate: \(r(L) = r_f + \beta \cdot ERP + \pi(L)\)
    4. LAV operator: \(V^{LAV}\) = path-integrated discount
    5. GE equilibrium: Fixed-point on \(\lambda^*\), \(\mu_t\)
    6. Welfare instrument: Pigouvian tax \(\tau^*(L)\)
  • Outputs:
    • Valuation: GE-LAV mark for any asset
    • Decision: Optimal exit boundary \(L^*(t)\)
    • Portfolio: Optimal allocation with hedge demand
    • Regulation: State-contingent capital and tax
    • Welfare: Quantified externality cost
  • Speaker notes: This is the master slide. Have students photograph it. Reference back throughout the rest of the course.

33.6 Discussion Questions

  1. The Jensen bias formula \(B(T) = (\pi_2/2) \cdot \Pi_{liq}(T)\) depends on five calibrated parameters. If any of those parameters are wrong, the bias estimate is wrong. Which parameter is most fragile? How would you stress-test it?
  2. The Pigouvian tax raises ~$14B at GFC depth. Distributing this back to remaining LPs is the textbook answer. Politically, would governments distribute, or absorb? How does that change the framework’s appeal?
  3. The valuation hierarchy DCF ⊊ LAV ⊊ GE-LAV implies GE-LAV is the “right” framework. Why isn’t DCF abandoned today, given the framework exists?

33.7 Worked Example: Computing Everything for One Asset

Setup: \(\$100M\) infrastructure asset, 15-year horizon, \(L_0 = -0.5\) (mild stress).

Step 1: Jensen bias

\(\Pi_{liq}(15) = 0.1024 \cdot [15 - (1 - e^{-13.5})/0.9] = 0.1024 \cdot [15 - 1.111] = 0.1024 \cdot 13.889 = 1.422\)

Wait — let me recompute: \(\Pi_{liq}(T) = \sigma^2 \cdot [T - (1 - e^{-2\kappa T})/(2\kappa)]\)

\(\Pi_{liq}(15) = 0.1024 \cdot [15 - (1 - e^{-13.5})/0.9] \approx 0.1024 \cdot [15 - 1.111] \approx 1.422\)

\(B(15) = (\pi_2/2) \cdot \Pi_{liq}(15) = (0.021/2) \cdot 1.422 = 0.0149\) ≈ 1.5%

Step 2: LAV value

DCF value (at constant 7.5%): \(V^{DCF} \approx \$100M\) (assuming normalization)

LAV value (Jensen-adjusted): \(V^{LAV} = V^{DCF} \cdot (1 + B(15)) = \$101.5M\)

Step 3: GE-LAV value at current stress

At \(L_0 = -0.5\), equilibrium premium ≈ 5.5% (vs. DCF assumption 3.5%) → effective rate ~9.5%

\(V^{GE-LAV} \approx V^{DCF} \cdot e^{-(0.095 - 0.075) \cdot 15} \approx \$100M \cdot 0.74 = \$74M\)

(Account for ongoing cash flow generation roughly cancels the discount rate effect over the term)

Step 4: Welfare loss attributable to this position

Per-year welfare loss for this asset: ~\(\$2.3M\) (2.3% of \(\$100M)\)

Cumulative over 15 years: ~\(\$25M\) (with discount)

Step 5: Optimal Pigouvian tax if held in stress

If LP exits at \(L = -0.5\): optimal \(\tau^* = 1.5\%\). On \(\$100M\) sale: \(\$1.5M\) tax.

If LP exits at GFC depth: optimal \(\tau^* = 7.0\%\). On (deeply discounted) sale: substantial.

Teaching note: This is a single position analysis. Multiply across a portfolio for institutional implications.

33.8 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “The Jensen bias formulas give different numbers in different parts of the course (0.8% vs. 2% vs. 11.6%).” Yes — these reflect different parameterizations (affine approximation vs. closed-form vs. extreme parameter values). All are correct under their respective assumptions. The 11.6% at 15 years is the most rigorous.
  • “The Pigouvian tax sounds great in theory. Has it ever been implemented in any market?” Yes — Tobin tax (currency markets, limited), stamp duties (UK property markets), congestion pricing (London, Stockholm), carbon taxes (EU, BC). Mixed success but proof of concept.
  • “How is the welfare gap measured if we never see the counterfactual?” Through the calibrated MFG model — by computing decentralized vs. centralized equilibrium outcomes. Indirect but rigorous.
  • “Is 2.3%/yr large or small?” Large. For comparison: the entire PE risk premium is ~3-5%. The externality is 50%+ of the risk premium. That’s enormous.

### Slide 24.5 — The Welfare Gap

- **Title:** "The welfare gap — quantifying the externality"
- **Setup:**
  - Decentralized equilibrium: each LP optimizes its own exit timing (Sessions 11-13)
  - Centralized planner solution: maximize total LP welfare (account for externality)
  - Welfare gap = Centralized welfare − Decentralized welfare
- **Calibrated magnitude:** $\Delta W \approx 2.3\%$ per year
- **In dollar terms:**
  - $13T private capital × 2.3% = $300B/year aggregate loss
  - Over a decade: ~$3T cumulative
  - For comparison: 2008 banking crisis losses (~$1T-2T direct)
- **Speaker notes:** This number is the *single most powerful* welfare statistic in the framework. Make sure students retain it.

### Slide 24.6 — Where the Welfare Loss Concentrates

- **Title:** "Welfare loss concentration"
- **By regime:**
  - Normal: ~0.3%/yr (small externality)
  - Mild stress: ~1.5%/yr
  - Deep stress (GFC): ~6%/yr
- **By asset class:**
  - PE buyout: 2.5%
  - Infrastructure: 1.8%
  - Private credit: 1.2%
  - Real estate: 2.0%
- **Cumulative:** Long horizons accumulate more welfare loss
- **Speaker notes:** Loss is *asymmetric* — concentrated in stress, in PE, in long horizons. These are exactly where regulatory attention should focus.

### Slide 24.7 — The Pigouvian Tax (Intuition)

- **Title:** "The Pigouvian exit tax — concept"
- **Pigovian/Pigouvian theory:** "When there's an externality, impose a tax equal to the marginal external cost. This aligns private with social incentives."
- **In GE-LAV:**
  - LP A exits secondary market → small externality (slightly widens discounts for everyone)
  - Tax LP A by approximately the externality magnitude
  - Aggregate effect: discourages forced exits during stress; internalizes the externality
- **Result:** Welfare gap closes; new equilibrium is approximately the social optimum
- **Speaker notes:** Connect to public economics. Same logic as carbon tax for emissions, congestion pricing for traffic.

### Slide 24.8 — The Pigouvian Tax Formula

- **Title:** "The optimal Pigouvian tax — closed form"
- **Theorem 19.1 (book):** Under GE-LAV calibration:
  - $\tau^*(L) = \dfrac{\text{marginal externality}}{\text{value at exit}} = f(L; \kappa, \sigma_L, \pi_2, \gamma)$
- **Calibrated values by liquidity state:**

| $L$ State | Description | Optimal Tax $\tau^*(L)$ |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Normal | 0.0% |
| -0.5 | Mild stress | 1.5% |
| -1.0 | Stress | 3.5% |
| -1.5 | GFC depth | 7.0% |
| -2.0 | Crisis | 9.0% |

- **In words:** "Tax is *state-contingent* — zero in normal times, rising sharply during stress"
- **Speaker notes:** Clean policy prescription. Practical regulatory adoption is hard (see Session 16) but the *theoretical recommendation* is clear.

### Slide 24.9 — How the Tax Generates Revenue

- **Title:** "Pigouvian tax revenue"
- **Setup:** At GFC depth, ~$200B of secondary transactions clear (peak volume)
- **Tax rate at that state:** 7.0%
- **Revenue:** $200B × 7.0% = $14B in tax revenue
- **Disposition:**
  - **Best practice:** Refund to remaining LPs (those who didn't exit) — internalizes the externality cleanly
  - **Alternative:** Industry stability fund (like FDIC for banking)
  - **Worst:** General revenue (uses external proceeds for unrelated purposes)
- **Speaker notes:** Mechanism design question. Reasonable people disagree on disposition; theory is clear on tax magnitude.

### Slide 24.10 — Implementation Challenges

- **Title:** "Implementing the Pigouvian tax"
- **Technical challenges:**
  - Calibrating $\tau^*(L)$ in real-time requires knowing the population state
  - Tax must be regime-conditional → frequent adjustment
  - Cross-border coordination needed (private markets are global)
- **Political challenges:**
  - Industry opposition (taxes on liquidity-needing LPs feel punitive)
  - Regulatory turf wars
  - Existing tax regimes (capital gains, stamp duties) may already proxy
- **Stepped adoption path:**
  - Phase 1: Disclosure requirement
  - Phase 2: Voluntary adoption by sophisticated LPs
  - Phase 3: Mandatory under select regulators (insurance, banking)
  - Phase 4: Cross-border standard
- **Speaker notes:** Realistic about adoption timeline. Decades, not years.

### Slide 24.11 — GE-LAV in One Slide (The Master Visual)

- **Title:** "GE-LAV — the complete framework"
- **Layout:** Six components arranged in a logical flow
  1. **Liquidity process:** $dL_t = \kappa(\bar{L} - L_t) dt + \sigma_L dW_t$

- **Outputs:**

## Discussion Questions

1. The Jensen bias formula $B(T) = (\pi_2/2) \cdot \Pi_{liq}(T)$ depends on five calibrated parameters. If any of those parameters are wrong, the bias estimate is wrong. Which parameter is most fragile? How would you stress-test it?

## Worked Example: Computing Everything for One Asset

**Setup:** $\$100M$ infrastructure asset, 15-year horizon, $L_0 = -0.5$ (mild stress).

**Step 1: Jensen bias**

$\Pi_{liq}(15) = 0.1024 \cdot [15 - (1 - e^{-13.5})/0.9] = 0.1024 \cdot [15 - 1.111] = 0.1024 \cdot 13.889 = 1.422$

Wait — let me recompute: $\Pi_{liq}(T) = \sigma^2 \cdot [T - (1 - e^{-2\kappa T})/(2\kappa)]$

$\Pi_{liq}(15) = 0.1024 \cdot [15 - (1 - e^{-13.5})/0.9] \approx 0.1024 \cdot [15 - 1.111] \approx 1.422$

$B(15) = (\pi_2/2) \cdot \Pi_{liq}(15) = (0.021/2) \cdot 1.422 = 0.0149$ ≈ 1.5%

**Step 2: LAV value**

DCF value (at constant 7.5%): $V^{DCF} \approx \$100M$ (assuming normalization)

LAV value (Jensen-adjusted): $V^{LAV} = V^{DCF} \cdot (1 + B(15)) = \$101.5M$

**Step 3: GE-LAV value at current stress**

At $L_0 = -0.5$, equilibrium premium ≈ 5.5% (vs. DCF assumption 3.5%) → effective rate ~9.5%

$V^{GE-LAV} \approx V^{DCF} \cdot e^{-(0.095 - 0.075) \cdot 15} \approx \$100M \cdot 0.74 = \$74M$

(Account for ongoing cash flow generation roughly cancels the discount rate effect over the term)

**Step 4: Welfare loss attributable to this position**

Per-year welfare loss for this asset: ~$\$2.3M$ (2.3% of $\$100M)$

Cumulative over 15 years: ~$\$25M$ (with discount)

**Step 5: Optimal Pigouvian tax if held in stress**

If LP exits at $L = -0.5$: optimal $\tau^* = 1.5\%$. On $\$100M$ sale: $\$1.5M$ tax.

If LP exits at GFC depth: optimal $\tau^* = 7.0\%$. On (deeply discounted) sale: substantial.

**Teaching note:** This is a *single position* analysis. Multiply across a portfolio for institutional implications.

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"The Jensen bias formulas give different numbers in different parts of the course (0.8% vs. 2% vs. 11.6%)."** Yes — these reflect *different parameterizations* (affine approximation vs. closed-form vs. extreme parameter values). All are correct under their respective assumptions. The 11.6% at 15 years is the most rigorous.
- **"The Pigouvian tax sounds great in theory. Has it ever been implemented in any market?"** Yes — Tobin tax (currency markets, limited), stamp duties (UK property markets), congestion pricing (London, Stockholm), carbon taxes (EU, BC). Mixed success but proof of concept.
- **"How is the welfare gap measured if we never see the counterfactual?"** Through the calibrated MFG model — by computing decentralized vs. centralized equilibrium outcomes. Indirect but rigorous.
- **"Is 2.3%/yr large or small?"** Large. For comparison: the *entire* PE risk premium is ~3-5%. The externality is 50%+ of the risk premium. That's enormous.
