16  Session 6: IRR · The Internal Rate of Return Problem

Unit 2 — Measurement and Theory
Book Chapter 4 (sections 4.2–4.4)
Slide source All new
Slides needed ~15 new
Track Common core (both tracks)

16.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. Define IRR precisely and state its mathematical relationship to NPV.
  2. Identify the two structural biases of IRR (reinvestment assumption + sign-pattern dependence).
  3. Distinguish money-weighted from time-weighted returns and explain when each is appropriate.
  4. Compute IRR for a simple cash flow pattern and identify when multiple IRRs exist.
  5. Explain why a constant-rate metric cannot correctly summarize performance when discount rates are stochastic.
  6. Prepare for Session 7’s PME discussion by understanding why IRR’s failures motivated PME’s development.

16.2 Pre-Class Assignment

  • Read: Book Chapter 4, sections 4.2–4.4 (~10 pages)
  • Optional: Phalippou (2008), “The Hazards of Using IRR to Measure Performance: The Case of Private Equity,” Journal of Performance Measurement

16.4 Slide Content Briefs (New Slides Needed)

16.4.1 Slide 6.1 — Title

  • Title: “The IRR Problem”
  • Tag line: “Why the most-used metric in private markets is structurally broken”
  • Speaker notes: Frame Unit 2 as the measurement counterpart to Unit 1’s valuation critique. Bad valuations cause bad returns calculations.

16.4.2 Slide 6.2 — IRR: Definition

  • Title: “IRR formally”
  • Definition: “IRR is the discount rate \(r\) that makes NPV = 0”
  • Equation: \(0 = \sum_{t=0}^{T} \dfrac{CF_t}{(1+r)^t}\)
  • Solving: Implicit equation; typically solved numerically (Newton-Raphson or Excel’s IRR function)
  • Standard interpretation: “Annualized return on capital invested”
  • Speaker notes: Set up the math cleanly. Students will know IRR intuitively; the goal is to make them careful about it.

16.4.3 Slide 6.3 — IRR: Numerical Example

  • Title: “A clean IRR computation”
  • Cash flows:
    • \(t = 0\): \(-\$100M\) (initial commitment, drawn over 3 years)
    • \(t = 4\): \(+\$25M\)
    • \(t = 6\): \(+\$50M\)
    • \(t = 8\): \(+\$75M\)
    • \(t = 10\): \(+\$30M\)
  • IRR: Solve \(-100 + 25(1+r)^{-4} + 50(1+r)^{-6} + 75(1+r)^{-8} + 30(1+r)^{-10} = 0\)
  • Solution: \(r \approx 14.2\%\)
  • Speaker notes: Walk through one Newton iteration on the board if time. Students should see how IRR is solved, not just trust Excel.

16.4.4 Slide 6.4 — Money-Weighted vs. Time-Weighted

  • Title: “Two different questions, two different metrics”
  • Money-weighted return (= IRR):
    • “What return did the capital earn?”
    • Heavily influenced by timing of large cash flows
    • Standard in private markets
  • Time-weighted return:
    • “What return did the manager generate?”
    • Independent of investor cash flow timing
    • Standard in public markets (mutual funds, hedge funds)
  • Why PE uses MWR (= IRR):
    • GP controls capital call timing
    • LP cares about return on dollars actually deployed
  • Speaker notes: This distinction matters for comparing PE returns to public benchmarks. PME (Session 7) is designed to bridge MWR-based PE returns with TWR-based public benchmarks.

16.4.5 Slide 6.5 — Worked: MWR vs. TWR Difference

  • Title: “When MWR and TWR diverge”
  • Setup: Two funds with identical underlying portfolio performance but different cash flow timing
  • Fund A: Calls capital early in fund life, distributes late
  • Fund B: Calls capital late, distributes early
  • Result: Same TWR (both managed the portfolio the same way), but Fund B has higher IRR (capital was deployed for less time on the larger amounts)
  • Speaker notes: This is a classic teaching example. Make sure students understand: the same investment skill can produce different IRRs depending on cash flow timing alone.

16.4.6 Slide 6.6 — IRR Bias #1: The Reinvestment Assumption

  • Title: “Bias 1: IRR assumes intermediate cash flows are reinvested at IRR itself”
  • Setup: “When IRR = 18%, the math implicitly assumes every distribution is reinvested at 18% for the remainder of the fund life”
  • Reality: “LPs typically receive distributions and either spend them or invest at much lower public market rates (~7-10% for diversified equities)”
  • Implication: “IRR systematically overstates the realized return when distributions are large and early”
  • Magnitude: “Can overstate by 2-5 percentage points for typical PE fund cash flow patterns”
  • Speaker notes: This is well-known but under-appreciated. The Modified IRR (MIRR) is a partial fix that uses a separate reinvestment rate, but isn’t standard in PE reporting.

16.4.7 Slide 6.7 — IRR Bias #2: Sign-Pattern Dependence

  • Title: “Bias 2: IRR is sensitive to the sign pattern of cash flows”
  • Standard case (-, 0, …, 0, +): Single IRR, well-defined
  • Mixed signs (-, +, -, +): Multiple IRRs can exist (Descartes’ rule of signs)
  • PE example: Continuation fund recapitalizations create intermediate negative cash flows (LP rolls over into new vehicle, takes some cash out) → multiple IRRs possible
  • Speaker notes: This is very common in modern PE with continuation funds and GP-led secondaries. Students will encounter this in practice.

16.4.8 Slide 6.8 — Multiple IRRs: A Concrete Example

  • Title: “When IRR isn’t unique”
  • Cash flows:
    • \(t = 0\): \(-\$100\)
    • \(t = 1\): \(+\$230\)
    • \(t = 2\): \(-\$132\)
  • NPV equation: \(-100 + 230/(1+r) - 132/(1+r)^2 = 0\)
  • Two solutions: \(r = 10\%\) and \(r = 20\%\)
  • Which is “the IRR”? Both, mathematically. Neither, economically.
  • Recommended approach: “Use NPV at a fixed discount rate; report IRR only with caveats about sign pattern”
  • Speaker notes: This blows students’ minds the first time. It’s textbook material but rarely emphasized.

16.4.9 Slide 6.9 — IRR Under Stochastic Discount Rates

  • Title: “Where IRR fundamentally breaks: when rates are stochastic”
  • Setup: “IRR by construction is a single number — one constant rate”
  • GE-LAV reality: Discount rate \(r(L_t)\) varies with liquidity state
  • The mismatch:
    • IRR averages out the rate variation
    • But the correct present value uses the full path of \(L_t\)
    • These give different answers (Jensen’s inequality again)
  • Speaker notes: Connect to Session 2’s Jensen discussion. IRR’s constant-rate assumption embeds the same convexity error as DCF’s constant-premium assumption.

16.4.10 Slide 6.10 — The Constant-Rate Fallacy

  • Title: “The Constant-Rate Fallacy”
  • Boxed statement: “Any metric that compresses time-varying discount rates into a single constant rate inherits the systematic bias of DCF.”
  • Implications:
    • IRR is biased
    • Money-weighted return is biased (same as IRR)
    • Hurdle rate comparisons are biased
    • Carried interest computations are biased
  • The cure: Liquidity-adjusted metrics (LA-IRR, LA-PME) — covered Session 7
  • Speaker notes: This is the bridge to Session 7. IRR’s problem is the same as DCF’s problem, restated.

16.4.11 Slide 6.11 — Empirical Magnitudes: How Wrong Is IRR?

  • Title: “How wrong is IRR in practice?”
  • Data: Pooled IRR of US PE buyout funds 1990–2024 (Preqin, Kaplan-Schoar)
  • Reported pooled IRR: ~12.5% per year
  • Phalippou (2009) adjustment for reinvestment assumption: ~9.5%
  • Stafford (2022) adjustment for valuation smoothing: ~7.5%
  • GE-LAV-adjusted estimate (LA-IRR): ~8.5% (intermediate)
  • Speaker notes: This is the number for students to remember. Reported PE returns are 3-5 percentage points overstated by methodology choices.

16.4.12 Slide 6.12 — IRR and Vintage-Year Effects

  • Title: “Why vintage matters more than skill”
  • Empirical finding: “Vintage year (when the fund started investing) explains 40-60% of variation in fund-level IRR across managers”
  • Implication: “Top-quartile vintage 2009 outperforms top-quartile vintage 2007 — not because managers were better, but because market entry conditions were”
  • Connection to GE-LAV: “Vintage effect is partly a liquidity state effect — funds starting in low-L states (high implied premium) benefit when L mean-reverts back to \(\bar{L}\)”
  • Speaker notes: Students often think of PE returns as manager-driven. Vintage effects dominate. GE-LAV explains why mathematically.

16.4.13 Slide 6.13 — IRR and Carry: Why Practitioners Defend IRR

  • Title: “Why IRR survives despite known flaws”
  • The institutional reasons:
    • Carried interest is computed on IRR — moving away from IRR moves the carry calculation
    • GP compensation is anchored to IRR hurdles — typically 8% preferred return
    • LP allocators benchmark on IRR — switching benchmarks is painful
    • No regulatory mandate to change — IRR is in IPEV, ILPA, and Solvency II guidance
  • Speaker notes: This is the political economy of IRR persistence. Students entering the industry should understand this.

16.4.14 Slide 6.14 — Proposed Solutions Overview (Tease for Session 7)

  • Title: “Three responses to the IRR problem”
  • Three columns:
    1. Reformed IRR: MIRR with explicit reinvestment rate; sign-pattern caveats; still constant-rate
    2. PME family: Compare to public benchmark — Kaplan-Schoar, Long-Nickels, Direct Alpha
    3. LA-IRR / LA-PME: Liquidity-adjusted version that handles stochastic rates correctly
  • Speaker notes: Session 7 covers all three families in detail.

16.4.15 Slide 6.15 — Summary

  • Title: “IRR: where it works, where it fails”
  • Works:
    • Simple sign pattern (single negative followed by positives)
    • Stable market conditions
    • Comparing across short hold periods
  • Fails:
    • Mixed sign patterns (continuation funds, recapitalizations)
    • Stochastic discount rates (always, in private markets)
    • Cross-vintage comparisons
    • Long-horizon assets where Jensen bias accumulates
  • Speaker notes: Honest summary. IRR isn’t useless; it’s incomplete.

16.5 Discussion Questions

  1. The GP-LP relationship has IRR baked into the carried interest formula. If GE-LAV becomes adopted methodology, does the carry formula need to change? How?
  2. Public mutual funds use TWR. Why has PE never moved to TWR despite known IRR flaws?
  3. Two PE funds have identical IRRs of 18% but very different cash flow timing patterns. How would you distinguish them from each other in selecting which to commit capital to?

16.6 Worked Numerical Example: When IRR Misleads

Setup: Two PE funds, both reporting IRR = 18%.

Fund A (front-loaded distributions): - \(t=0\): \(-\$100M\) - \(t=2\): \(+\$80M\) - \(t=4\): \(+\$40M\) - \(t=6\): \(+\$20M\) - IRR: 18% (capital out quickly)

Fund B (back-loaded distributions): - \(t=0\): \(-\$100M\) - \(t=2\): \(+\$0M\) - \(t=4\): \(+\$0M\) - \(t=8\): \(+\$200M\) - IRR: 18% (capital out only at end)

The reinvestment problem:

If LP can reinvest at 7% public market rate:

  • Fund A realized return: $80M from year 2 grows at 7% to \((80 \cdot 1.07^6) + (40 \cdot 1.07^4) + 20 = \$120M + \$52M + \$20M = \$192M\) at year 8 — total return 92% over 8 years = 8.4% annualized
  • Fund B realized return: $200M at year 8 — total return 100% = 9.05% annualized

Interpretation: Same reported IRR (18%), but Fund B’s realized return to the LP is higher because the IRR overstatement is larger for Fund A.

Lesson: Reported IRR can rank funds in the wrong order when reinvestment assumption matters.

16.7 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “Isn’t MIRR the standard fix?” MIRR exists but isn’t standard in PE reporting. ILPA and IPEV use plain IRR. Industry inertia.
  • “Can’t I just use the Modified Dietz return?” That’s a TWR computation. It tells you a different thing (manager skill) than IRR (return on dollars deployed). Both have a role.
  • “If IRR is so flawed, why do all PE funds report it?” Because LPs demand it, regulators sanction it, and carry computation depends on it. Network effects.
  • “What is Direct Alpha vs. Long-Nickels PME?” Tease — covered Session 7.

16.8 What to Expect Next Session

Session 7 dives into PME (Public Market Equivalent) and its variants. We’ll cover: - The Kaplan-Schoar PME and what it computes - Long-Nickels PME (with implicit reinvestment in the benchmark) - Direct Alpha (Gredil-Griffiths-Stucke) - LA-IRR and LA-PME — the GE-LAV-corrected versions - Which method to use in which context

Reading: Book Chapter 4, sections 4.5–4.10 (~12 pages).

- **Title:** "Three responses to the IRR problem"
- **Three columns:**
  1. **Reformed IRR:** MIRR with explicit reinvestment rate; sign-pattern caveats; still constant-rate
  2. **PME family:** Compare to public benchmark — Kaplan-Schoar, Long-Nickels, Direct Alpha
  3. **LA-IRR / LA-PME:** Liquidity-adjusted version that handles stochastic rates correctly
- **Speaker notes:** Session 7 covers all three families in detail.

### Slide 6.15 — Summary

- **Title:** "IRR: where it works, where it fails"
- **Works:**
  - Simple sign pattern (single negative followed by positives)
  - Stable market conditions
  - Comparing across short hold periods
- **Fails:**
  - Mixed sign patterns (continuation funds, recapitalizations)
  - Stochastic discount rates (always, in private markets)
  - Cross-vintage comparisons
  - Long-horizon assets where Jensen bias accumulates
- **Speaker notes:** Honest summary. IRR isn't useless; it's incomplete.

## Worked Numerical Example: When IRR Misleads

**Setup:** Two PE funds, both reporting IRR = 18%.

**Fund A (front-loaded distributions):**
- $t=0$: $-\$100M$
- $t=2$: $+\$80M$
- $t=4$: $+\$40M$
- $t=6$: $+\$20M$
- IRR: 18% (capital out quickly)

**Fund B (back-loaded distributions):**
- $t=0$: $-\$100M$
- $t=2$: $+\$0M$
- $t=4$: $+\$0M$
- $t=8$: $+\$200M$
- IRR: 18% (capital out only at end)

**The reinvestment problem:**

If LP can reinvest at 7% public market rate:

- **Fund A realized return:** $80M from year 2 grows at 7% to $(80 \cdot 1.07^6) + (40 \cdot 1.07^4) + 20 = \$120M + \$52M + \$20M = \$192M$ at year 8 — total return 92% over 8 years = **8.4% annualized**
- **Fund B realized return:** $200M at year 8 — total return 100% = **9.05% annualized**

**Interpretation:** Same reported IRR (18%), but Fund B's realized return to the LP is higher because the IRR overstatement is larger for Fund A.

**Lesson:** Reported IRR can rank funds in the wrong order when reinvestment assumption matters.

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"Isn't MIRR the standard fix?"** MIRR exists but isn't standard in PE reporting. ILPA and IPEV use plain IRR. Industry inertia.
- **"Can't I just use the Modified Dietz return?"** That's a TWR computation. It tells you a different thing (manager skill) than IRR (return on dollars deployed). Both have a role.
- **"If IRR is so flawed, why do all PE funds report it?"** Because LPs demand it, regulators sanction it, and carry computation depends on it. Network effects.
