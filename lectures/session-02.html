12  Session 2: The Three Structural Failures · LA-CAPM

Unit 1 — Why DCF Fails
Book Chapter 1 (sections 1.5–1.7)
Slide source New (~12 slides) + Class 9 leftovers
Slides needed ~12 new
Track Common core (both tracks)

12.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. State and explain the three structural failures of DCF for private market valuation.
  2. Articulate Jensen’s inequality at an intuition level — why \(E[f(X)] \neq f(E[X])\) when \(f\) is nonlinear — and apply it to the discount factor \(e^{-rT}\).
  3. Explain the McKean-Vlasov externality as a collective-action problem in liquidity provision.
  4. Position GE-LAV within the existing illiquidity premium literature (Amihud-Mendelson, Pastor-Stambaugh, Acharya-Pedersen).
  5. State the Liquidity-Adjusted CAPM (LA-CAPM) and how it generalizes standard CAPM.

12.2 Pre-Class Assignment

  • Read: Book Chapter 1, sections 1.5–1.7 (~15 pages)
  • Optional: Acharya & Pedersen (2005), “Asset Pricing with Liquidity Risk,” Journal of Financial Economics — abstract and conclusion only

12.4 Slide Content Briefs (New Slides Needed)

12.4.1 Slide 2.1 — Title and Recap

  • Title: “Three Structural Failures of DCF”
  • Visual: Carry forward the valuation hierarchy diagram from Session 1
  • One-line recap: “Last session: the illiquidity premium is the weak link. Today: three specific ways it breaks DCF.”

12.4.2 Slide 2.2 — Failure 1: The Premium Is Stochastic

  • Title: “Failure 1: A constant premium contradicts the data”
  • Layout: Headline + key evidence
  • Headline: “Secondary market discounts move 10× the magnitude of the assumed constant premium”
  • Evidence (table):
    • 2007 peak: secondary discount 3–8% → implied premium near floor
    • 2009 GFC: discount 35–50% → implied premium ~28%
    • 2020 COVID: discount 15–25% → implied premium ~18%
    • 2022 rate shock: discount 15–20% sustained → premium ~12%
    • “Long-run mean: ~3.5%. Volatility: σ ≈ 0.34”
  • Bottom-line: “If the assumption is 3.5% constant, the data is screaming ‘no.’”
  • Speaker notes: Establish the credibility of the data. Mention Preqin, Setter Capital, and broker reports.

12.4.3 Slide 2.3 — The OU Calibration (Numbers)

  • Title: “The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck calibration”
  • Equation (large, centered): \(dL_t = \kappa(\bar{L} - L_t)\,dt + \sigma_L\,dW_t\)
  • Calibrated parameters (boxed):
    • \(\kappa = 0.45\)/yr (mean reversion speed; half-life ≈ 18 months)
    • \(\bar{L} = 1.0\) (normalized long-run mean liquidity state)
    • \(\sigma_L = 0.32\) (diffusion)
    • Stationary std: \(\sqrt{\sigma_L^2/(2\kappa)} \approx 0.34\)
  • Visual: Simulated paths of OU process — 5 sample paths overlaid, all converging to \(\bar{L}\) but with stochastic fluctuation
  • Bottom-line: “This is the engine that drives everything in the course. Memorize the form.”
  • Speaker notes: Don’t derive yet — that’s Session 4. Just establish the process and the numbers. Connect κ to a half-life concept (similar to interest rate models — Vasicek).

12.4.4 Slide 2.4 — Failure 2: Jensen Convexity Bias (Setup)

  • Title: “Failure 2: Even if you got the mean right, the math fails”
  • Setup (text): “Suppose by miracle you knew \(E[r] = 7.5\%\). Is your DCF correct?”
  • Statement of Jensen’s inequality:
    • For any strictly convex function \(f\) and any non-degenerate random variable \(X\): \[E[f(X)] > f(E[X])\]
  • Visual: Standard Jensen’s inequality diagram — convex function with two points and a chord
  • Speaker notes: Many students will have seen Jensen in stats class but never in finance. Make the connection.

12.4.5 Slide 2.5 — Why Discount Factors Are Convex

  • Title: “The discount factor is strictly convex in the rate”
  • Math display:
    • \(f(r) = e^{-rT}\)
    • \(f'(r) = -T e^{-rT}\)
    • \(f''(r) = T^2 e^{-rT} > 0 \quad \forall r, T > 0\)
  • Implication: “\(E[e^{-rT}] > e^{-E[r]T}\) whenever \(r\) is genuinely stochastic”
  • Visual: Plot of \(e^{-rT}\) as function of \(r\) for \(T = 10\) years, with the chord vs. curve gap shaded
  • Speaker notes: Convexity gets larger with \(T\) — this is why long-horizon assets have larger Jensen bias.

12.4.6 Slide 2.6 — The Magnitude of the Jensen Bias

  • Title: “Jensen bias by asset class and horizon”
  • Layout: Table
  • Data:
Asset Class Horizon T Jensen Bias B(T)
5-year buyout 5 yr 0.8%
10-year growth equity 10 yr 1.7%
15-year secondaries 15 yr 2.5%
20-year infrastructure 20 yr 3.6%
  • Bottom-line: “Bias scales approximately linearly with \(T\) at calibrated parameters.”
  • Speaker notes: These are closed-form results derived in Chapter 18 of the book. Track 2 students will see the proof in Session 29.

12.4.7 Slide 2.7 — Worked Numerical Example: 20-Year Infrastructure

  • Title: “Worked: a $1B infrastructure fund”
  • Layout: Comparison table
  • Data:
    • DCF mark (constant 7.5% discount): $1,000M
    • LAV mark (Jensen-corrected, normal markets): $1,036M
    • Gap: $36M = 3.6% on Jensen bias alone
    • GE-LAV mark (normal markets): can be lower or higher depending on regime
    • GE-LAV mark (GFC depth): ~$300M
  • Speaker notes: This is the building block of the larger story. The Jensen bias is one of the three corrections. The McKean-Vlasov correction (Failure 3) is the bigger one, especially in stressed regimes.

12.4.8 Slide 2.8 — Failure 3: McKean-Vlasov Externality (Setup)

  • Title: “Failure 3: Liquidity is collective”
  • Thought experiment:
    • A single LP wants to exit a $50M PE position
    • Lazard quotes 5% discount on volume; the LP transacts; no aggregate effect
    • Now 100 large pension funds each want to exit $500M positions simultaneously
    • The total $50B is suddenly trying to find buyers
    • Equilibrium clearing price drops; discount widens to 25–40%
  • Punchline: “The clearing price depends on everyone’s behavior, not just yours.”
  • Speaker notes: This is the central conceptual move of GE-LAV. McKean-Vlasov is the mathematical formalization of this dependence — but the idea is intuitive.

12.4.9 Slide 2.9 — McKean-Vlasov: The Math Preview (Intuition Only)

  • Title: “How math formalizes ‘liquidity depends on everyone’”
  • Layout: Side-by-side
  • Left (standard SDE): \(dL_t = \kappa(\bar{L} - L_t)\,dt + \sigma_L\,dW_t\) — just one investor’s view
  • Right (McKean-Vlasov): \(dL_t = \kappa(\bar{L} - L_t)\,dt + \gamma \int \phi(L_t, l') \mu_t(dl')\,dt + \sigma_L\,dW_t\) — includes coupling to distribution \(\mu_t\) of all investors
  • Intuition: “\(\mu_t\) is the aggregate distribution of liquidity states across all investors. Your dynamics depend on it. Theirs depends on yours.”
  • Bottom-line: “This is a fixed point: behavior depends on premium; premium depends on behavior.”
  • Speaker notes: Do NOT derive. Just point at the structure. Track 2 students see this for real in Session 26.

12.4.10 Slide 2.10 — Why DCF Misses This Entirely

  • Title: “DCF cannot see the externality”
  • Bullets:
    • DCF takes the illiquidity premium as a fixed parameter
    • It cannot ask: “What premium will clear the market given other investors’ positions?”
    • It is, mathematically, a partial equilibrium calculation
    • The 2008 crisis revealed this: every DCF mark was wrong because they ignored coordinated exit behavior
  • Speaker notes: Connect to 2008. Many students will know the story of “the markdown that wasn’t.” This is the formalization.

12.4.11 Slide 2.11 — The Three Failures Together

  • Title: “Failures compound”
  • Layout: Three-row table
  • Rows:
    • Failure 1: stochastic premium → adds 2–4% premium volatility to base 3.5%
    • Failure 2: Jensen bias → adds 1–4% systematic positive bias in valuation
    • Failure 3: McKean-Vlasov externality → adds 5–20% in normal markets, 50–70% in crisis
  • Bottom-line: “Each failure compounds the others. GE-LAV is the framework that handles all three simultaneously.”
  • Speaker notes: This slide motivates the rest of the course. Each failure gets its own session(s).

12.4.12 Slide 2.12 — Liquidity-Adjusted CAPM

  • Title: “LA-CAPM: connecting to portfolio theory you know”
  • Layout: Standard CAPM → LA-CAPM transformation
  • Standard CAPM: \(E[r_i] - r_f = \beta_i (E[r_m] - r_f)\)
  • LA-CAPM (Acharya-Pedersen 2005): \(E[r_i] - r_f = E[c_i] + \beta_i^1 \cdot (\text{premium}_m) + \beta_i^2 \cdot (\text{premium}_{L}) - \beta_i^3 \cdot (\text{cov with } r_m, L)\)
  • Annotation: Four channels — (1) expected illiquidity cost; (2) standard market beta; (3) liquidity beta (sensitivity to aggregate liquidity); (4) cross-effects
  • GE-LAV connection: “GE-LAV refines the aggregate liquidity factor into a stochastic, equilibrium-determined process”
  • Speaker notes: This is the bridge from standard finance theory to GE-LAV. Students with prior asset pricing background will recognize the structure.

12.5 Discussion Questions

For the last 10 minutes if time permits:

  1. Which of the three failures do you think most practitioners are aware of? Which are they unaware of? Why?
  2. The Pastor-Stambaugh (2003) liquidity factor is widely used in factor models. Is it a satisfactory response to the three failures we covered today? What does it miss?
  3. Suppose you were the regulator overseeing private market fund NAVs. Which of the three failures would you prioritize fixing first? Why?

12.6 Worked Example: Quantifying Each Failure

Setup: A 10-year private equity fund, current NAV $100M.

Failure 1 (stochastic premium): If the OU stationary std is 0.34 and current state is at the long-run mean, the expected premium next year has standard deviation ~3.4%. A constant premium of 3.5% has 0 std. Failure: ignoring 3.4 percentage points of uncertainty.

Failure 2 (Jensen bias): For T = 10 and the calibrated parameters, \(B(10) \approx 1.7\%\). So LAV value ≈ \(101.7M\) vs. DCF \(100M\). Failure: \(1.7M\) systematic undervaluation in DCF.

Failure 3 (McKean-Vlasov): In normal markets, GE-LAV equilibrium rate exceeds DCF assumed rate by ~1%, so GE-LAV value ≈ \(98M\). In GFC depth, equilibrium rate is 32.3% vs. DCF 7.5% → GE-LAV value ≈ \(30M\).

Walk through: “Are the corrections all in the same direction? Sometimes (Failure 2 always positive). Sometimes they offset (Failure 2 positive, Failure 3 can be negative in crisis). GE-LAV handles them simultaneously and correctly.”

12.7 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “Jensen bias seems small. Why do we care?” Two reasons: (1) it’s always positive and systematic — every long-horizon asset is underpriced by DCF, every quarter, forever; (2) cumulative across the $13T market, the bias is ~$300B–$500B mispricing.
  • “McKean-Vlasov sounds like a panic-selling story. Isn’t that already in CAPM via correlations?” No — CAPM uses covariance (a linear concept). The MV externality is fixed-point structure: clearing price depends on coordinated behavior, not just correlation. Different math entirely.
  • “If LA-CAPM exists, why do we need GE-LAV?” LA-CAPM (Acharya-Pedersen) is a static factor model. GE-LAV is a continuous-time general equilibrium with endogenous, stochastic, collectively-determined premium dynamics. LA-CAPM is the “linearized snapshot”; GE-LAV is the dynamic system.

12.8 What to Expect Next Session

Session 3 dives deep into Failure 1: the secondary market evidence. We’ll examine the data carefully — what the secondary market quotes mean, who participates, the four regimes (2003–2007 boom, 2008–2010 GFC, 2011–2019 normalization, 2020–present multi-shock era), and what the discount-to-NAV ratio actually measures. This is the empirical session of Unit 1.

Reading: Book Chapter 2, all sections (~30 pages). Pay particular attention to sections 2.3–2.6.

## Worked Example: Quantifying Each Failure

**Setup:** A 10-year private equity fund, current NAV $100M.

**Failure 1 (stochastic premium):** If the OU stationary std is 0.34 and current state is at the long-run mean, the *expected* premium next year has standard deviation ~3.4%. A constant premium of 3.5% has 0 std. Failure: ignoring 3.4 percentage points of uncertainty.

**Failure 2 (Jensen bias):** For T = 10 and the calibrated parameters, $B(10) \approx 1.7\%$. So LAV value ≈ $101.7M$ vs. DCF $100M$. **Failure: $1.7M$ systematic undervaluation in DCF.**

**Failure 3 (McKean-Vlasov):** In normal markets, GE-LAV equilibrium rate exceeds DCF assumed rate by ~1%, so GE-LAV value ≈ $98M$. In GFC depth, equilibrium rate is 32.3% vs. DCF 7.5% → GE-LAV value ≈ $30M$.

**Walk through:** "Are the corrections all in the same direction? Sometimes (Failure 2 always positive). Sometimes they offset (Failure 2 positive, Failure 3 can be negative in crisis). GE-LAV handles them simultaneously and correctly."

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"Jensen bias seems small. Why do we care?"** Two reasons: (1) it's *always positive and systematic* — every long-horizon asset is underpriced by DCF, every quarter, forever; (2) cumulative across the $13T market, the bias is ~$300B–$500B mispricing.
- **"McKean-Vlasov sounds like a panic-selling story. Isn't that already in CAPM via correlations?"** No — CAPM uses *covariance* (a linear concept). The MV externality is *fixed-point structure*: clearing price depends on coordinated behavior, not just correlation. Different math entirely.
- **"If LA-CAPM exists, why do we need GE-LAV?"** LA-CAPM (Acharya-Pedersen) is a static factor model. GE-LAV is a *continuous-time general equilibrium* with endogenous, stochastic, collectively-determined premium dynamics. LA-CAPM is the "linearized snapshot"; GE-LAV is the dynamic system.
