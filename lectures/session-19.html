ImportantReading Notes for Unit 4 (Sessions 19–24)

This unit teaches the conceptual content of Part 2 of the book — what the math means, what it does, and why it works — without proofs or detailed derivations. The goal is for all students to leave Unit 4 with a working intuition about the GE-LAV machinery.

Track 1 students are assessed only on conceptual understanding. PS3 includes intuition-level questions for Track 1.

Track 2 students will revisit each topic in Sessions 25–29 with full mathematical rigor — proofs, derivations, numerical implementations. PS3 for Track 2 includes mathematical work.

If you find Unit 4 too easy, you’ll love Sessions 25–29. If you find it too hard, the framework’s applications (Unit 3 and Session 25+ Track 1) don’t require this level of detail.

27.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. Explain Brownian motion in plain language — what it is, why it’s the canonical model of randomness in continuous time.
  2. State the four key properties of Brownian motion (continuity, independent increments, normal distribution of increments, scale).
  3. State Itô’s lemma conceptually — the “chain rule” of stochastic calculus — and why it has an extra term compared to ordinary calculus.
  4. Re-derive (informally) the OU process as a special case of an Itô SDE with mean-reverting drift.
  5. Recognize when Itô calculus is the right tool (continuous random processes) and when it isn’t (jumps, discrete states).

27.4.3 Slide 19.3 — Brownian Motion: Visual First

  • Title: “Brownian motion: what it looks like”
  • Visual: 5 simulated Brownian motion paths overlaid on the same time axis (0 to 10)
  • Annotations:
    • All start at 0
    • All wander in different directions
    • All are continuous (no jumps)
    • All look “fractal” — zoom in, still random
  • Speaker notes: Start with the picture. Words come after.

27.4.4 Slide 19.4 — Brownian Motion: Four Properties

  • Title: “Brownian motion: the canonical continuous random process”
  • Four defining properties:
    1. Starts at zero: \(W_0 = 0\)
    2. Continuous paths: No jumps; you can draw the path without lifting the pen
    3. Independent increments: What happens in \([t, t+\Delta]\) is independent of what happened before \(t\)
    4. Normally distributed increments: \(W_{t+\Delta} - W_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Delta)\) — variance scales with time
  • Origin: “Discovered by botanist Robert Brown (1827) watching pollen grains in water. Formalized by Wiener (1923).”
  • Speaker notes: Connect to physical Brownian motion. Same math.

27.4.5 Slide 19.5 — Why Brownian Motion for Finance?

  • Title: “Why Brownian motion is the right model for randomness in finance”
  • Three reasons:
    1. Many small independent shocks → normal distribution (Central Limit Theorem)
    2. Continuous trading limit: As \(\Delta \to 0\) in discrete random walks, you get Brownian motion
    3. Tractability: Closed-form solutions for many problems
  • Caveats:
    • Real financial returns have fat tails (Brownian motion underestimates extremes)
    • Real returns have jumps (Brownian motion is purely continuous)
    • For most pricing problems, Brownian motion is “good enough” as a baseline; corrections handle the rest
  • Speaker notes: Honest about limitations. The framework starts here but doesn’t stay here.

27.4.6 Slide 19.6 — Itô SDEs: The Workhorse

  • Title: “Itô SDEs: stochastic ordinary differential equations”
  • General form: \(dX_t = \mu(X_t, t)\,dt + \sigma(X_t, t)\,dW_t\)
  • Plain English translation:
    • \(X_t\) changes over time according to two terms
    • Drift term \(\mu(X_t, t)\,dt\): deterministic, like a regular ODE
    • Diffusion term \(\sigma(X_t, t)\,dW_t\): random, scaled by Brownian motion increment
  • Examples in finance:
    • Black-Scholes (geometric BM): \(dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t\)
    • Vasicek (interest rate): \(dr_t = a(b - r_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t\) — mean reversion
    • GE-LAV (liquidity): \(dL_t = \kappa(\bar{L} - L_t)\,dt + \sigma_L\,dW_t\) — same as Vasicek!
  • Speaker notes: Show students they already know this structure. GE-LAV is one step beyond Vasicek.

27.4.7 Slide 19.7 — Itô’s Lemma: The Concept

  • Title: “Itô’s lemma: the chain rule of stochastic calculus”
  • Ordinary calculus chain rule: If \(f(X_t)\) and \(X_t\) varies, then \(df = f'(X_t) dX_t\)
  • Itô’s lemma: If \(X_t\) is an Itô process and \(f\) is a smooth function, then:
    • \(df(X_t) = f'(X_t) dX_t + \frac{1}{2} f''(X_t) \sigma^2(X_t, t) dt\)
    • Extra term: \(\frac{1}{2} f''(X_t) \sigma^2 dt\) — the Itô correction
  • Why the extra term: The Brownian motion increment \(dW_t\) has variance \(dt\), so \((dW_t)^2 = dt\) at order 1. Squaring matters when \(f\) is curved.
  • Speaker notes: This is the one technical concept of the session. Don’t gloss over the Itô correction. Tie back to Jensen’s inequality.

27.4.8 Slide 19.8 — Itô’s Lemma in Pictures

  • Title: “Why the Itô correction term”
  • Visual: Two panels side-by-side
  • Left panel (deterministic): Smooth function \(f(x)\); small change \(\Delta x\) → small change \(\Delta f = f'(x) \Delta x\). Standard linearization.
  • Right panel (stochastic): Same function; random \(\Delta x \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 \Delta t)\); expected \(\Delta f\) has two terms:
    • \(f'(x) E[\Delta x] = 0\) (Brownian increment has zero mean)
    • \(\frac{1}{2} f''(x) \text{Var}(\Delta x) = \frac{1}{2} f''(x) \sigma^2 \Delta t\)
  • Bottom-line: “The Itô term is the second-order curvature correction. It’s identical in spirit to the Jensen convexity correction we saw in Session 2.”
  • Speaker notes: Connect to Jensen. This is the same math applied differently.

27.4.9 Slide 19.9 — Itô-Jensen Connection

  • Title: “The Itô-Jensen connection”
  • Reminder: Jensen’s inequality says \(E[f(X)] > f(E[X])\) when \(f\) is strictly convex
  • Itô’s lemma says: For an Itô process \(X_t\), the expected value of \(f(X_t)\) has an extra term proportional to \(f''(X_t) \sigma^2 dt\) — exactly the convexity correction
  • Bottom-line: “Jensen bias = integrated Itô correction over the holding period”
  • In GE-LAV: “The Jensen bias formula \(B(T) = (\pi_2/2) \int_0^T \text{Var}(L_t) E[C_t] e^{-r_f t} dt\) comes directly from this connection”
  • Speaker notes: This slide unifies what students learned in Session 2 with what they’re learning today. Light bulb moment for many.

27.4.10 Slide 19.10 — Back to the OU Process

  • Title: “The OU process as a special case”
  • OU SDE: \(dL_t = \kappa(\bar{L} - L_t)\,dt + \sigma_L\,dW_t\)
  • Identification with general Itô form:
    • Drift: \(\mu(L) = \kappa(\bar{L} - L)\) (linear in \(L\), pointing toward \(\bar{L}\))
    • Diffusion: \(\sigma(L) = \sigma_L\) (constant)
  • Solvable in closed form because both drift and diffusion are simple
  • Result (stated, not derived): Stationary distribution \(\mathcal{N}(\bar{L}, \sigma^2/(2\kappa))\) — follows from applying Itô’s lemma to the change-of-variable
  • Speaker notes: Bridge to Session 4 material. Now students know why OU has the stationary distribution they learned earlier.

27.4.11 Slide 19.11 — Computing E[L_t] Over Time

  • Title: “Where does \(L_t\) go over time?”
  • Conditional expectation (stated): \(E[L_t | L_0] = \bar{L} + (L_0 - \bar{L}) e^{-\kappa t}\)
  • Plain English: “From any starting point \(L_0\), the expected value decays exponentially toward \(\bar{L}\) at rate \(\kappa\)”
  • Half-life again: \(\ln(2)/\kappa\)
  • Conditional variance: \(\text{Var}[L_t | L_0] = \dfrac{\sigma_L^2}{2\kappa}(1 - e^{-2\kappa t})\)
    • At \(t = 0\): variance = 0 (we know the starting point)
    • As \(t \to \infty\): variance approaches stationary \(\sigma_L^2/(2\kappa)\)
  • Speaker notes: This is the forecasting formula. Used implicitly in every exit boundary computation.

27.4.12 Slide 19.12 — What Itô Can’t Do

  • Title: “Limitations of pure Itô calculus”
  • Three things Itô doesn’t handle:
    1. Jumps: Real markets have discrete jumps (March 2020). Itô + Brownian motion is purely continuous.
      • Extension: Itô-Lévy calculus, jump-diffusion models
    2. Fat tails: Brownian increments are Gaussian. Real returns have power-law tails.
      • Extension: Stable processes, Variance Gamma, etc.
    3. Non-Markov dynamics: Itô assumes \(dX_t\) depends only on current \(X_t\). Real markets have memory.
      • Extension: Fractional Brownian motion, rough volatility
  • GE-LAV extensions: Book Ch. 13 introduces Lévy jumps to the OU process
  • Speaker notes: Honesty about model limitations.

27.4.13 Slide 19.13 — Where We Are in the Course

  • What we just did: Built intuition for the stochastic process layer of GE-LAV
  • Next: Session 20 — Stochastic control and the HJB equation (the optimal decision layer)
  • Speaker notes: Frame this as the first of six bridge sessions. Each builds on the previous.

27.5 Discussion Questions

  1. The Itô correction term \(\frac{1}{2} f''(X_t) \sigma^2 dt\) has the same mathematical form as the Jensen convexity correction \(\frac{1}{2} f''(X) \text{Var}(X)\). Why are these two expressions the same? What’s the deep connection?
  2. The OU process has \(\kappa = 0.45\)/yr, implying half-life of liquidity shocks ~18 months. Many practitioners would estimate half-life closer to 6-12 months (faster mean reversion). What evidence would resolve this?
  3. Real liquidity has jumps (March 2020). The GE-LAV core uses pure Itô (no jumps). For the purposes of valuation, is this approximation acceptable? When does it break?

27.6 Worked Example: Applying Itô’s Lemma

Setup: Let \(L_t\) follow OU with \(\kappa = 0.45\), \(\sigma_L = 0.32\), \(\bar{L} = 1.0\). Compute the expected value and variance of \(f(L_t) = L_t^2\) at \(t = 5\), given \(L_0 = 0\).

Step 1: Apply Itô’s lemma to \(f(L) = L^2\)

\(f'(L) = 2L\), \(f''(L) = 2\)

\(df(L_t) = 2L_t dL_t + \frac{1}{2}(2)\sigma_L^2 dt = 2L_t [\kappa(\bar{L} - L_t)dt + \sigma_L dW_t] + \sigma_L^2 dt\)

Take expectations (Brownian term vanishes in expectation): \(\frac{d}{dt} E[L_t^2] = 2\kappa \bar{L} E[L_t] - 2\kappa E[L_t^2] + \sigma_L^2\)

Step 2: We need \(E[L_t]\) first

\(E[L_t | L_0 = 0] = \bar{L}(1 - e^{-\kappa t}) = 1.0 \cdot (1 - e^{-2.25}) = 1.0 \cdot 0.894 = 0.894\)

So at \(t = 5\), \(E[L_5] = 0.894\) (close to long-run mean).

Step 3: Variance

\(\text{Var}[L_t | L_0] = \frac{\sigma_L^2}{2\kappa}(1 - e^{-2\kappa t}) = \frac{0.1024}{0.9}(1 - e^{-4.5}) = 0.1138 \cdot 0.989 = 0.1126\)

Step 4: \(E[L_t^2]\)

\(E[L_t^2] = \text{Var}[L_t] + (E[L_t])^2 = 0.1126 + 0.894^2 = 0.1126 + 0.799 = 0.912\)

Interpretation: Starting at \(L_0 = 0\) (stressed but not crisis), 5 years later we expect \(E[L] \approx 0.89\) (close to long-run mean) with variance \(\approx 0.11\) (close to stationary). The \(E[L^2]\) value \(\approx 0.91\) matters for the quadratic premium function \(\pi(L)\).

Teaching note: Don’t expect students to do this calculation cold. Walk through it. The point is to see how Itô’s lemma turns SDEs into ODE-like equations for moments.

27.7 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “Why is \((dW_t)^2 = dt\)? That makes no sense.” It’s a notational shorthand for the variance of the Brownian increment. The variance accumulates at rate \(dt\), so squaring the random increment behaves like \(dt\) in expectation.
  • “If the Itô correction term is always there, isn’t every expectation biased?” Yes — every expectation of a nonlinear function of a stochastic process has an Itô correction. For linear functions, the correction vanishes (because \(f'' = 0\)).
  • “Why do we use Brownian motion instead of just a normal random variable?” Brownian motion is the continuous-time version. It lets us write SDEs that hold at every instant, not just at discrete times.
  • “How is this different from a random walk?” Random walk is discrete time. Brownian motion is the continuous-time limit. As \(\Delta t \to 0\) in a scaled random walk, you converge to Brownian motion.

  - **GE-LAV (liquidity):** $dL_t = \kappa(\bar{L} - L_t)\,dt + \sigma_L\,dW_t$ — same as Vasicek!
- **Speaker notes:** Show students they already know this structure. GE-LAV is *one step* beyond Vasicek.

### Slide 19.7 — Itô's Lemma: The Concept

- **Title:** "Itô's lemma: the chain rule of stochastic calculus"
- **Ordinary calculus chain rule:** If $f(X_t)$ and $X_t$ varies, then $df = f'(X_t) dX_t$
- **Itô's lemma:** If $X_t$ is an Itô process and $f$ is a smooth function, then:
  - $df(X_t) = f'(X_t) dX_t + \frac{1}{2} f''(X_t) \sigma^2(X_t, t) dt$
  - **Extra term:** $\frac{1}{2} f''(X_t) \sigma^2 dt$ — the Itô correction
- **Why the extra term:** The Brownian motion increment $dW_t$ has variance $dt$, so $(dW_t)^2 = dt$ at order 1. Squaring matters when $f$ is curved.
- **Speaker notes:** This is the *one technical concept* of the session. Don't gloss over the Itô correction. Tie back to Jensen's inequality.

### Slide 19.8 — Itô's Lemma in Pictures

- **Title:** "Why the Itô correction term"
- **Visual:** Two panels side-by-side
- **Left panel (deterministic):** Smooth function $f(x)$; small change $\Delta x$ → small change $\Delta f = f'(x) \Delta x$. Standard linearization.
- **Right panel (stochastic):** Same function; random $\Delta x \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 \Delta t)$; expected $\Delta f$ has two terms:
  - $f'(x) E[\Delta x] = 0$ (Brownian increment has zero mean)
  - $\frac{1}{2} f''(x) \text{Var}(\Delta x) = \frac{1}{2} f''(x) \sigma^2 \Delta t$
- **Bottom-line:** "The Itô term is the second-order curvature correction. It's identical in spirit to the Jensen convexity correction we saw in Session 2."
- **Speaker notes:** Connect to Jensen. This is *the same math* applied differently.

### Slide 19.9 — Itô-Jensen Connection

- **Title:** "The Itô-Jensen connection"
- **Reminder:** Jensen's inequality says $E[f(X)] > f(E[X])$ when $f$ is strictly convex
- **Itô's lemma says:** For an Itô process $X_t$, the expected value of $f(X_t)$ has an *extra term* proportional to $f''(X_t) \sigma^2 dt$ — exactly the convexity correction
- **Bottom-line:** "Jensen bias = integrated Itô correction over the holding period"
- **In GE-LAV:** "The Jensen bias formula $B(T) = (\pi_2/2) \int_0^T \text{Var}(L_t) E[C_t] e^{-r_f t} dt$ comes directly from this connection"
- **Speaker notes:** This slide unifies what students learned in Session 2 with what they're learning today. Light bulb moment for many.

### Slide 19.10 — Back to the OU Process

- **Title:** "The OU process as a special case"
- **OU SDE:** $dL_t = \kappa(\bar{L} - L_t)\,dt + \sigma_L\,dW_t$
- **Identification with general Itô form:**
  - Drift: $\mu(L) = \kappa(\bar{L} - L)$ (linear in $L$, pointing toward $\bar{L}$)
  - Diffusion: $\sigma(L) = \sigma_L$ (constant)
- **Solvable in closed form** because both drift and diffusion are simple
- **Result (stated, not derived):** Stationary distribution $\mathcal{N}(\bar{L}, \sigma^2/(2\kappa))$ — *follows from applying Itô's lemma to the change-of-variable*
- **Speaker notes:** Bridge to Session 4 material. Now students know *why* OU has the stationary distribution they learned earlier.

### Slide 19.11 — Computing E[L_t] Over Time

- **Title:** "Where does $L_t$ go over time?"
- **Conditional expectation (stated):** $E[L_t | L_0] = \bar{L} + (L_0 - \bar{L}) e^{-\kappa t}$
- **Plain English:** "From any starting point $L_0$, the expected value decays exponentially toward $\bar{L}$ at rate $\kappa$"
- **Half-life again:** $\ln(2)/\kappa$
- **Conditional variance:** $\text{Var}[L_t | L_0] = \dfrac{\sigma_L^2}{2\kappa}(1 - e^{-2\kappa t})$
  - At $t = 0$: variance = 0 (we know the starting point)
  - As $t \to \infty$: variance approaches stationary $\sigma_L^2/(2\kappa)$
- **Speaker notes:** This is the *forecasting* formula. Used implicitly in every exit boundary computation.

### Slide 19.12 — What Itô Can't Do

- **Title:** "Limitations of pure Itô calculus"
- **Three things Itô doesn't handle:**
  1. **Jumps:** Real markets have discrete jumps (March 2020). Itô + Brownian motion is purely continuous.
     - **Extension:** Itô-Lévy calculus, jump-diffusion models
  2. **Fat tails:** Brownian increments are Gaussian. Real returns have power-law tails.
     - **Extension:** Stable processes, Variance Gamma, etc.
  3. **Non-Markov dynamics:** Itô assumes $dX_t$ depends only on current $X_t$. Real markets have memory.
     - **Extension:** Fractional Brownian motion, rough volatility
- **GE-LAV extensions:** Book Ch. 13 introduces Lévy jumps to the OU process
- **Speaker notes:** Honesty about model limitations.

## Worked Example: Applying Itô's Lemma

**Setup:** Let $L_t$ follow OU with $\kappa = 0.45$, $\sigma_L = 0.32$, $\bar{L} = 1.0$. Compute the expected value and variance of $f(L_t) = L_t^2$ at $t = 5$, given $L_0 = 0$.

**Step 1: Apply Itô's lemma to $f(L) = L^2$**

$f'(L) = 2L$, $f''(L) = 2$

$df(L_t) = 2L_t dL_t + \frac{1}{2}(2)\sigma_L^2 dt = 2L_t [\kappa(\bar{L} - L_t)dt + \sigma_L dW_t] + \sigma_L^2 dt$

Take expectations (Brownian term vanishes in expectation):
$\frac{d}{dt} E[L_t^2] = 2\kappa \bar{L} E[L_t] - 2\kappa E[L_t^2] + \sigma_L^2$

**Step 2: We need $E[L_t]$ first**

$E[L_t | L_0 = 0] = \bar{L}(1 - e^{-\kappa t}) = 1.0 \cdot (1 - e^{-2.25}) = 1.0 \cdot 0.894 = 0.894$

So at $t = 5$, $E[L_5] = 0.894$ (close to long-run mean).

**Step 3: Variance**

$\text{Var}[L_t | L_0] = \frac{\sigma_L^2}{2\kappa}(1 - e^{-2\kappa t}) = \frac{0.1024}{0.9}(1 - e^{-4.5}) = 0.1138 \cdot 0.989 = 0.1126$

**Step 4: $E[L_t^2]$**

$E[L_t^2] = \text{Var}[L_t] + (E[L_t])^2 = 0.1126 + 0.894^2 = 0.1126 + 0.799 = 0.912$

**Interpretation:** Starting at $L_0 = 0$ (stressed but not crisis), 5 years later we expect $E[L] \approx 0.89$ (close to long-run mean) with variance $\approx 0.11$ (close to stationary). The $E[L^2]$ value $\approx 0.91$ matters for the *quadratic* premium function $\pi(L)$.

**Teaching note:** Don't expect students to do this calculation cold. Walk through it. The point is to *see* how Itô's lemma turns SDEs into ODE-like equations for moments.

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"Why is $(dW_t)^2 = dt$? That makes no sense."** It's a notational shorthand for the variance of the Brownian increment. The variance accumulates at rate $dt$, so squaring the random increment behaves like $dt$ in expectation.
- **"If the Itô correction term is always there, isn't every expectation biased?"** Yes — every expectation of a *nonlinear function* of a stochastic process has an Itô correction. For linear functions, the correction vanishes (because $f'' = 0$).
- **"Why do we use Brownian motion instead of just a normal random variable?"** Brownian motion is the *continuous-time* version. It lets us write SDEs that hold at every instant, not just at discrete times.
- **"How is this different from a random walk?"** Random walk is discrete time. Brownian motion is the continuous-time limit. As $\Delta t \to 0$ in a scaled random walk, you converge to Brownian motion.
