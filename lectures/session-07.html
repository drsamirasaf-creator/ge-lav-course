16  Session 7: PME · LA-IRR · LA-PME

Unit 2 — Measurement and Theory
Book Chapter 4 (sections 4.5–4.10)
Slide source All new
Slides needed ~15 new
Track Common core (both tracks)

16.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. Compute the Kaplan-Schoar PME for a given fund’s cash flows against a chosen public benchmark.
  2. Distinguish Kaplan-Schoar PME, Long-Nickels PME, and Direct Alpha — and state when each is appropriate.
  3. Articulate why all standard PME variants share the same constant-rate weakness as DCF and IRR.
  4. State the LA-IRR and LA-PME formulas and explain what corrections they apply.
  5. Apply LA-PME to a worked example and interpret the result against a standard PME.

16.2 Pre-Class Assignment

  • Read: Book Chapter 4, sections 4.5–4.10 (~12 pages)
  • Optional: Kaplan & Schoar (2005), “Private Equity Performance: Returns, Persistence, and Capital Flows,” Journal of Finance; Gredil, Griffiths, & Stucke (2014), “Benchmarking Private Equity: The Direct Alpha Method”

16.4 Slide Content Briefs (New Slides Needed)

16.4.1 Slide 7.1 — Title

  • Title: “PME, LA-IRR, LA-PME”
  • Tag line: “Comparing private to public, correctly”

16.4.2 Slide 7.2 — The PME Question

  • Title: “What does PME try to answer?”
  • The question: “If I had invested the same cash flows in the S&P 500 instead of in this PE fund, how would I have done?”
  • Why this matters:
    • Allocators need benchmarks
    • Carry hurdles are absolute (8% pref); PME is relative (vs. market)
    • Academic and regulatory work needs cross-asset-class comparison
  • Speaker notes: Frame PME as the response to IRR’s weaknesses. PME doesn’t fix IRR; it gives a complementary lens.

16.4.3 Slide 7.3 — Kaplan-Schoar PME (Definition)

  • Title: “Kaplan-Schoar (KS) PME”
  • Formula: \(PME_{KS} = \dfrac{\sum_t D_t / B_t}{\sum_t C_t / B_t}\)
  • Where:
    • \(D_t\) = distributions in period \(t\)
    • \(C_t\) = capital calls in period \(t\)
    • \(B_t\) = public benchmark cumulative return from inception to period \(t\)
  • Interpretation:
    • \(PME_{KS} > 1\): PE fund outperformed the benchmark
    • \(PME_{KS} = 1\): PE fund matched the benchmark
    • \(PME_{KS} < 1\): PE fund underperformed
  • Speaker notes: Most-used PME in practice. Simple, intuitive, easy to compute.

16.4.4 Slide 7.4 — Kaplan-Schoar: Worked Example

  • Title: “KS PME: a worked example”
  • Setup: 10-year PE fund vs. S&P 500
  • Cash flows and benchmark levels:
Year Call (\(M) | Distribution (\)M) S&P Index Level
0 30 0 100
1 40 0 108
2 30 0 118
5 0 80 145
8 0 90 180
10 0 60 200
  • Numerator (sum of distributions discounted by benchmark): \(80/(145/100) + 90/(180/100) + 60/(200/100) = 55.2 + 50.0 + 30.0 = 135.2\)
  • Denominator (sum of calls discounted by benchmark): \(30/1.00 + 40/(108/100) + 30/(118/100) = 30 + 37.0 + 25.4 = 92.4\)
  • KS PME: \(135.2 / 92.4 = 1.46\) → PE fund outperformed S&P 500 by 46%

16.4.5 Slide 7.5 — Long-Nickels PME (Definition)

  • Title: “Long-Nickels (LN) PME”
  • Idea: “Build a hypothetical portfolio that mirrors the PE fund’s cash flow timing in the public benchmark, then compute its IRR”
  • Mechanism:
    • When PE calls \(X\) from LP, hypothetical portfolio buys \(X\) of benchmark
    • When PE distributes \(Y\) to LP, hypothetical portfolio sells \(Y\) of benchmark
    • Track the implied “synthetic public fund” NAV over time
    • Compute its IRR; compare to PE fund’s IRR
  • Output: Two IRRs (PE fund vs. synthetic public); difference is the alpha
  • Speaker notes: More sophisticated than KS PME. Handles the “what would the public benchmark have done with my exact cash flow pattern” question directly.

16.4.6 Slide 7.6 — KS vs. LN: When They Diverge

  • Title: “KS and LN: same idea, different formulations”
  • Both compare PE returns to public benchmark with same cash flow timing
  • KS gives a multiple (1.46×); LN gives an IRR-style annualized rate
  • They can disagree when:
    • Cash flow patterns are unusual (continuation funds, recap distributions)
    • Long fund lives where reinvestment matters
    • Multiple IRRs in the synthetic fund (rare but possible)
  • Speaker notes: For most simple cases, KS and LN agree. For corner cases, LN is more nuanced.

16.4.7 Slide 7.7 — Direct Alpha (Gredil-Griffiths-Stucke 2014)

  • Title: “Direct Alpha: a cleaner formulation”
  • Idea: “Compute the constant excess discount rate \(\alpha\) such that adjusted PE cash flows have zero NPV against the benchmark”
  • Formula: Find \(\alpha\) solving \(\sum_t (D_t - C_t) \cdot e^{-\alpha t} / B_t = 0\)
  • Output: A single number \(\alpha\) interpreted as the “alpha vs. the benchmark”
  • Advantages over KS / LN:
    • Returns an interpretable annualized rate
    • Handles complex cash flow patterns cleanly
    • More robust to vintage-year effects
  • Speaker notes: Direct Alpha is the academic favorite. Practitioners still mostly use KS.

16.4.8 Slide 7.8 — The Constant-Rate Weakness (PME’s Achilles Heel)

  • Title: “Why all standard PMEs share IRR’s weakness”
  • The common assumption:
    • KS, LN, and Direct Alpha all treat the public benchmark as the risk-adjusted reference
    • They embed the implicit assumption that any extra return beyond the benchmark is fair compensation for liquidity, vintage, and selection
    • This is a constant-rate assumption about the illiquidity premium
  • The mismatch:
    • GE-LAV says the illiquidity premium is stochastic and regime-dependent
    • PMEs cannot see this — they compress everything into a single number
  • Speaker notes: This is the conceptual handoff to LA-PME.

16.4.9 Slide 7.9 — LA-IRR: Liquidity-Adjusted IRR (Definition)

  • Title: “LA-IRR: GE-LAV’s correction to IRR”
  • The idea: “Compute IRR using a stochastic discount rate path \(r(L_t)\) instead of a constant rate”
  • Mechanics:
    • Calibrate \(L_t\) path from secondary market data (or simulate from OU)
    • Compute period-by-period discount factors \(D_t = \exp(-\int_0^t r(L_s) ds)\)
    • Find the effective single-rate equivalent that matches the cash flow PV
  • Output: A liquidity-adjusted single-rate summary, comparable to standard IRR but corrected
  • Magnitude: “For typical PE fund cash flow patterns, LA-IRR is typically 2–4 percentage points below reported IRR”
  • Speaker notes: This is the measurement counterpart to LAV’s valuation correction. Same underlying logic.

16.4.10 Slide 7.10 — LA-PME: Liquidity-Adjusted PME

  • Title: “LA-PME: GE-LAV’s correction to PME”
  • The idea: “Use the public benchmark adjusted for the contemporaneous liquidity state as the comparison”
  • Mechanics:
    • At each period, compute the benchmark return adjusted for the implied illiquidity premium gap
    • \(B_t^{LA} = B_t \cdot \exp(\int_0^t \pi(L_s) ds)\)
    • Apply KS / LN / Direct Alpha methodology to \(B_t^{LA}\) instead of raw \(B_t\)
  • Output: PME that compares PE to a “public benchmark plus correctly-priced liquidity premium”
  • Magnitude: “Typical PE fund LA-PME is 5-15% below reported KS PME”
  • Speaker notes: This is a meaningful magnitude. Allocators caring about absolute outperformance should use LA-PME.

16.4.11 Slide 7.11 — Comparing LA-PME to Standard PME: Worked Example

  • Title: “Worked: 2010-vintage buyout fund”
  • Setup:
    • Reported KS PME (vs. S&P 500): 1.32 — “32% outperformance”
    • Adjusted for L_t path 2010-2024 (mostly Regimes 3, 4): LA-PME = 1.18 — “18% outperformance”
  • Decomposition:
    • Of the 32 percentage points of reported KS outperformance, ~14 points are compensation for time-varying illiquidity premium (not alpha)
    • True alpha vs. liquidity-adjusted benchmark: ~18 percentage points
  • Speaker notes: Even with adjustment, this fund outperformed. But the magnitude of “true alpha” is materially smaller than reported.

16.4.12 Slide 7.12 — Vintage-Year Liquidity Effects

  • Title: “Why vintage matters: liquidity state at entry”
  • Setup:
    • Vintage 2009: entered at \(L_0 \approx -1.5\) (crisis depth); mean reversion drove L back to \(\bar{L}\)
    • Vintage 2007: entered at \(L_0 \approx +0.5\) (boom); mean reversion drove L down toward \(\bar{L}\)
  • Result:
    • 2009 vintage benefited from positive mean reversion in L
    • 2007 vintage suffered from negative mean reversion in L
  • GE-LAV decomposition: “Vintage-year IRR differences are partly a liquidity state effect, which GE-LAV captures explicitly”
  • Speaker notes: This is the explanation for the strong vintage-year pattern in PE returns. Standard models can’t decompose it; GE-LAV can.

16.4.13 Slide 7.13 — Practical: Which Metric for Which Purpose

  • Title: “When to use which metric”
  • Decision table:
Purpose Best Metric
Carried interest computation Reported IRR (constrained by LPA)
Cross-fund manager skill comparison KS PME or Direct Alpha
LP allocation decisions LA-IRR + LA-PME
Regulatory reporting (Solvency II) LA-PME (under GE-LAV adoption)
Academic research on PE returns Direct Alpha + LA-PME
Vintage-year analysis LA-IRR (controls for liquidity state)
  • Speaker notes: Different metrics for different uses. The point isn’t “use LA-PME for everything” but “know which metric you need.”

16.4.14 Slide 7.14 — Implementation: Where to Get the Data

  • Title: “Implementing LA-IRR and LA-PME”
  • Inputs needed:
    • PE fund cash flows (LP statements, Preqin, PitchBook)
    • Public benchmark returns (S&P 500, MSCI, asset-class-specific)
    • \(L_t\) path: derived from secondary market discount data (Preqin, Setter, Lazard)
    • OU calibration parameters: from book Ch. 12 or GE-LAV platform
  • Implementation tools:
    • GE-LAV® platform (liquidityillusion.com): native LA-IRR and LA-PME functions
    • Python / R: 50–100 lines of code
  • Speaker notes: Students will do this in PS1 (Track 1) for their term project.

16.4.15 Slide 7.15 — Summary and Bridge to Session 8

  • Title: “Measurement summary”
  • Key takeaways:
    • IRR has two structural biases (Session 6)
    • Standard PMEs share IRR’s constant-rate weakness
    • LA-IRR and LA-PME correct for time-varying liquidity premium
    • Magnitudes: 2–4 pp adjustment for LA-IRR; 5–15% adjustment for LA-PME
  • Tomorrow (Session 8): “What does a correct theory of private market valuation require? Five requirements that GE-LAV satisfies.”
  • Speaker notes: Set up Session 8 as the synthesis of Unit 2.

16.5 Discussion Questions

  1. A 2009-vintage PE fund reports IRR of 22% and KS PME of 1.45. LA-IRR is 16% and LA-PME is 1.18. How should an LP allocating capital today interpret these numbers?
  2. The carry computation in a typical LPA uses reported IRR with an 8% preferred return. If LA-IRR becomes standard, does the 8% hurdle remain at 8%? What should it be?
  3. Some recent academic work argues that PE has earned zero alpha after correct adjustments (Phalippou, Stafford). Other work argues it earns positive alpha (Harris-Jenkinson-Kaplan). Where do you sit, and what’s the role of methodology choice in your answer?

16.6 Worked Numerical Example: Full LA-PME Calculation

Setup: A 2010-vintage US buyout fund.

Year Capital Call (\(M) | Distribution (\)M) S&P 500 cumulative Implied L_t
2010 50 0 1.00 −0.4
2011 30 0 1.02 −0.3
2012 20 0 1.20 0.0
2014 0 30 1.55 +0.2
2016 0 50 1.65 +0.3
2018 0 80 2.00 +0.1
2020 0 25 1.95 −0.5
2022 0 45 2.30 −0.6
2024 0 60 2.70 −0.4

Step 1: Standard KS PME

Numerator: \(30/1.55 + 50/1.65 + 80/2.00 + 25/1.95 + 45/2.30 + 60/2.70 = 19.4 + 30.3 + 40.0 + 12.8 + 19.6 + 22.2 = 144.3\)

Denominator: \(50/1.00 + 30/1.02 + 20/1.20 = 50 + 29.4 + 16.7 = 96.1\)

\(KS PME = 144.3 / 96.1 = 1.50\) → 50% outperformance

Step 2: Compute liquidity-adjusted benchmark \(B_t^{LA}\)

Using calibrated \(\pi(L) = 0.045 - 0.025 L + 0.021 L^2\):

At each year, compute \(\pi(L_t)\) and integrate over time. The cumulative liquidity premium drag on the benchmark over 14 years (assuming average \(\pi\) ≈ 4.5%) is approximately \(\exp(0.045 \cdot 14) = 1.88\).

Adjusted benchmark levels: \(B_t^{LA} = B_t \cdot e^{\int_0^t \pi(L_s) ds}\)

For simplicity, applying a uniform 4.5%/year drag:

Year Adjusted Benchmark
2010 1.00
2011 1.067 (1.02 × 1.046)
2014 1.79 (1.55 × 1.156)
2018 2.66 (2.00 × 1.33)
2022 3.45 (2.30 × 1.50)
2024 4.21 (2.70 × 1.56)

Step 3: Recompute PME with adjusted benchmark

Numerator: \(30/1.79 + 50/[B_{2016}^{LA}] + 80/2.66 + 25/[B_{2020}^{LA}] + 45/3.45 + 60/4.21\) ≈ \(16.8 + 24.5 + 30.1 + 9.5 + 13.0 + 14.3 = 108.2\)

Denominator: \(50/1.00 + 30/1.067 + 20/1.135 = 50 + 28.1 + 17.6 = 95.7\)

\(LA PME = 108.2 / 95.7 = 1.13\) → 13% outperformance

Interpretation: - Reported KS PME: 1.50 (50% outperformance) - LA-PME: 1.13 (13% outperformance) - 37 percentage points of reported outperformance was compensation for the liquidity premium drag the benchmark didn’t reflect

16.7 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “If LA-PME is lower, doesn’t that mean PE is worse than we thought?” Not exactly. It means reported outperformance was partly fair compensation for liquidity risk, not true alpha. The fund may still be a good investment, just for different reasons.
  • “How do I get an L_t time series?” From secondary market discount data. PS1 Track 1 walks you through this.
  • “Doesn’t this assume the public benchmark is the right risk-free reference?” It assumes the risk-adjusted benchmark is the right reference. Beta adjustments are typically applied separately. LA-PME handles the illiquidity risk specifically.
  • “What if the secondary market is itself wrong?” GE-LAV assumes secondary market prices reflect the marginal investor’s willingness to pay. If you reject that assumption, you reject the entire framework. Reasonable critique; addressed in book Ch. 2.

16.8 What to Expect Next Session

Session 8 is the synthesis of Unit 2: what would a correct theory of private market valuation actually require? We’ll cover the five requirements from book Chapter 5:

  1. Stochastic illiquidity premia
  2. Investor heterogeneity
  3. Endogenous market-clearing
  4. Empirical implementability
  5. Welfare and regulatory analysis support

GE-LAV satisfies all five. No other current framework does. Session 8 sets up the midterm and the conceptual transition to Unit 3 (decision and application).

Reading: Book Chapter 5, all sections (~10 pages — short chapter).

Reminder: PS1 is due Session 8. Allow yourself the weekend to work through the OU calibration questions.

- **The common assumption:**
  - KS, LN, and Direct Alpha all treat the public benchmark as the *risk-adjusted reference*
  - They embed the implicit assumption that *any extra return beyond the benchmark* is fair compensation for liquidity, vintage, and selection
  - This is a *constant-rate* assumption about the illiquidity premium
- **The mismatch:**
  - GE-LAV says the illiquidity premium is *stochastic and regime-dependent*
  - PMEs cannot see this — they compress everything into a single number
- **Speaker notes:** This is the conceptual handoff to LA-PME.

### Slide 7.9 — LA-IRR: Liquidity-Adjusted IRR (Definition)

- **Title:** "LA-IRR: GE-LAV's correction to IRR"
- **The idea:** "Compute IRR using a *stochastic* discount rate path $r(L_t)$ instead of a constant rate"
- **Mechanics:**
  - Calibrate $L_t$ path from secondary market data (or simulate from OU)
  - Compute period-by-period discount factors $D_t = \exp(-\int_0^t r(L_s) ds)$
  - Find the *effective single-rate equivalent* that matches the cash flow PV
- **Output:** A liquidity-adjusted single-rate summary, comparable to standard IRR but corrected
- **Magnitude:** "For typical PE fund cash flow patterns, LA-IRR is typically 2–4 percentage points *below* reported IRR"
- **Speaker notes:** This is the *measurement counterpart* to LAV's valuation correction. Same underlying logic.

### Slide 7.10 — LA-PME: Liquidity-Adjusted PME

- **Title:** "LA-PME: GE-LAV's correction to PME"
- **The idea:** "Use the public benchmark *adjusted for the contemporaneous liquidity state* as the comparison"
- **Mechanics:**
  - At each period, compute the benchmark return *adjusted* for the implied illiquidity premium gap
  - $B_t^{LA} = B_t \cdot \exp(\int_0^t \pi(L_s) ds)$
  - Apply KS / LN / Direct Alpha methodology to $B_t^{LA}$ instead of raw $B_t$
- **Output:** PME that compares PE to a "public benchmark plus correctly-priced liquidity premium"
- **Magnitude:** "Typical PE fund LA-PME is 5-15% below reported KS PME"
- **Speaker notes:** This is a meaningful magnitude. Allocators caring about absolute outperformance should use LA-PME.

### Slide 7.11 — Comparing LA-PME to Standard PME: Worked Example

- **Title:** "Worked: 2010-vintage buyout fund"
- **Setup:**
  - Reported KS PME (vs. S&P 500): 1.32 — "32% outperformance"
  - Adjusted for L_t path 2010-2024 (mostly Regimes 3, 4): LA-PME = 1.18 — "18% outperformance"
- **Decomposition:**
  - Of the 32 percentage points of reported KS outperformance, **~14 points are compensation for time-varying illiquidity premium** (not alpha)
  - **True alpha vs. liquidity-adjusted benchmark: ~18 percentage points**
- **Speaker notes:** Even with adjustment, this fund outperformed. But the magnitude of "true alpha" is materially smaller than reported.

### Slide 7.12 — Vintage-Year Liquidity Effects

- **Title:** "Why vintage matters: liquidity state at entry"
- **Setup:**
  - Vintage 2009: entered at $L_0 \approx -1.5$ (crisis depth); mean reversion drove L back to $\bar{L}$
  - Vintage 2007: entered at $L_0 \approx +0.5$ (boom); mean reversion drove L *down* toward $\bar{L}$
- **Result:**
  - 2009 vintage benefited from positive mean reversion in L
  - 2007 vintage suffered from negative mean reversion in L
- **GE-LAV decomposition:** "Vintage-year IRR differences are partly a *liquidity state* effect, which GE-LAV captures explicitly"
- **Speaker notes:** This is *the* explanation for the strong vintage-year pattern in PE returns. Standard models can't decompose it; GE-LAV can.

### Slide 7.13 — Practical: Which Metric for Which Purpose

- **Title:** "When to use which metric"
- **Decision table:**

| Purpose | Best Metric |
|---|---|
| Carried interest computation | Reported IRR (constrained by LPA) |
| Cross-fund manager skill comparison | KS PME or Direct Alpha |
| LP allocation decisions | LA-IRR + LA-PME |
| Regulatory reporting (Solvency II) | LA-PME (under GE-LAV adoption) |
| Academic research on PE returns | Direct Alpha + LA-PME |
| Vintage-year analysis | LA-IRR (controls for liquidity state) |

- **Speaker notes:** Different metrics for different uses. The point isn't "use LA-PME for everything" but "know which metric you need."

## Worked Numerical Example: Full LA-PME Calculation

**Setup:** A 2010-vintage US buyout fund.

| Year | Capital Call ($M) | Distribution ($M) | S&P 500 cumulative | Implied L_t |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 50 | 0 | 1.00 | −0.4 |
| 2011 | 30 | 0 | 1.02 | −0.3 |
| 2012 | 20 | 0 | 1.20 | 0.0 |
| 2014 | 0 | 30 | 1.55 | +0.2 |
| 2016 | 0 | 50 | 1.65 | +0.3 |
| 2018 | 0 | 80 | 2.00 | +0.1 |
| 2020 | 0 | 25 | 1.95 | −0.5 |
| 2022 | 0 | 45 | 2.30 | −0.6 |
| 2024 | 0 | 60 | 2.70 | −0.4 |

**Step 1: Standard KS PME**

Numerator: $30/1.55 + 50/1.65 + 80/2.00 + 25/1.95 + 45/2.30 + 60/2.70 = 19.4 + 30.3 + 40.0 + 12.8 + 19.6 + 22.2 = 144.3$

Denominator: $50/1.00 + 30/1.02 + 20/1.20 = 50 + 29.4 + 16.7 = 96.1$

$KS PME = 144.3 / 96.1 = 1.50$ → 50% outperformance

**Step 2: Compute liquidity-adjusted benchmark $B_t^{LA}$**

Using calibrated $\pi(L) = 0.045 - 0.025 L + 0.021 L^2$:

At each year, compute $\pi(L_t)$ and integrate over time. The cumulative liquidity premium drag on the benchmark over 14 years (assuming average $\pi$ ≈ 4.5%) is approximately $\exp(0.045 \cdot 14) = 1.88$.

Adjusted benchmark levels: $B_t^{LA} = B_t \cdot e^{\int_0^t \pi(L_s) ds}$

For simplicity, applying a uniform 4.5%/year drag:

| Year | Adjusted Benchmark |
|---|---|
| 2010 | 1.00 |
| 2011 | 1.067 (1.02 × 1.046) |
| 2014 | 1.79 (1.55 × 1.156) |
| 2018 | 2.66 (2.00 × 1.33) |
| 2022 | 3.45 (2.30 × 1.50) |
| 2024 | 4.21 (2.70 × 1.56) |

**Step 3: Recompute PME with adjusted benchmark**

Numerator: $30/1.79 + 50/[B_{2016}^{LA}] + 80/2.66 + 25/[B_{2020}^{LA}] + 45/3.45 + 60/4.21$
≈ $16.8 + 24.5 + 30.1 + 9.5 + 13.0 + 14.3 = 108.2$

Denominator: $50/1.00 + 30/1.067 + 20/1.135 = 50 + 28.1 + 17.6 = 95.7$

$LA PME = 108.2 / 95.7 = 1.13$ → 13% outperformance

**Interpretation:**
- Reported KS PME: 1.50 (50% outperformance)
- LA-PME: 1.13 (13% outperformance)
- **37 percentage points** of reported outperformance was compensation for the liquidity premium drag the benchmark didn't reflect

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"If LA-PME is lower, doesn't that mean PE is worse than we thought?"** Not exactly. It means *reported* outperformance was partly fair compensation for liquidity risk, not true alpha. The fund may still be a good investment, just for different reasons.
- **"How do I get an L_t time series?"** From secondary market discount data. PS1 Track 1 walks you through this.
- **"Doesn't this assume the public benchmark is the right risk-free reference?"** It assumes the *risk-adjusted* benchmark is the right reference. Beta adjustments are typically applied separately. LA-PME handles the *illiquidity* risk specifically.
- **"What if the secondary market is itself wrong?"** GE-LAV assumes secondary market prices reflect the marginal investor's willingness to pay. If you reject that assumption, you reject the entire framework. Reasonable critique; addressed in book Ch. 2.
