37.1 Track 1: Private Credit Case Workshop

37.1.1 Track 1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, Track 1 students will be able to:

  1. Apply GE-LAV to a private credit / direct lending position.
  2. Decompose private credit spreads into credit risk, illiquidity premium, and complexity premium components.
  3. Distinguish GE-LAV for credit vs. equity strategies (cash flow profile, default risk interaction).
  4. Compute liquidity-adjusted spread expectations for a direct lending strategy.
  5. Evaluate a real BDC (business development company) or middle-market direct lending fund.

37.1.2 Track 1 Pre-Class Assignment

  • Read: Recent BDC quarterly report or direct lending fund presentation
  • Watch: 10-min video on private credit market structure (link on course site)

37.1.4 Track 1 Slide Briefs (New Slides Needed)

Slide T1-28.1 — Private Credit: Different Risk Profile

  • Title: “Private credit is debt, not equity”
  • Key differences from PE buyout:
    • Senior in capital structure: Lower expected return, lower variance
    • Shorter duration: Most direct lending is 3-7 years
    • Credit risk: Default + recovery rather than equity participation
    • Floating rate: Most direct lending pays SOFR + spread
  • GE-LAV implications:
    • Smaller hedge demand (less correlation with aggregate liquidity)
    • Smaller Jensen bias (shorter duration)
    • But: in stress, credit and liquidity premia interact (credit spreads widen when liquidity stresses)
  • Speaker notes: Students often think credit is “PE without the equity story.” It’s structurally different. Establish that.

Slide T1-28.2 — Decomposing Private Credit Spreads

  • Title: “What’s in a 700 bps spread?”
  • Typical direct lending spread: 5-8% above SOFR
  • Decomposition:
    • Credit risk: 200-400 bps (expected losses + idiosyncratic risk premium)
    • Illiquidity premium: 100-300 bps (GE-LAV territory)
    • Complexity premium: 50-100 bps (origination + monitoring costs)
    • Total: 350-800 bps
  • GE-LAV contribution: Quantifies the illiquidity component stochastically
  • Speaker notes: Spreads are not pure credit risk. Many investors don’t decompose carefully.

Slide T1-28.3 — Private Credit Sharpe Ratio Illusion

  • Title: “Private credit’s worst-in-class Sharpe penalty”
  • Recall Session 7’s slide: Private credit Sharpe ratio drops 84% under liquidity adjustment (highest of any private asset class)
  • Why:
    • High conventional Sharpe (~0.5) → looks attractive
    • But low return volatility and high illiquidity premium = denominator vastly understates true risk
    • Adjusted Sharpe (~0.08) reveals private credit may not be the “safe income” alternative many believe
  • Implication: “Private credit allocations sized on Sharpe-based optimization are likely substantially over-sized”
  • Speaker notes: This is a career-relevant finding. Many institutions have piled into private credit. The framework says they may be over-exposed.

Slide T1-28.4 — Lab Setup: BDC Valuation

  • Title: “Lab: a real BDC valuation”
  • Setup:
    • Choose a publicly-traded BDC (ARCC, MAIN, ORCC, or similar)
    • Public market price: known (one number)
    • NAV: reported quarterly
    • Question: Is the public market premium/discount to NAV consistent with GE-LAV?
  • Group output:
    • Estimate the “fair” NAV under GE-LAV
    • Compare to reported NAV (typically equals book value of loans)
    • Compare to public market price
    • Identify any arbitrage opportunity or risk signal
  • Speaker notes: BDCs offer a unique test: they’re publicly traded but hold private assets. The discount/premium structure is rich data.

Slide T1-28.5 — Cross-Asset Risk: Credit + Liquidity Interaction

  • Title: “When credit and liquidity stress together”
  • Setup: 2008-2010 GFC and 2020 COVID both saw simultaneous credit + liquidity stress
  • In private credit:
    • Default rates spiked
    • Secondary discounts widened
    • Recovery rates fell
    • All three correlated → much worse outcome than independence assumption
  • GE-LAV captures this:
    • Cross-asset \(\beta\)s in the multi-asset framework
    • Stress test for joint credit+liquidity events
    • More conservative capital recommendations
  • Speaker notes: This is why the integrated framework matters. Independent risk models miss the correlations.

37.2 Track 2: GE Equilibrium Existence Proof

By the end of this session, Track 2 students will be able to:

37.2.4 Track 2 Slide Briefs (New Slides Needed)

Slide T2-28.1 — GE-LAV Equilibrium: Definition

  • Title: “GE-LAV competitive equilibrium”
  • Definition: An equilibrium is a tuple \((\mu^*_t, \lambda^*(L, \mu^*), u^*(L, \mu^*))\) such that:
    1. Individual optimality: \(u^*(L, \mu^*)\) solves the HJB given \(\lambda^*\)
    2. Market clearing: \(\lambda^*(L, \mu^*)\) is the unique premium for which aggregate exit flows balance (sellers = buyers)
    3. Distributional consistency: \(\mu^*_t\) is the law of \(L_t\) under dynamics induced by \(u^*\)
  • In one sentence: “Everyone’s optimal decisions, given the equilibrium premium, generate the distribution that produces that premium.”
  • Speaker notes: Set up the formal equilibrium notion. The three conditions are mutually self-consistent.

Slide T2-28.2 — Existence Theorem 17.4

  • Title: “Theorem 17.4: existence of GE-LAV equilibrium”
  • Statement: Under standard regularity conditions on \(\phi\), \(r(L)\), and \(C(L, t)\):
    • There exists at least one GE-LAV competitive equilibrium tuple \((\mu^*, \lambda^*, u^*)\)
    • Equilibrium is unique under the stability condition \(\kappa > \gamma \cdot \text{Lip}(\phi)\)
  • Proof outline:
    • Define an equilibrium map \(\Psi: \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{M}\):
      • \(\Psi(\mu)\) = the new measure induced by optimal individual decisions
    • Show \(\Psi\) is continuous and compact on \(\mathcal{M}\)
    • Apply Schauder’s fixed-point theorem
  • Speaker notes: State the theorem; sketch the structure. Full proof in book Section 17.4.

Slide T2-28.3 — Schauder Fixed-Point Argument (Detail)

  • Title: “Schauder applied to the equilibrium map”
  • The setup:
    • \(\mathcal{M}\) = closed, bounded, convex subset of \(\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R})\) (probability measures with finite second moment)
    • \(\Psi\) continuous (HJB and market clearing both stable in measure)
    • \(\Psi\) compact (uniform bounds on second moments)
  • The conclusion: \(\Psi\) has a fixed point in \(\mathcal{M}\)
  • Why Schauder, not Brouwer?
    • Brouwer requires finite dimension
    • \(\mathcal{M}\) is infinite-dimensional
    • Schauder is the appropriate generalization
  • Speaker notes: Tie to functional analysis. Most Track 2 students have seen Schauder in PDE classes.

Slide T2-28.4 — Market Clearing as Fixed Point

  • Title: “Market clearing: a fixed point on \(\lambda\)”
  • For given \(\mu\), \(\lambda^*\) satisfies:
    • Aggregate exit flow at premium \(\lambda^*\) = Aggregate buy flow at premium \(\lambda^*\)
  • Concrete form:
    • \(\sum_i [u^*_i(L_i, \mu, \lambda^*) \cdot \text{notional}_i] = 0\)
  • Where:
    • \(u^*_i = 1\) if LP \(i\) exits at premium \(\lambda^*\), else 0
    • \(\text{notional}_i\) = LP \(i\)’s position size
  • Iteration: Bisection on \(\lambda^*\) until aggregate flow ≈ 0
  • Speaker notes: Make the market clearing concrete. It’s just supply = demand at the right price.

Slide T2-28.5 — Connection to Numerical Algorithm

  • Title: “From proof to platform”
  • The existence proof guarantees the algorithm converges:
    • Start with guess \(\mu^{(0)}\)
    • Solve HJB → get \(u^{*(0)}\)
    • Solve FP → get \(\mu^{(1)}\)
    • Iterate: \(\mu^{(0)} \to \mu^{(1)} \to \mu^{(2)} \to ...\)
  • Under stability condition:
    • Iteration converges to the unique fixed point
    • GE-LAV platform implements this
  • Computational cost:
    • Typically 10-20 iterations
    • Total time: seconds to minutes
  • Speaker notes: The platform’s algorithm is based on the existence theorem. Theory → practice.

37.2.6 Track 2 Discussion Questions

  1. The existence proof requires that \(\Psi\) is continuous on \(\mathcal{M}\). What kind of discontinuity could break this? When would discontinuity be economically realistic (e.g., regulatory thresholds, IPS triggers)?
  2. Under the stability condition \(\kappa > \gamma \cdot \text{Lip}(\phi)\), uniqueness holds. If the condition fails temporarily during a stress event but is restored as conditions normalize, what does the system look like during the failure period?
  3. The market clearing condition treats sellers and buyers symmetrically. In real markets, intermediaries (brokers) take spread. How would the proof change if we added market makers?
