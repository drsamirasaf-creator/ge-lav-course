10  Session 1: Course Intro · Why Private Market Valuation Is Broken

Unit 1 — Why DCF Fails
Book Chapter 1 (sections 1.1–1.4, 1.10–1.16)
Slide source New (60%) + existing Class 9 (40%)
Slides needed ~15 new + ~6 adapted from Class 9
Track Common core (both tracks)
Assessment milestone PS1 drops

10.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  1. State the scale of the private capital market ($13 trillion AUM, ~10,000+ funds) and articulate why valuation methodology matters at this scale.
  2. Describe how DCF is applied to private market assets in current industry practice, including the typical structure of a discount rate (risk-free + ERP + size + illiquidity).
  3. Preview the GE-LAV® valuation hierarchy (DCF ⊊ LAV ⊊ GE-LAV) and the five main results of the GE-LAV framework.
  4. Identify that the illiquidity premium is the critical and problematic component of the DCF discount rate.
  5. Commit to a course working agreement: track preview, assessment expectations, AI use policy, project orientation.

10.4.3 Slide 1.3 — The Scale of the Problem

  • Title: “Private capital is now too big for valuation errors to be tolerable”
  • Stat callouts (large numbers):
    • $13T Global private capital AUM (2025)
    • ~10,000+ Active private market funds
    • $2.5T Public pension fund private market allocations
    • 15-25% Typical private market allocation for endowments and large pensions
  • Bottom-of-slide line: “A 5% systematic valuation error implies $650B mismeasured. This is not a rounding issue.”
  • Visual: Single bar showing PE+VC+Infra+RE+PC AUM growth 2000–2025 (use stylized bars; do not reproduce copyrighted images)
  • Speaker notes: This is the motivation. Students need to feel that this matters. Reference specific allocators (CalPERS, CalSTRS, Yale endowment) by way of example.

10.4.4 Slide 1.4 — Who Cares About This?

  • Title: “Who has skin in the game”
  • Format: Four-quadrant or icon-grid layout
  • Items:
    • LPs (pensions, endowments, sovereign wealth funds, insurers): need correct NAVs for allocation, rebalancing, and reporting
    • GPs (PE/VC/RE/PC fund managers): need correct valuations for IC review, board reporting, and carry calculation
    • Regulators (insurance, banking, securities): need correct valuations for capital adequacy and systemic risk
    • Auditors / valuation firms: need defensible methodology for fair value reporting
  • Speaker notes: Career relevance — wherever students end up, this matters. Ask: “Who here is going to work for an LP? GP? Regulator? Auditor?” — get a hand count.

10.4.5 Slide 1.5 — How DCF Is Applied (Standard Practice)

  • Title: “DCF in current private market practice”
  • Layout: Equation with annotated components
  • Equation: \(V_0 = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \dfrac{CF_t}{(1+r)^t}\)
  • Discount rate decomposition (boxed): \(r = r_f + \beta \cdot ERP + \text{Size premium} + \text{Illiquidity premium}\)
  • Typical PE buyout calibration:
    • \(r_f = 4.0\%\) (SOFR + term)
    • \(\beta \cdot ERP = 3{-}4\%\)
    • Size premium = 1–2%
    • Illiquidity premium = 3–4%
    • Total = 11–14%, held constant for fund life
  • Speaker notes: The whole rest of the lecture is about that bottom row. Why is it 3–4%? Why is it constant? Both questions are wrong.

10.4.6 Slide 1.6 — Where the Illiquidity Premium Comes From

  • Title: “The 3% illiquidity premium: history, not theory”
  • Bullets:
    • Amihud & Mendelson (1986): illiquidity premium ∝ bid-ask spread (for public markets)
    • Private market application: averaged historical excess return of PE over public equity (~3.5%)
    • IPEV valuation guidelines (2022): endorse this practice
    • Solvency II (insurance): embeds similar fixed premium structure
    • Result: the 3.5% is a historical average, not a theoretical equilibrium
  • Speaker notes: Stress that this is “what the data showed on average” — but averages mask everything that matters about liquidity.

10.4.7 Slide 1.7 — Secondary Market Evidence (Tease)

  • Title: “What the secondary market shows”
  • Visual: Time series chart of secondary market discount to NAV, 2003–2024 (you’ll need to build or recreate this; cite Preqin data)
  • Annotations on chart:
    • 2007 peak: 3–8% discount
    • 2009 GFC trough: 35–50% discount
    • 2020 COVID: 15–25% discount briefly
    • 2022 rate shock: 15–20% discount sustained
  • Bottom-line: “If the premium is fixed at 3.5%, where do these moves come from?”
  • Speaker notes: This is the first appearance of the chart that will dominate Session 3. Tease it here; don’t unpack fully.

10.4.8 Slide 1.8 — The Three Structural Failures (Preview)

  • Title: “Three reasons DCF fails for private assets”
  • Layout: Three columns or three icons
  • Failure 1: “The premium is stochastic, not constant”
  • Failure 2: “Premium variability creates Jensen bias”
  • Failure 3: “Liquidity is collectively determined (McKean-Vlasov externality)”
  • Speaker notes: This is a preview. Session 2 unpacks each failure in detail. Don’t drill down here.

10.4.9 Slide 1.9 — The Valuation Hierarchy

  • Title: “Three nested theories”
  • Visual: Three nested rectangles or set-theoretic diagram showing DCF ⊊ LAV ⊊ GE-LAV
  • Annotations:
    • DCF: constant premium, partial equilibrium, deterministic discount factor
    • LAV: stochastic premium (OU process), partial equilibrium, Jensen-corrected discount factor
    • GE-LAV: stochastic premium, general equilibrium (market clearing), McKean-Vlasov collective dynamics
  • Speaker notes: This diagram appears throughout the course. Establish it now. Strict containment matters — DCF is a special case of LAV which is a special case of GE-LAV.

10.4.10 Slide 1.10 — The Five Main Results (Preview)

  • Title: “What GE-LAV delivers”
  • Numbered list:
    1. Jensen convexity bias: closed-form formula, calibrated 0.8–3.6% by asset class
    2. Optimal exit boundary: when to hold, sell, or delay
    3. Welfare gap: quantified cost of the McKean-Vlasov externality (~2.3%/year)
    4. Pigouvian exit tax: the corrective instrument
    5. Valuation hierarchy: strict nesting DCF ⊊ LAV ⊊ GE-LAV
  • Speaker notes: Foreshadowing. Each of these is a session or set of sessions later. Tell students they will be able to compute or apply each by mid-semester.

10.4.11 Slide 1.11 — A Worked Number to Anchor the Course

  • Title: “The $73 million gap”
  • Setup: “A pension fund holds a 20-year infrastructure asset”
  • DCF mark: $103.6M (constant 7.5% discount, 3.5% illiquidity premium)
  • GE-LAV equilibrium at GFC depth (L = −1.5): $30.9M
  • Gap: $73M, or 70.1%
  • Bottom-line: “This is not a mark-to-market fluctuation. It is the correct equilibrium price at which the market would clear.”
  • Visual: Two bars side-by-side — DCF vs. GE-LAV, with the gap shaded
  • Speaker notes: This is the single most powerful number in the course. Anchor everything around it. Refer back when students ask “but how big is the error really?”

10.6 Discussion Questions

For the last 10 minutes of class. Choose 2–3 depending on time:

  1. The Black-Scholes-Merton option pricing formula was published in 1973 but not adopted by major investment banks until the late 1980s. What lessons does this hold for GE-LAV’s path to adoption?
  2. If you were a CIO of a $50B pension fund and learned that GE-LAV implies your private market portfolio is overvalued by 15% in current conditions, what would you do? What would the constraints be?
  3. IPEV guidelines and IFRS 13 embed DCF-style methodology. Is changing the methodology a technical problem or a political/coordination problem? Both?

10.7 Worked Example for the Lecture

The Infrastructure Mispricing Example. A 20-year infrastructure asset with $50M annual contracted cash flows.

Method Discount rate PV
DCF (constant 7.5%, illiq premium 3.5%) r = 7.5% $103.6M
LAV (Jensen-corrected, normal markets) r ≈ 7.7% ~$108M
GE-LAV (normal markets) r ≈ 8.4% ~$95M
GE-LAV (GFC depth, L = −1.5) r ≈ 32.3% $30.9M

Teaching note: Walk through why GFC GE-LAV is so far from DCF: it’s the McKean-Vlasov collective externality — when everyone tries to exit simultaneously, the equilibrium price clears at a much higher rate than the GP-assumed constant. This is the preview of what comes in later sessions.

10.8 Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

  • “If GE-LAV says NAV is lower, isn’t that just a markdown?” No — the GE-LAV value is the equilibrium clearing price given collective behavior. It’s the correct value, not a discount on the correct value. The DCF “value” is the wrong value.
  • “If everyone uses DCF, doesn’t that make it the right value?” No — convention isn’t correctness. The market clearing price (secondary market) reveals the true equilibrium; DCF is a model that fails to compute it correctly.
  • “Why hasn’t the industry adopted this already?” Practitioner consensus trap (covered in section 1.4 of book) — being the first GP to mark down to GE-LAV creates LP pushback. Coordination problem.
  • “Is this an academic exercise or does it matter for real portfolios?” $73M on one $103M asset matters. Magnify across a $13T market and the implications are systemic.

10.9 What to Expect Next Session

Session 2 unpacks each of the three structural failures in detail. We’ll cover:

  • The secondary market data that proves the premium is stochastic
  • The mathematics of Jensen’s inequality and why convex discount factors create systematic bias
  • The McKean-Vlasov externality — what it is and why it makes liquidity a collective phenomenon, not an individual asset property
  • The Liquidity-Adjusted CAPM as a connection to the portfolio theory students already know

Reading: Book Chapter 1, sections 1.5–1.7. Skim sections 1.8–1.10 (the worked example).

## Worked Example for the Lecture

**The Infrastructure Mispricing Example.** A 20-year infrastructure asset with $50M annual contracted cash flows.

| Method | Discount rate | PV |
|---|---|---|
| DCF (constant 7.5%, illiq premium 3.5%) | r = 7.5% | $103.6M |
| LAV (Jensen-corrected, normal markets) | r ≈ 7.7% | ~$108M |
| GE-LAV (normal markets) | r ≈ 8.4% | ~$95M |
| GE-LAV (GFC depth, L = −1.5) | r ≈ 32.3% | **$30.9M** |

**Teaching note:** Walk through *why* GFC GE-LAV is so far from DCF: it's the McKean-Vlasov collective externality — when everyone tries to exit simultaneously, the equilibrium price clears at a much higher rate than the GP-assumed constant. This is the preview of what comes in later sessions.

## Common Student Confusions (Instructor Notes)

- **"If GE-LAV says NAV is lower, isn't that just a markdown?"** No — the GE-LAV value is the equilibrium clearing price *given collective behavior*. It's the correct value, not a discount on the correct value. The DCF "value" is the wrong value.
- **"If everyone uses DCF, doesn't that make it the right value?"** No — convention isn't correctness. The market clearing price (secondary market) reveals the true equilibrium; DCF is a model that fails to compute it correctly.
- **"Why hasn't the industry adopted this already?"** Practitioner consensus trap (covered in section 1.4 of book) — being the first GP to mark down to GE-LAV creates LP pushback. Coordination problem.
- **"Is this an academic exercise or does it matter for real portfolios?"** $73M on one $103M asset matters. Magnify across a $13T market and the implications are systemic.
