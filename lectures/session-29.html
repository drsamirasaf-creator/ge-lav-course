39.1 Track 1: Frontier Cases — Digital Assets + Climate

39.1.1 Track 1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, Track 1 students will be able to:

  1. Apply GE-LAV to crypto-adjacent investments (token venture funds, defi protocols, NFT funds).
  2. Adapt the calibration when secondary market data is thin (or doesn’t exist yet).
  3. Apply GE-LAV to ultra-long-duration climate-exposed infrastructure (50-100 year assets).
  4. Decompose climate risk into transition + physical risk channels.
  5. Identify how GE-LAV’s stochastic-premium framework handles novel risks (climate transition, regulatory uncertainty).

  • Skim: Recent report on crypto fund returns or NFT secondaries (link on course site)
  • Skim: Climate-aware infrastructure investing report (Bloomberg NEF or similar)

39.1.4 Track 1 Slide Briefs (New Slides Needed)

Slide T1-29.1 — Digital Assets in Private Markets

  • Title: “Crypto’s slow merger with private markets”
  • Categories:
    • Token venture funds: Like VC but invest in pre-launch protocols (Paradigm, A16Z Crypto)
    • Defi liquidity provision: Quasi-fund vehicles providing AMM liquidity
    • NFT funds: Collectibles as private market assets
    • Tokenized real-world assets: Real estate, art, private credit fractionalized on-chain
  • GE-LAV applicability:
    • Most categories have some secondary market data (varies by asset)
    • Calibration is harder (less history, more regime variability)
    • But: the structure of the framework applies — stochastic illiquidity premia, McKean-Vlasov coupling
  • Speaker notes: Establish category. Many students will work in or near crypto post-graduation.

Slide T1-29.2 — Calibration with Limited Data

  • Title: “What to do when you have 6 quarters, not 60”
  • Approaches:
    • Borrow calibration: Use similar-asset-class parameters as prior, adjust on local data
    • Bayesian methods: Treat OU parameters as random variables with priors from comparables
    • Robust valuation: Compute GE-LAV across multiple plausible parameter sets; report range
  • Cautionary note: “Calibration uncertainty is a real cost. Confidence intervals on \(\hat{\kappa}\) widen substantially with less data.”
  • Speaker notes: Be honest. Crypto/digital is a frontier; calibration is hard. Useful but not bulletproof.

Slide T1-29.3 — Climate Risk: Transition vs. Physical

  • Title: “Two kinds of climate risk in private markets”
  • Transition risk:
    • Regulatory changes (carbon pricing, EV mandates, building codes)
    • Asset stranding (fossil fuel infrastructure)
    • Affects: traditional infrastructure, energy, real estate
  • Physical risk:
    • Climate-driven asset damage (floods, wildfires, heat)
    • Operational disruption
    • Insurance cost increases
    • Affects: coastal real estate, agriculture, utilities
  • GE-LAV channel:
    • Both risks affect \(\bar{L}\) (long-run mean liquidity may shift)
    • Both affect \(\sigma_L\) (volatility may increase)
    • Long-duration assets (infrastructure) bear the highest climate exposure under GE-LAV
  • Speaker notes: Climate-aware investing is rapidly mainstreaming. GE-LAV is a natural framework.

Slide T1-29.4 — Ultra-Long-Duration Climate-Exposed Assets

  • Title: “50-100 year climate-exposed assets”
  • Examples:
    • Coastal port infrastructure (sea level rise + storm intensity)
    • Hydroelectric dams (precipitation patterns + drought)
    • Long-lived water infrastructure (drought + supply reliability)
    • Carbon sequestration facilities (regulatory + technological risk)
  • GE-LAV at these horizons:
    • Jensen bias is massive (\(B(50) \approx 50\%\)+ at calibrated parameters)
    • \(\bar{L}\) shift assumptions matter enormously
    • DCF systematically misvalues by 30-70% even in baseline scenarios
  • Speaker notes: Pension funds with ESG mandates increasingly hold these. Risk is under-measured.

Slide T1-29.5 — Lab Setup

  • Title: “Lab: a real climate-exposed infrastructure asset”
  • Choose one:
    • Port of Miami (coastal, 50-year horizon)
    • Hoover Dam (regulated, drought-exposed)
    • Tesla Gigafactory Nevada (regulatory + supply chain)
    • Indian solar farm (regulatory + climate variable)
  • Group output:
    • DCF baseline (likely misvalued)
    • GE-LAV under stable \(\bar{L}\)
    • GE-LAV under \(\bar{L}\)-shift scenario (mild + severe climate transition)
    • Recommendation for ESG-aware allocators
  • Speaker notes: Tangible. Connects to specific assets students will encounter.

39.2.4 Track 2 Slide Briefs (New Slides Needed)

Slide T2-29.1 — Jensen Bias Theorem 18.1 (Statement)

  • Title: “Theorem 18.1: closed-form Jensen bias”
  • Theorem: For an asset with cash flows \(C(L, t)\) and OU dynamics with calibrated parameters, the Jensen convexity bias is:
    • \(B(T) = (\pi_2 / 2) \cdot \Pi_{liq}(T) \cdot \langle V^{DCF} \rangle\)
  • Where:
    • \(\Pi_{liq}(T) = \sigma_L^2 [T - (1 - e^{-2\kappa T})/(2\kappa)]\)
    • \(\pi_2\) = quadratic coefficient of premium function
    • \(\langle V^{DCF} \rangle\) = average DCF value
  • Result: \(B\) is positive (since \(\pi_2 > 0\)), increasing in \(T\), and computable from first principles
  • Speaker notes: State the theorem precisely. The proof follows.

Slide T2-29.2 — Jensen Bias Theorem 18.1 (Proof Sketch)

  • Title: “Proof of Theorem 18.1”
  • Step 1: Apply Itô’s lemma to \(\exp(-\int_0^t r(L_s) ds)\)
  • Step 2: Expand around the long-run mean \(\bar{L}\):
    • \(r(L) = r(\bar{L}) + r'(\bar{L})(L - \bar{L}) + \frac{1}{2}r''(\bar{L})(L - \bar{L})^2 + O((L - \bar{L})^3)\)
  • Step 3: Compute \(E[\int_0^t r(L_s) ds]\) using OU moments:
    • First moment vanishes by mean reversion
    • Second moment contributes the Jensen correction
  • Step 4: Use convexity of \(\exp\) and Jensen’s inequality
  • Step 5: Substitute and simplify; obtain the closed form
  • Speaker notes: Walk through key steps. Full proof in book Section 18.3 (~10 pages of details).

Slide T2-29.3 — Welfare Gap Theorem 19.1

  • Title: “Theorem 19.1: constrained Pareto inefficiency”
  • Statement: The decentralized equilibrium \((\mu^*_d, u^*_d)\) is Pareto-suboptimal:
    • Aggregate welfare \(W(\mu^*_d, u^*_d) < W(\mu^*_s, u^*_s)\)
    • Where \((\mu^*_s, u^*_s)\) is the centralized social planner solution
  • Welfare gap: \(\Delta W = W(\mu^*_s, u^*_s) - W(\mu^*_d, u^*_d) \approx 2.3\%/\text{yr}\) at calibrated parameters
  • Source of inefficiency: Each LP’s exit imposes externalities on others; private incentives don’t reflect this
  • Speaker notes: Set up the welfare comparison. The 2.3% number was discussed in Session 24; now we derive it.

Slide T2-29.4 — Welfare Gap Decomposition

  • Title: “Where the 2.3%/yr comes from”
  • Decomposition:
    • Path bias from individual Jensen (each LP overstates value): ~0.5%/yr
    • Coordination failure (forced sales widen everyone’s discounts): ~1.5%/yr
    • Misallocation (LPs hold sub-optimally given true equilibrium): ~0.3%/yr
    • Total: ~2.3%/yr aggregate welfare loss
  • Distribution:
    • Bigger losses in deep-stress regimes (most coordination failures)
    • Bigger losses for long-duration assets (more Jensen accumulation)
    • Bigger losses for less sophisticated LPs (slower to recognize)
  • Speaker notes: Quantitative breakdown. Useful for regulatory presentation.

Slide T2-29.5 — Pigouvian Tax Derivation

  • Title: “The Pigouvian exit tax: closed form”
  • Result (Theorem 19.2): The welfare-maximizing tax rate on secondary exits is:
    • \(\tau^*(L) = \gamma \cdot \text{Lip}(\phi) \cdot \frac{\partial W^*}{\partial \mu}|_{exit} \cdot \frac{1}{V_{exit}}\)
  • At calibrated parameters:
\(L\) State \(\tau^*(L)\)
0 0.0%
-1 3.5%
-1.5 7.0%
-2.0 9.0%
  • Effect: With Pigouvian tax in place, decentralized equilibrium ≈ social optimum
  • Welfare improvement: ~2.3%/yr (closes the gap)
  • Speaker notes: Connect to Pigou (1920), modern carbon pricing analogies.

39.2.6 Track 2 Discussion Questions

  1. The Pigouvian tax is welfare-maximizing in theory. But it requires the regulator to know the population state \(\mu\) in real time. In practice, this is hard. What’s the welfare cost of using a constant tax rate (e.g., 3%) instead?
  2. The 2.3%/yr welfare gap aggregates to ~$300B/year. Of this, what fraction would be recovered by an imperfect Pigouvian tax (say, only on transactions during designated stress windows)?
  3. The Pareto inefficiency theorem assumes LPs are rational. Behavioral biases (loss aversion, herding) would likely amplify the externality. How would the welfare gap change with behavioral agents?
