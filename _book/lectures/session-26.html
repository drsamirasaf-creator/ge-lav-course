34.1 Track 1: VC + Secondaries Valuation Lab

34.1.1 Track 1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, Track 1 students will be able to:

  1. Apply GE-LAV to a venture capital fund position (different cash flow profile from buyout).
  2. Adjust the calibration for asset classes with thinner secondary market data.
  3. Value a specific VC secondary opportunity using GE-LAV.
  4. Distinguish LP-led from GP-led secondary structures and apply GE-LAV to each.
  5. Communicate findings to a hypothetical VC GP and IC.

34.1.2 Track 1 Pre-Class Assignment

  • Read: VC secondary case (distributed); skim recent Lazard or Evercore VC secondary report
  • Try: Platform exercise on a sample VC position

34.1.4 Track 1 Slide Briefs (New Slides Needed)

Slide T1-26.1 — VC vs. Buyout: Different Cash Flow Profiles

  • Title: “VC: longer J-curve, larger tail risk”
  • Comparison:
    • Buyout: Capital deployed in years 1-3, distributions start years 4-5, terminal exit year 7-8
    • VC: Capital deployed in years 1-4, very few distributions until years 6-7, terminal events highly bimodal (some IPOs, many writedowns)
  • GE-LAV implications:
    • Longer effective duration → larger Jensen bias accumulation
    • Tail risk demands wider hedge demand
    • Secondary market thinner; less data for calibration
  • Speaker notes: Set up why VC is not just “buyout with bigger volatility.” Different problem.

Slide T1-26.2 — VC Secondary Market Specifics

  • Title: “VC secondaries: thin, idiosyncratic, growing”
  • Structure:
    • Smaller than PE secondary market (~$20B/yr vs. $130B for PE)
    • More direct (less broker-mediated)
    • More structured deals (strip sales, NAV financings, GP-leds dominating)
  • Calibration challenges:
    • Limited public price discovery
    • Discounts often estimated indirectly via comparables
    • Implied \(L_t\) noisier than for PE
  • Speaker notes: Be honest about data limitations. Calibration confidence intervals are wider for VC.

Slide T1-26.3 — Calibrated VC Parameters

  • Title: “VC asset class calibration”
  • Parameters (vs. PE buyout):
Parameter PE Buyout VC
\(\beta_i\) (systemic) 1.2 1.1
\(\sigma_V\) 0.25 0.45
\(\rho\) (corr with \(L\)) 0.30 0.20
Hedge demand at \(L=0\) 8% 4%
Jensen bias slope (T=10) 0.17% 0.18%
  • Insight: “VC has higher idiosyncratic volatility but lower correlation with aggregate liquidity → smaller proportional hedge demand”
  • Speaker notes: Counterintuitive result. Students often think VC is “riskier all around.” It is — but the liquidity dimension is less correlated.

Slide T1-26.4 — LP-Led vs. GP-Led Secondaries

  • Title: “Two transaction types, two GE-LAV applications”
  • LP-led:
    • Single LP exits position via secondary sale
    • GE-LAV: standard valuation + exit boundary check
  • GP-led (continuation funds):
    • GP rolls portfolio company into new vehicle
    • Existing LPs choose: cash out at offered price OR roll into new fund
    • GE-LAV evaluates: is the “cash out” price the right GE-LAV value? Is the “roll” priced as a new commitment at fair value?
  • Common pitfall:
    • GP-led terms often anchored on DCF-style valuations
    • GE-LAV may say “cash out” is too low or “roll” terms are too expensive
  • Speaker notes: GP-leds are the fastest-growing segment. Students entering the industry will face these.

Slide T1-26.5 — Workshop Setup

  • Title: “Lab: a real GP-led decision”
  • The case:
    • VC fund vintage 2017, currently 7 years in
    • GP proposes continuation fund for the top 5 portfolio companies
    • Existing LPs offered: $850M cash for $1B NAV (15% discount) OR roll into new 4-year fund at same NAV
    • Your job: GE-LAV-evaluate both options
  • Group output:
    • GE-LAV value of “cash out” option
    • GE-LAV value of “roll” option (treating as new commitment)
    • Recommendation with rationale
  • Speaker notes: This is meaty. Groups will need most of class time.

34.2 Track 2: McKean-Vlasov Mean-Field Game Proofs

34.2.1 Track 2 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, Track 2 students will be able to:

  1. State and prove the propagation of chaos for the GE-LAV mean-field system (Theorem 13.4).
  2. Derive the Schauder fixed-point existence argument for MFG equilibrium (Theorem 13.2).
  3. State and verify the stability condition \(\kappa > \gamma\) for uniqueness (Theorem 13.3).
  4. Implement an MFG fixed-point iteration numerically.
  5. Discuss the limits of the framework: when stability fails, what happens.

34.2.2 Track 2 Pre-Class Assignment

  • Read: Book Chapter 13 in full, including all proofs
  • Pre-read: Carmona & Delarue, Probabilistic Theory of Mean-Field Games, Chapter 1 — at least sections 1.1–1.3

34.2.4 Track 2 Slide Briefs (New Slides Needed)

Slide T2-26.1 — The McKean-Vlasov SDE for GE-LAV

  • Title: “The McKean-Vlasov system, formally”
  • Individual SDE:
    • \(dL_t^i = \kappa(\bar{L} - L_t^i) dt + \gamma \int \phi(L_t^i, l) \mu_t(dl) dt + \sigma_L dW_t^i\)
  • Where:
    • \(\mu_t\) = distribution of \(L_t^j\) across the population
    • \(\phi(L, l)\) = pairwise interaction kernel
    • \(\gamma\) = coupling strength
  • Self-consistency:
    • $_t = $ Law \((L_t^i)\) for representative \(i\)
    • This is the fixed-point requirement
  • Speaker notes: Formal statement. Don’t dwell on the kernel form; refer to book for specifics.

Slide T2-26.2 — Propagation of Chaos: Theorem 13.4

  • Title: “Propagation of chaos for GE-LAV”
  • Theorem statement: Let \(L_t^{1,N}, ..., L_t^{N,N}\) be the \(N\)-particle system. As \(N \to \infty\):
    • \(\sup_{t \leq T} E\left[ \dfrac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \delta_{L_t^{i,N}} - \mu_t \right]_{W_1} \leq \dfrac{C}{\sqrt{N}}\)
    • where \(W_1\) is the 1-Wasserstein distance
  • Proof sketch:
    • Construct coupling between particle system and MV limit
    • Use Lipschitz properties of \(\phi\) + Grönwall inequality
    • Apply standard rates for empirical measure convergence
  • Implications:
    • At \(N = 100\): error ~10%
    • At \(N = 10,000\): error <1%
    • Private markets have \(N \approx 10,000\) → mean-field is a very good approximation
  • Speaker notes: State the theorem; sketch the proof. Don’t do every step. Refer to Sznitman (1991) for the canonical argument.

Slide T2-26.3 — Schauder Fixed-Point and Existence (Theorem 13.2)

  • Title: “Existence of MFG equilibrium”
  • The fixed-point map \(\Phi: \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{M}\):
    • Take \(\mu \in \mathcal{M}\) (set of measures with finite second moment)
    • Solve HJB given \(\mu\): get optimal policy \(u^*_\mu\)
    • Solve Fokker-Planck under \(u^*_\mu\): get new measure \(\Phi(\mu)\)
    • Fixed point: \(\mu = \Phi(\mu)\)
  • Schauder’s argument:
    • \(\mathcal{M}\) is convex, closed under Wasserstein topology
    • \(\Phi\) is continuous (HJB and FP both stable in measure)
    • \(\Phi\) is compact (uniform bounds on moments)
    • Therefore \(\Phi\) has a fixed point in \(\mathcal{M}\)
  • Speaker notes: This is the existence argument. State carefully. Full proof in book Section 13.3.

Slide T2-26.4 — Stability Condition and Uniqueness (Theorem 13.3)

  • Title: “When the equilibrium is unique”
  • Stability condition: \(\kappa > \gamma \cdot \text{Lip}(\phi)\)
  • Calibrated values:
    • \(\kappa = 0.45\)
    • \(\gamma \cdot \text{Lip}(\phi) \approx 0.1\) (well below \(\kappa\))
    • Stability: comfortable
  • Theorem 13.3 (uniqueness): Under the stability condition, the MFG fixed point is unique
  • Proof sketch:
    • Contraction argument on \(\Phi\): \(W_1(\Phi(\mu_1), \Phi(\mu_2)) \leq \rho \cdot W_1(\mu_1, \mu_2)\) for some \(\rho < 1\)
    • Banach fixed-point theorem gives uniqueness
  • When stability fails:
    • \(\gamma \cdot \text{Lip}(\phi) > \kappa\): multiple equilibria possible
    • Market can “tip” between equilibria (analogous to bank runs)
    • This is a research frontier; GE-LAV’s basic calibration assumes stability
  • Speaker notes: Honest about limits. Stable regime is empirically supported.

34.2.6 Track 2 Discussion Questions

  1. The propagation of chaos rate is \(1/\sqrt{N}\). At \(N = 10,000\), error is ~1%. But private markets have concentration (a few mega-LPs hold most of the AUM). Does that violate the i.i.d. assumption? How would you account for concentration?
  2. The stability condition \(\kappa > \gamma\) is satisfied empirically. But in a hypothetical “panic” scenario, could the system enter an unstable regime? What would the off-equilibrium dynamics look like?
  3. The MFG framework assumes Markov dynamics. Real markets have memory (regret, anchoring). How would non-Markov extensions change the proofs?

34.3 Both Tracks: Cross-Audit

Students in either track may audit the other track’s session via recording. Track 1 students benefit from hearing the MFG existence proof (it explains why GE-LAV works); Track 2 students benefit from the applied VC case (it grounds the math).
